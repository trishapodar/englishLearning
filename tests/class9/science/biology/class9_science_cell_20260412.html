SECTION A — 1 Mark Each

Q1.   Who coined the term "cell"?   [1 Mark]

Answer: Robert Hooke (1665).

Marking: Correct name — 1 mark. Accept: "Hooke" without the first name. Common Error: Confusing with Anton van Leeuwenhoek, who observed living cells — not the one who coined the term "cell".

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q2.   Living boundary of cell   [1 Mark]

Answer: Plasma membrane (also called cell membrane).

Marking: Correct term — 1 mark. Accept: Cell membrane. Common Error: Writing "cell wall" — the cell wall is non-living and is found only in plant cells and bacteria.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q3.   Direction of water in osmosis   [1 Mark]

Answer: Water moves from a region of lower solute concentration (higher water concentration) to a region of higher solute concentration (lower water concentration).

Marking: Correct direction stated — 1 mark. Accept: "From hypotonic to hypertonic solution" or "from dilute to concentrated solution". Common Error: Reversing the direction — stating water moves toward low solute concentration.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q4.   Prokaryotic DNA location   [1 Mark]

Answer: Nucleoid.

Marking: Correct term — 1 mark. Accept: Nucleoid region. Common Error: Writing "nucleus" — prokaryotes do not have a membrane-bound nucleus.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q6.   Fluid mosaic model of plasma membrane   [2 Marks]

Answer: The plasma membrane is made up of a phospholipid bilayer in which proteins are embedded. The lipid bilayer gives it fluidity (it is not rigid), and the proteins are scattered in a "mosaic" pattern — hence the name "fluid mosaic model".

→ Phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins — 1 mark.

→ Fluidity + mosaic arrangement explained — 1 mark.

Marking: Bilayer + proteins (1 mark) + fluid + mosaic explanation (1 mark). Accept: Reference to Singer and Nicolson model. Common Error: Describing it as rigid or confusing it with the cell wall.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q7. ★   Raisins swelling in water — osmosis   [2 Marks]

Answer: The process is Osmosis (specifically endosmosis). Water moves from the surrounding hypotonic (plain water) solution into the raisins — which have a higher solute concentration — through the semi-permeable membrane, causing swelling.

→ Identifying osmosis / endosmosis — 1 mark.

→ Correct direction: water moves into the raisins from outside — 1 mark.

Marking: Osmosis/endosmosis (1 mark) + correct direction (1 mark). Accept: "From plain water into raisin cells" as the direction statement. Common Error: Writing "diffusion" instead of osmosis; reversing the direction of water movement.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q8.   Prokaryotic vs eukaryotic cytoplasm   [2 Marks]

Answer: (i) Prokaryotic cytoplasm has no membrane-bound organelles; eukaryotic cytoplasm contains membrane-bound organelles (e.g., mitochondria, ER, Golgi apparatus). (ii) In prokaryotes, the genetic material (DNA) floats freely in the cytoplasm; in eukaryotes, it is enclosed within a membrane-bound nucleus.

→ First difference — 1 mark.

→ Second difference — 1 mark.

Marking: 1 mark per valid, clearly contrasting difference. Accept: Any two valid differences specifically about the cytoplasm. Common Error: Writing general differences about the cell rather than specifically about the cytoplasm.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q12.   Active transport definition and example   [3 Marks]

Answer: Active transport is the movement of molecules from a region of lower concentration to higher concentration (against the gradient) using energy in the form of ATP.

→ Definition with "against gradient" and "energy (ATP)" — 1 mark.

→ Contrast with passive transport (no energy, along gradient) — 1 mark.

→ Example: Absorption of minerals by root hair cells from soil even when mineral concentration inside the root is already higher — 1 mark.

Marking: Definition (1 mark) + contrast with passive (1 mark) + example (1 mark). Accept: Glucose re-absorption in kidney tubules as an alternate example. Common Error: Defining active transport as merely "faster" transport — speed is not the key; energy use and direction are.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q13. ★   Distilled water IV drip — cellular consequence   [3 Marks]

Answer: Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) is isotonic to blood cells — RBCs maintain normal shape. Distilled water is hypotonic relative to RBCs. When distilled water enters the bloodstream, the RBCs are surrounded by a hypotonic solution. By osmosis (endosmosis), water enters the RBCs rapidly. The cells swell and may burst — a process called haemolysis. This is a medical emergency.

→ Identifying distilled water as hypotonic — 1 mark.

→ Explaining endosmosis: water moves into RBCs — 1 mark.

→ Stating RBCs swell and burst / haemolysis — 1 mark.

Marking: Hypotonic identification (1) + endosmosis direction (1) + haemolysis/bursting (1). Accept: Lysis / haemolysis / cell bursting. Common Error: Stating cells will shrink — shrinkage occurs in a hypertonic solution, not hypotonic.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5; NCERT Exemplar Ch. 5 (HOTS)

Q14.   Nucleus structure — 3 components   [3 Marks]

Answer: The nucleus is bounded by a double-layered nuclear membrane (with pores). Inside:

→ (i) Chromosomes / Chromatin — carries genes (DNA); function: heredity and control of cell activities — 1 mark.

→ (ii) Nucleolus — small spherical body; function: synthesis of RNA and ribosomes — 1 mark.

→ (iii) Nucleoplasm — gel-like fluid; function: supports nuclear contents and allows enzymatic reactions — 1 mark.

Marking: 1 mark per correctly named component with its function (3 marks). Accept: Any three correctly named components with correct functions. Common Error: Confusing nucleolus with nucleus; omitting functions.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

SECTION D — 5 Marks Each

Q15.   Cell discovery and fundamental unit of life   [5 Marks]

Answer — Part (a) [3 marks]:

→ Robert Hooke (1665) — Observed dead cork cells; coined the term "cell" — 1 mark.

→ Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1674) — First to observe free-living cells (bacteria, protists) — 1 mark.

→ Schleiden & Schwann (1838–39) — Proposed Cell Theory (all plants/animals made of cells) — 1 mark.

→ Rudolf Virchow (1855): "Omnis cellula e cellula" — all cells arise from pre-existing cells.

Answer — Part (b) [2 marks]:

→ All living organisms, whether unicellular or multicellular, are made of cells — basic structural unit — 1 mark.

→ All life processes (respiration, nutrition, excretion, reproduction) occur within the cell — basic functional unit — 1 mark.

Marking: Part (a): 1 mark each for any three scientists with correct contributions (3 marks). Part (b): 1 mark per valid reason (2 marks). Accept: Any three correctly attributed scientists; any two valid justifications. Common Error: Attributing Cell Theory to one person; not distinguishing structural from functional role.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5; NCERT Exemplar Ch. 5

Q16.   Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic table + Endocytosis/Exocytosis   [5 Marks]

Answer — Part (a) [3 marks]:

Feature Prokaryotic Cell Eukaryotic Cell
Nucleus Absent (nucleoid only) Well-defined, membrane-bound
Cell size Smaller (1–10 µm) Larger (10–100 µm)
Membrane-bound organelles Absent Present (mitochondria, ER, etc.)
Examples Bacteria, Blue-green algae Plant cells, Animal cells, Fungi

Answer — Part (b) [2 marks]:

→ Endocytosis: The plasma membrane folds inward to engulf external substances, forming a vesicle inside the cell. Exocytosis: Vesicles inside the cell fuse with the plasma membrane and release their contents outside — 1 mark.

→ Importance for Amoeba: It has no mouth, so it uses endocytosis (phagocytosis) to engulf food particles and exocytosis to expel undigested waste — these are its primary mechanisms for feeding and excretion — 1 mark.

Marking: Part (a): 3 marks for correct table (any 3 rows = 1 mark each). Part (b): endocytosis + exocytosis defined (0.5+0.5 mark) + importance for Amoeba (1 mark). Accept: Any three valid rows in the table; any unicellular organism for the importance example. Common Error: Confusing endocytosis/exocytosis with osmosis; stating Amoeba eats through a mouth.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5; NCERT Exemplar Ch. 5
