Landmark National Milestones

★ HISTORIC MILESTONE · 31 MARCH 2026

India Officially Declared Naxal-Free — MHA Meets Landmark Deadline

The Union Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA) officially declared on March 31, 2026, that India has achieved its target of becoming Naxal-free — meeting Home Minister Amit Shah's promise made in the Rajya Sabha. Left-Wing Extremism (LWE) affected districts have been reduced from 126 in 2014 to just 7 by early 2026, with only 3 districts — Bijapur, Sukma, and Narayanpur (all in Chhattisgarh) — still in the "most affected" category.

  • LWE-affected districts: 126 (2014) → ~7 (early 2026) — an 85%+ reduction
  • Violent incidents: Declined by over 53%; security personnel deaths fell by 73% in a decade
  • Operation Black Forest (Apr–May 2025): 31 top Naxal cadres neutralised in 21-day operation at Karreguttalu Hills, Chhattisgarh–Telangana border
  • Infrastructure: 15,000+ km of roads built; 9,200+ mobile towers commissioned in LWE areas
  • SAMADHAN Doctrine: Multi-pronged approach — Smart leadership, Aggressive strategy, Motivation, Action plan, Dashboard, Harnessing technology, Action on finances, No access to supply chain
  • 31 "Legacy & Thrust" districts: Violence ended but require continued governance support
  • Legal framework: Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), 1967; NIA Act, 2008; NDPS Act
CLAT Angle: LWE, internal security under the Union List (List I, Entry 2A), UAPA, and MHA's role are key for CLAT. The constitutional basis for labelling organisations as "unlawful" and the UAPA's individual designation provisions (2019 Amendment) are high-priority topics. Also connects to tribal rights under Part X (Fifth & Sixth Schedules).

Constitutional & Legal Developments

★ CONSTITUTION BENCH · 5 APRIL

SC Notifies 9-Judge Constitution Bench to Review Sabarimala Temple Entry Case

The Supreme Court on April 5, 2026 formally notified the constitution of a nine-judge Constitution Bench headed by CJI Surya Kant to re-examine the Sabarimala temple entry matter and connected cases. The bench is scheduled to commence hearings from April 7, 2026. This is among the most consequential constitutional proceedings in recent times — addressing the interplay between equality rights (Articles 14–17) and freedom of religion (Articles 25–28), and the doctrine of constitutional morality vs. popular morality.

  • Original 2018 judgment (5-judge bench): Struck down ban on women aged 10–50 from entering Sabarimala; allowed entry of women of all ages
  • 2019 review reference (3:2 majority): Five-judge bench referred broader questions to a 9-judge bench
  • Questions referred: Essential religious practices doctrine; interplay of equality and religion across faiths (including cases on mosque entry, dargah entry, and Parsi Fire Temple entry)
  • 9-Judge Bench: CJI Surya Kant, Justices Bagchi, B.V. Nagarathna, Mahadevan, Sundresh, Amanullah, Aravind Kumar, A.G. Masih, Prasanna B. Varale
  • Constitutional provisions: Article 25 (freedom of conscience and religion), Article 26 (freedom to manage religious affairs), Article 14 (equality), Article 17 (abolition of untouchability)
CLAT Angle: This is a MEGA CLAT topic — Constitution benches, Articles 25–28 vs. Articles 14–17, the "essential religious practices" test, and constitutional morality are staple CLAT themes. Examiners frequently set passages around landmark temple-entry cases. Know Indian Young Lawyers Association v. State of Kerala (2018).
ARTICLE 32 · TRANSGENDER RIGHTS

Transgender Persons Amendment Act, 2026 Challenged Before SC Under Article 32

A writ petition was filed directly before the Supreme Court under Article 32 challenging the constitutional validity of the Transgender Persons (Protection of Rights) Amendment Act, 2026. The petition contends that the Act dismantles the fundamental right of transgender persons to self-identify their gender as recognised in the landmark NALSA judgment, inflicting "irreparable constitutional injury" by violating Articles 14, 15, 19, and 21.

  • Article 32: Right to constitutional remedies — "heart and soul of the Constitution" (Dr. Ambedkar)
  • Core constitutional question: Can the State legally define a person's gender identity, overriding self-perception?
  • NALSA v. Union of India (2014): Held self-determination of gender identity is a fundamental right under Article 21
  • The 2026 Amendment introduces Medical Board certification, replacing self-identification
  • Petitioner's argument: The Act violates the doctrine of constitutional morality and the basic structure
CLAT Angle: Article 32 (original jurisdiction of SC), writs, NALSA judgment, and the constitutional validity challenge mechanism are core CLAT constitutional law topics. This case perfectly illustrates why CLAT asks students to reason from constitutional principles, not just memorise facts.
DIGITAL RIGHTS · FREE SPEECH · CRITICAL

IT Rules 2021 Amendments Proposed — Sweeping Powers to Curb Social Media

The Union Government proposed significant amendments to the Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules, 2021. The amendments dramatically expand the government's takedown powers — extending them from news publishers to any individual posting news or current affairs content on social media, with compliance time reduced to just 2–3 hours.

  • Takedown power: Ministry of Information & Broadcasting can issue notices directly to individual social media users
  • Safe Harbour at risk: Non-compliance may cause loss of Section 79 protection (IT Act, 2000) for intermediaries
  • Timeline: Reduced from 24–36 hours to 2–3 hours — critics call this operationally impossible
  • Expanded IDC role: Inter-Departmental Committee can now hear government-referred grievances
  • Constitutional concern: May violate Article 19(1)(a) — freedom of speech and expression
  • Earlier context: Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015) — SC struck down Section 66A of IT Act as unconstitutional
CLAT Angle: Article 19(1)(a) and its reasonable restrictions under Article 19(2), Section 79 of the IT Act (safe harbour), and the Shreya Singhal ruling are frequently tested CLAT topics. The tension between national security/public order and free speech is an evergreen passage topic.

Supreme Court Weekly Round-Up (30 March – 5 April)

SC Matter #1

POCSO Bail Challenge — Minors & Serious Offences

An SLP was filed challenging anticipatory bail granted to Swami Avimukteshwaranand by Allahabad HC in a POCSO case. Petition argues that bail in serious offences involving minors should be granted only in exceptional circumstances.

SC Matter #2

PIL for Separate Revenue Judicial Service Cadre

A PIL under Article 32 seeks a dedicated Revenue Judicial Service for land dispute adjudication, noting that revenue officers lacking formal legal training are currently deciding complex property matters.

SC Matter #3

Bhojshala Temple – Kamal Maula Mosque Dispute

The SC directed the MP High Court to consider Muslim side's objections to the ASI survey of the Bhojshala-Kamal Maula Mosque site. The site is contested between Hindu and Muslim groups.

SC Matter #4

Chambal Illegal Sand Mining — "Mining Mafia are New Dacoits"

SC termed illegal sand mining in Chambal sanctuary an "extremely sad state of affairs," warning the mining mafia threatens the fragile ecosystem and endangered gharial habitat. Suo motu proceedings ongoing.

SC Matter #5

Toll Tax on National Highways — Union List Jurisdiction

SC dismissed an SLP challenging the Union's power to levy toll on national highways, holding such charges fall squarely within the Union List (Entry related to NHs) and are a fee traceable to NHAI.

SC Matter #6

Incarceration Without Trial = Punishment — SC Grants Bail

SC reaffirmed that prolonged incarceration without trial amounts to punishment, granting bail after nearly 2 years in custody. Bail jurisprudence: "Bail is the rule, jail is the exception."

CLAT Angle on SC Roundup: The Bhojshala dispute connects to Article 25–26 (religion) and ASI's powers under the Ancient Monuments Act. Chambal mining links to Article 48A (protect environment) and wildlife law. POCSO matters test knowledge of special courts and victim protection laws.

Economy, Finance & Governance

RBI FOUNDATION DAY · 1 APRIL

RBI Turns 91 — Inflation Target Band of 4% ±2% Retained for 5 More Years

On April 1, 2026, the Reserve Bank of India celebrated its 91st Foundation Day (established April 1, 1935). In a significant monetary policy announcement on the same day, the Ministry of Finance confirmed that India's inflation target band of 4% ±2% (i.e., 2%–6%) will remain unchanged for the next five years, till March 2031 — ensuring continuity in monetary policy framework.

  • RBI established: April 1, 1935 under the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934
  • Inflation targeting adopted: Under the RBI (Amendment) Act, 2016 — mandates RBI to maintain CPI inflation at 4% ± 2%
  • Monetary Policy Committee (MPC): 6-member committee (3 RBI officials + 3 external members) sets repo rate
  • Current Repo Rate context: Elevated due to West Asia oil shock driving inflation above 5.5%
  • Rupee hit ₹94.81/USD — historic low, RBI capped bank forex exposure at $100 million/day
CLAT Angle: RBI's structure, the Monetary Policy Committee, inflation targeting framework, and the RBI Act are frequently tested in CLAT GK passages. The Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management (FRBM) Act and RBI's autonomy vs. government direction are also excellent CLAT topics.
FISCAL FEDERALISM · 1 APRIL

15th Finance Commission Grants Released to States from April 1, 2026

The Central Government released grants recommended by the 15th Finance Commission (XV-FC) to states including Chhattisgarh, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Punjab, Sikkim, Himachal Pradesh, Odisha, and Tripura. The grants are structured as 60% tied (for sanitation, drinking water, and basic services) and 40% untied (for flexible local use).

  • Finance Commission: Constituted under Article 280 — reviews Centre-State fiscal relations every 5 years
  • 15th Finance Commission (Chairman: N.K. Singh): Period 2021–26; devolution ratio = 41% of divisible pool to states
  • Tied grants: Earmarked for specific uses (sanitation, water); untied grants give states fiscal flexibility
  • Grants also cover local bodies (Panchayats and Municipalities) under Articles 243I and 243Y
  • 16th Finance Commission: Being constituted for 2026–31 period
CLAT Angle: Article 280 (Finance Commission), fiscal federalism, Centre-State financial relations, and the constitutional basis of revenue sharing are important CLAT topics. The devolution ratio and tied vs. untied grants have appeared in previous CLAT passages.
INSURANCE · REGULATION · 1 APRIL

IRDAI Mandates Ind AS for Insurance Sector from April 2026

The Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India (IRDAI) has mandated the adoption of Indian Accounting Standards (Ind AS) — aligned with International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) — for all insurers from April 2026. The transition replaces the older Indian GAAP (IGAAP) for insurance reporting and introduces market-based valuation of insurance contracts.

  • Key standards: Ind AS 117 (insurance contracts) and Ind AS 109 (financial instruments)
  • IRDAI: Statutory regulator established under the IRDAI Act, 1999
  • Impact: Enhances FDI attractiveness; aligns India with global insurance accounting norms
  • FDI in insurance: Increased to 74% (2021) and recently to 100% in some categories
CLAT Angle: IRDAI's regulatory role, insurance sector reforms, FDI policy, and global financial standards align with CLAT's GK passages on economic regulatory bodies.
GOVERNANCE · AI · INTERNAL SECURITY

MHA Deploys AI for Predictive Policing, Cybercrime Detection & IVFRT 3.0

The Ministry of Home Affairs announced increased deployment of AI tools for predictive policing, cybercrime detection, and financial fraud prevention. The Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C) now uses AI to monitor dark web activity and scam networks. A new IVFRT 3.0 system (Immigration, Visa, Foreigners Registration and Tracking), integrating AI and blockchain, is set to launch in April 2026.

  • I4C: Set up under MHA; runs the National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in)
  • Surakshini framework: Automated hash-matching to prevent upload of illegal content online
  • Concern: Predictive policing using AI raises issues of profiling and Article 21 (privacy — Puttaswamy judgment 2017)
  • IVFRT 3.0: Uses blockchain for tamper-proof immigration records
  • Maoist decline also attributed to 9,000+ mobile towers breaking insurgent propaganda
CLAT Angle: Privacy under Article 21 (Puttaswamy, 2017), the tension between national security surveillance and civil liberties, and emerging cyber law (IT Act, 2000) are very high-priority CLAT themes as AI governance becomes more relevant.
SEMICONDUCTORS · INFRASTRUCTURE · 31 MARCH

PM Launches Kaynes Semicon Plant in Sanand, Gujarat — India's Semiconductor Push

On Mahavir Jayanti (March 31), PM Modi inaugurated the Kaynes Semicon semiconductor plant in Sanand, Gujarat, alongside ₹20,000+ crore worth of infrastructure projects in the state (power, railways, roads, housing, expressway). The semicon plant is part of India's push to build a domestic semiconductor manufacturing ecosystem under the India Semiconductor Mission.

  • India Semiconductor Mission (ISM): Nodal agency under MeitY; approved ₹76,000 crore incentive scheme
  • Semicon India Programme: Provides up to 50% fiscal support for greenfield semiconductor fabrication units
  • Other approved plants: Tata Electronics (Dholera), CG Power–Renesas (Sanand), Micron Technology (Sanand)
  • Strategic importance: Reduces India's dependence on Taiwan, South Korea for chips — critical in context of US-China tensions
  • Semiconductors are in the High-Technology items under India's export control lists
CLAT Angle: India's industrial policy, Make in India, PLI (Production Linked Incentive) schemes, and technology governance are relevant for CLAT GK passages on India's economic transformation and strategic autonomy.

International Affairs

WEST ASIA · ENERGY CRISIS

Rupee Hits Historic Low of ₹94.81/$; RBI Caps Bank Forex Exposure Amid Oil Shock

As global crude oil prices surged above $116 per barrel due to the West Asia conflict, the Indian Rupee fell to a historic low of ₹94.81 against the US dollar — a 4% decline since the conflict started in late February. The RBI directed banks to limit their foreign currency exposure to $100 million per day by April 10, 2026. Simultaneously, Russia announced a ban on gasoline exports from April 1, citing the West Asia crisis, further straining global energy markets.

  • India's crude import dependence: Over 88% — making it acutely vulnerable to oil price shocks
  • Rupee depreciation impact: Increases cost of imports, widens Current Account Deficit (CAD)
  • India's Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR): ~9.5 days of crude import cover (Visakhapatnam, Mangalore, Padur)
  • RBI intervention: Forex market operations + cap on bank exposure to stabilise the rupee
  • India exploring: Biofuel alternatives (ethanol blending), accelerated renewable energy push
CLAT Angle: India's energy security, current account deficit, RBI's regulatory tools, and India's diplomatic balancing act with both US-Israel bloc and Iran-Russia are strong CLAT GK passage themes connecting law, policy, and economics.
INDIA-US · TRADE & DIPLOMACY

India-US Trade Deal: Tariff Cut to 18%; Russian Oil Waiver Extended to April 4

The US-India trade deal (announced February 2, 2026) reduced US tariffs on Indian exports to 18% in exchange for India agreeing to stop purchases of Russian oil. However, the US gave India a short waiver to continue Russian oil purchases until April 4, 2026. Post-April 4, India's energy diplomacy faces a difficult choice between its long-standing ties with Russia and the economic benefits of the US trade deal.

  • Trade deal significance: India is the US's 8th largest trading partner; bilateral trade exceeds $200 billion
  • India's Russian oil: Russia became India's top oil supplier post-2022, supplying ~35–40% of India's crude
  • India's diplomatic principle: Strategic autonomy — maintaining independent foreign policy
  • WTO context: Any bilateral preferential trade arrangement must comply with WTO's Most-Favoured-Nation (MFN) principle or qualify as an FTA under GATT Article XXIV
CLAT Angle: India's foreign policy doctrine, international trade law (WTO/GATT), and the tension between India's strategic autonomy and bilateral economic dependencies are increasingly tested in CLAT GK comprehension passages.

WOMEN'S SAFETY · GOVERNANCE

MWC Highlights: Conviction Rate in Rape Cases Falls to 22.7%; 90%+ Judicial Pendency

The Ministry of Women and Child Development highlighted that conviction rates in rape cases in India fell to 22.7% in 2023 from 27–28% in 2018–2022. Over 90% of crimes against women remain pending in courts. The MWC linked the decline to weak investigations, poor forensic evidence collection, victim-blaming culture, and a shortage of fast-track special courts.

  • Fast Track Special Courts (FTSCs): Set up under Nirbhaya Fund for POCSO and rape cases
  • Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS), 2023: Replaced IPC; Section 64–71 covers rape and sexual offences
  • Nirbhaya Case (2012): Led to Criminal Law (Amendment) Act, 2013 — expanded definition of rape, increased punishment
  • Article 15(3): State may make special provisions for women and children
  • POCSO Act, 2012: Protection of Children from Sexual Offences — special courts, presumption of guilt
CLAT Angle: Criminal law reforms for women's safety, POCSO, BNS provisions, fast-track courts, and access to justice are extremely important for CLAT legal reasoning and GK passages.

🎯 Weekly CLAT Practice Quiz

Attempt each question — then click "Reveal Answer" to verify.

Q1. The MHA declared India "Naxal-free" on March 31, 2026. Under which List of the 7th Schedule does "internal security" fall, and which doctrine guided the anti-Naxal strategy?
Internal security falls under Union List (List I), Entry 2A — "Deployment of any armed force of the Union or any other force subject to the control of the Union or any contingent or unit thereof in any State in aid of the civil power; powers, jurisdiction, privileges and liabilities of the members of such forces while on such deployment." The strategy was guided by the SAMADHAN Doctrine — an acronym covering Smart leadership, Aggressive strategy, Motivation, Action plan, Dashboard, Harnessing technology, Action on finances, No access to supply chain.
Q2. The 9-judge Constitution Bench on Sabarimala will address the "essential religious practices" test. What is this test, and in which case was it first articulated?
The Essential Religious Practices (ERP) test holds that only practices "essential" or integral to a religion are protected under Article 25 of the Constitution. If a practice is not essential to the religion, the State can regulate or prohibit it. The test was first articulated in Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments v. Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar of Sri Shirur Mutt (1954) — the "Shirur Mutt case." Critics argue the test involves courts deciding what is "essential" in a religion, which is itself problematic.
Q3. The IT Rules 2021 amendment proposes removal of "safe harbour" protection. Under which section of the IT Act, 2000 does this protection exist, and which landmark SC case struck down a related provision of the IT Act?
Section 79 of the IT Act, 2000 provides "safe harbour" protection to intermediaries (platforms like Twitter, YouTube, etc.) — they are not liable for third-party content if they observe due diligence. The landmark case is Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015), where the Supreme Court struck down Section 66A of the IT Act (which penalised "offensive" online speech) as unconstitutional for being vague and violating Article 19(1)(a).
Q4. The 15th Finance Commission was chaired by N.K. Singh. Under which Article of the Constitution is the Finance Commission established, and what is the current devolution ratio to states?
The Finance Commission is established under Article 280 of the Constitution. It is constituted every five years (or earlier) by the President. The 15th Finance Commission (2021–26) recommended a devolution of 41% of the Centre's divisible pool of taxes to states — down from 42% recommended by the 14th Finance Commission, primarily due to the creation of J&K as a Union Territory.
Q5. RBI was established on April 1, 1935. Under which Act was it constituted, and when did RBI adopt inflation targeting as its primary monetary policy objective?
RBI was established under the Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934. Inflation targeting was adopted through the Reserve Bank of India (Amendment) Act, 2016, which amended the RBI Act to mandate the RBI to maintain Consumer Price Index (CPI) inflation at 4% (± 2%) i.e. within the band of 2%–6%. The MPC (Monetary Policy Committee) was also constituted under this amendment. As of April 2026, this band has been retained for another 5 years till March 2031.
Q6. Which Article of the Constitution gives every citizen the right to move the Supreme Court directly for enforcement of fundamental rights, and who called it the "heart and soul of the Constitution"?
Article 32 — the Right to Constitutional Remedies — gives every citizen the right to directly approach the Supreme Court for the enforcement of fundamental rights. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar described Article 32 as the "heart and soul of the Constitution," noting that without the right to enforce fundamental rights, the rights themselves would be meaningless. Article 32 is itself a fundamental right and cannot be suspended except during a national emergency under Article 359.
