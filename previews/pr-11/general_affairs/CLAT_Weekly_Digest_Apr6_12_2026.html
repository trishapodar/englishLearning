Constitutional & Legal Developments

★ CONSTITUTION BENCH · 7 APRIL — THE CASE OF THE DECADE

9-Judge Constitution Bench Begins Sabarimala Hearing — Seven Core Questions on Religious Freedom

India's largest Constitutional Bench in recent memory — 9 judges, led by CJI Surya Kant — commenced hearing on April 7 in Kantaru Rajeevaru v. Indian Young Lawyers Association. The bench is reviewing the landmark 2018 judgment that allowed women of all ages into the Sabarimala temple, and will settle 7 foundational questions on the scope of religious freedom under Articles 25–26 vis-à-vis equality rights under Articles 14–17. Crucially, the bench has expanded the reference to cover 66 connected matters across multiple faiths.

On Day 1, Solicitor General Tushar Mehta (for Union of India, supporting review) argued that the "Essential Religious Practices" test was first articulated without noticing the Constituent Assembly Debates, and that sub-denominations within a religion deserve independent Article 26 protection.

  • Original 2018 ruling (4:1): Women of all ages allowed into Sabarimala; the bar on women aged 10–50 was unconstitutional
  • 2019 Review Reference (3:2 majority): Referred broad religion-vs-equality questions to a larger bench
  • 66 connected matters include: (i) Muslim women's entry into mosques; (ii) Parsi women married to non-Parsis entering Fire Temples; (iii) Female Genital Mutilation (FGM) in Dawoodi Bohra community
  • 7 Questions framed: Scope of ERP test; meaning of 'morality' in Arts. 25–26; whether Art. 26 rights override other Part III rights; meaning of 'sections of Hindus' (Art. 25(2)(b)); scope of judicial review in religious matters
  • Bench: CJI Surya Kant + Justices BV Nagarathna, MM Sundresh, Ahsanuddin Amanullah, Aravind Kumar, AG Masih, R Mahadevan, Prasanna B Varale, Joymalya Bagchi
  • Schedule: Apr 7–9 (Review petitioners); Apr 14–16 (Opposing parties); Apr 21 (Rejoinder); Apr 22 (Amicus)
CLAT Angle: This is the single most important pending constitutional case for CLAT 2026 preparation. Master Articles 25–28 (religion), Articles 14–17 (equality), the Essential Religious Practices test (Shirur Mutt, 1954), the doctrine of constitutional morality (Navtej Singh Johar, 2018), and Article 26 (religious denominations). CLAT will almost certainly set a comprehension passage on this topic.
★ LANDMARK VERDICT · 6 APRIL — RAREST OF RARE

Sathankulam Custodial Death Case: 9 Policemen Sentenced to Death — "Rarest of Rare"

In a historic and emotionally charged verdict, the First Additional District and Sessions Court in Madurai on April 6, 2026, awarded the death penalty to all 9 police officers convicted of custodial torture and murder of father-son duo P. Jeyaraj (58) and J. Bennix (31) in June 2020. Judge G. Muthukumaran held the case fell within the "rarest of rare" doctrine, noting: "If ordinary citizens had committed the same crime, ordinary punishment could have been given, but the police themselves have committed the crime." The court drew a parallel to the killing of George Floyd in the US.

  • Case: CBI v. Sridhar & Ors. | First Additional Sessions Court, Madurai | Decided 6-4-2026
  • Background: Father and son picked up on June 19, 2020 for allegedly violating COVID-19 lockdown curfew; CBI later found no actual violation occurred
  • CBI involvement: TN government transferred case to CBI after Madras HC took suo motu cognisance; 2,027-page chargesheet; 52 of 135 witnesses examined
  • "Rarest of Rare" doctrine: First articulated in Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980) — death penalty only when life imprisonment is "unquestionably foreclosed"
  • Charges: Sections 302 (murder), 342 (wrongful confinement), 120-B (criminal conspiracy), 201 (destruction of evidence) of IPC, 1860
  • Additional penalty: ₹1.4 crore compensation ordered to victims' family
  • What's next: Convicts expected to appeal in Madras High Court; confirmation required by HC for death sentences under Section 413 CrPC/BNSS
  • Data context: India recorded 170 custodial deaths Jan–mid-March 2026 alone (Lok Sabha data)
CLAT Angle: This case is a goldmine for CLAT — "rarest of rare" doctrine (Bachan Singh v. Punjab), custodial rights under Articles 21 & 22, D.K. Basu guidelines (1997), CBI's role as a federal investigative agency, and the debate around abolition of death penalty. Also connects to the persistent question: India has never ratified UNCAT against torture.

Defence, Space & Technology

★ NUCLEAR TRIAD COMPLETE · APRIL 2026

INS Aridhaman Inducted — India Completes Nuclear Triad with 3rd SSBN

India's third nuclear-powered ballistic missile submarine (SSBN), INS Aridhaman, was handed over to the Indian Navy in April 2026, completing India's nuclear triad — the ability to deliver nuclear weapons from land, air, and sea. Defence Minister Rajnath Singh attended the handover ceremony. INS Aridhaman, built under India's classified Advanced Technology Vessel (ATV) project, belongs to the Arihant class and can launch K-4 and K-15 (Kalam series) nuclear-capable ballistic missiles.

  • Nuclear Triad: Land (ICBMs/Agni series) + Air (Su-30 MKI/Rafale with nuclear delivery) + Sea (SSBN submarines) = credible second-strike capability
  • Arihant Class SSBNs: INS Arihant (2016) → INS Arighat (2024) → INS Aridhaman (2026)
  • K-4 Missile: Range ~3,500 km (submarine-launched); K-15 (Sagarika): Range ~750 km
  • India's No First Use (NFU) Policy: India will not use nuclear weapons first; but promises "massive retaliation" if hit
  • Make in India: Built at Ship Building Centre, Visakhapatnam — demonstrates indigenous defence manufacturing
  • INS Taragiri (Apr 3): 4th stealth frigate of Nilgiri Class (Project 17A) also handed over; names reflect India's mountain ranges
CLAT Angle: India's nuclear doctrine (NFU), Make in India in defence, DRDO's role, and maritime security as part of India's strategic autonomy are important GK areas. The nuclear triad completes India's minimum credible deterrence posture.
NUCLEAR ENERGY · SHANTI BILL

SHANTI Bill: India Opens Nuclear Sector to Private Players

The SHANTI (Sustainable Harnessing and Advancement of Nuclear Technology for India) Bill, passed in December 2025 and now in implementation phase, repeals the Atomic Energy Act, 1962 to allow private companies to build, own, and operate nuclear plants — targeting 100 GW of nuclear capacity by 2047.

  • Earlier regime: Only NPCIL (state-owned) could operate nuclear plants under the Atomic Energy Act, 1962
  • India currently: 24 reactors; PHWR (Pressurised Heavy Water Reactor) is India's indigenous design
  • Target: 100 GW nuclear by 2047 (Viksit Bharat) — from current ~7 GW
  • Strategic: Nuclear = baseload power; 10x less land-intensive than solar/wind
CLAT: Atomic Energy Act (Union List, Entry 58), Parliament's exclusive power over nuclear energy, and India's nuclear liability framework (Civil Liability for Nuclear Damage Act, 2010) are important GK topics.

GST 2.0 · TAX REFORM

GST 2.0: Three-Slab Structure Approved; Compensation Cess Ends March 31, 2026

The GST Council approved a transition to a simplified three-slab GST structure: 5%, 18%, and 40% (replacing the earlier 4-tier system of 5%, 12%, 18%, 28%). The GST Compensation Cess — extended to service Union borrowings during COVID-19 — formally ended on March 31, 2026. The revised structure aims to reduce classification disputes and ease compliance.

  • GST launched: July 1, 2017 — "One Nation, One Tax"; Constitutional basis: Article 246A (inserted by 101st Amendment, 2016)
  • GST Council: Article 279A — chaired by Union Finance Minister; states have 2/3 voting weight
  • Compensation Cess: States guaranteed 14% revenue growth for 5 years; cess extended post-2022 for COVID borrowings
  • New apparel/footwear threshold: Tax-free up to ₹2,500 (from ₹1,000) — relief for lower-income consumers
  • GST revenue: India collected record ₹2.37 lakh crore in March 2026 (FY-end month)
CLAT Angle: Article 246A and the 101st Constitutional Amendment (GST), the GST Council's composition and powers, fiscal federalism, and India's indirect tax reforms are high-priority constitutional-economic CLAT topics.
PM MODI · CONSTITUTIONAL RECORD

PM Modi Becomes India's Longest-Serving Prime Minister — Constitutional Debate on Term Limits

In April 2026, PM Narendra Modi surpassed the tenure record of Jawaharlal Nehru to become India's longest-serving Prime Minister. This milestone has renewed the debate over whether India should codify executive term limits, as the Constitution provides no fixed cap on PM tenure.

  • Article 75: PM holds office at the pleasure of the President but must enjoy confidence of the Lok Sabha
  • No fixed term: Dr. Ambedkar argued continuous parliamentary accountability (questions, no-confidence motions) is more effective than fixed terms
  • Collective responsibility: Under Article 75(3), Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to Lok Sabha
  • UK analogy: No term limits in UK Westminster model either — India follows parliamentary supremacy
  • Nehru's tenure: May 1947 – May 1964 (17 years)
CLAT Angle: Article 74–75 (PM's appointment and tenure), collective responsibility vs. individual responsibility, and the no-confidence motion mechanism are standard CLAT polity questions.

Question 01 — Sabarimala / ERP Test

The 9-judge Constitution Bench hearing the Sabarimala review is examining the "Essential Religious Practices" (ERP) test. In which landmark case was this test first articulated? What does the test determine?

The ERP test was first articulated in Commissioner, Hindu Religious Endowments v. Sri Lakshmindra Thirtha Swamiar of Sri Shirur Mutt (1954) — commonly called the Shirur Mutt case. The test determines which religious practices are "essential" or "integral" to a religion and thereby protected under Article 25(1) of the Constitution. Only practices that pass this test get constitutional protection from State regulation. Critics argue that courts — not religious communities — end up deciding what is "essential" to a faith, which itself raises concerns about judicial overreach into religious matters.
Question 02 — Sathankulam / Death Penalty

The Sathankulam verdict applied the "rarest of rare" doctrine to award death penalty. In which case did the Supreme Court establish this doctrine, and what two-step test must a court apply before awarding capital punishment?

The "rarest of rare" doctrine was established in Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980) — a five-judge Constitution Bench upheld the constitutional validity of the death penalty but held it can only be imposed when: (1) the alternative of life imprisonment is "unquestionably foreclosed," and (2) the crime is so heinous that it shocks the collective conscience of society. Courts must balance aggravating vs. mitigating circumstances. Additionally, under Section 413 of the BNSS (formerly CrPC), every death sentence must be confirmed by the High Court before execution.
Question 03 — Nuclear Triad / INS Aridhaman

What is India's nuclear doctrine, and how does the completion of the nuclear triad with INS Aridhaman strengthen it?

India's nuclear doctrine, adopted in 2003, is based on: (1) No First Use (NFU) — India will not use nuclear weapons first against any state; (2) Credible Minimum Deterrence — maintaining only enough nuclear weapons to deter adversaries; and (3) Massive Retaliation — if attacked with nuclear weapons, India will respond with overwhelming force. The nuclear triad — land (Agni missiles), air (fighter aircraft), and sea (SSBNs like INS Aridhaman) — ensures India's second-strike capability: even if land and air bases are destroyed in a first strike, submarine-based missiles survive to retaliate. This makes India's NFU policy more credible and stabilises deterrence.
Question 04 — GST / Constitutional Amendment

GST was introduced through a constitutional amendment. Which amendment inserted Article 246A, what does it provide, and how is the GST Council constituted under the Constitution?

GST was introduced by the 101st Constitutional Amendment Act, 2016, which inserted Article 246A — granting Parliament and State Legislatures concurrent power to legislate on GST (except on petroleum, alcohol, and tobacco, which remain outside GST). The GST Council is established under Article 279A and consists of the Union Finance Minister (Chairperson), the Union Minister of State for Finance, and Finance Ministers of all States. Voting: Union government has 1/3 weight; States collectively have 2/3 weight. Decisions require a 3/4 majority. The GST Council is a quasi-federal institution — a cooperative federalism mechanism.
Question 05 — PM's Tenure / Article 75

PM Modi has become India's longest-serving PM. Under which Articles of the Constitution is the PM appointed? Can Parliament fix term limits for the PM by a simple amendment?

The PM is appointed under Article 75 — the President appoints the PM, and other ministers on the PM's advice. The PM holds office at the pleasure of the President but, by constitutional convention, continues as long as they command the confidence of the Lok Sabha (Article 75(3) — collective responsibility to Lok Sabha). Parliament cannot fix term limits for PM by a simple amendment — any such change would alter the basic structure of parliamentary democracy (which is part of the Basic Structure Doctrine from Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala, 1973). Parliamentary accountability — through no-confidence motions and Question Hour — is the constitutional check on PM tenure.
Question 06 — India-Bhutan / Neighbourhood Policy

India signed the Punatsangchhu-II hydropower agreement with Bhutan. Name India's overarching foreign policy for neighbouring nations, and explain Bhutan's unique constitutional status vis-à-vis India.

India's policy is called the Neighbourhood First Policy, prioritising relations with South Asian and Indian Ocean neighbours. Bhutan has a unique status: under the India-Bhutan Friendship Treaty of 1949 (renewed and updated in 2007), Bhutan agreed to be guided by India in its foreign policy in exchange for India's guarantee of Bhutan's security. Bhutan is not a SAARC founding member but joined in 1985. Hydropower is the backbone of Bhutan's economy — exports to India generate ~30% of Bhutan's GDP. The Punatsangchhu-II (1,020 MW) and Mangdechhu (720 MW) projects are joint India-Bhutan ventures. Bhutan also features in CLAT passages due to its unique Gross National Happiness (GNH) constitutional framework — the world's only constitution based on happiness rather than GDP.
