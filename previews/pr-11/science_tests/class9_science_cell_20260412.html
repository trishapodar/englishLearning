Q12.  [3 Marks]

Answer: Active transport is the movement of molecules from a region of lower concentration to higher concentration (against the concentration gradient) using energy (ATP).
Difference from passive: Passive transport does not require energy and substances move along the concentration gradient; active transport requires energy (ATP) and moves substances against the gradient.
Example: Absorption of minerals by root hair cells from soil — even when the concentration of minerals inside the root is already higher than in the soil, the plant actively absorbs more minerals using ATP.

Marking: Step 1 — Definition of active transport including "against gradient" and "energy (ATP)" (1 mark).
Step 2 — Contrast with passive transport (1 mark).
Step 3 — Correct example from plant cells (1 mark).

Accept: Glucose re-absorption in kidney tubules as an alternate example.

Common Error: Defining active transport as "faster" transport — speed is not the defining feature; energy use and gradient direction are.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q13.  [3 Marks] ★

Answer: Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) is isotonic to blood cells — RBCs maintain their shape. Distilled water is hypotonic to blood cells. When distilled water enters the bloodstream, the RBCs are surrounded by a solution of lower solute concentration (hypotonic). By osmosis, water enters the RBCs (endosmosis). The cells swell and may burst (haemolysis), causing rupture of red blood cells. This is a medical emergency.

Marking: Step 1 — Identifying distilled water as hypotonic (1 mark).
Step 2 — Explaining endosmosis / water moving into RBCs (1 mark).
Step 3 — Stating swelling and bursting / haemolysis (1 mark).

Accept: Lysis / haemolysis / cell bursting.

Common Error: Stating cells will shrink — this would happen in a hypertonic solution, not a hypotonic one.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5; NCERT Exemplar Ch. 5 Q. (HOTS)

Q14.  [3 Marks]

Answer: Structure of nucleus: The nucleus is bound by a double-layered nuclear membrane with nuclear pores for material exchange. Inside is the nucleoplasm containing:
(i) Chromosomes / Chromatin — carry genes (DNA); function: heredity and control of cell activities.
(ii) Nucleolus — small spherical body; function: synthesis of RNA and ribosomes.
(iii) Nuclear matrix (nucleoplasm) — gel-like fluid; function: supports the nuclear contents and allows enzymatic reactions.

Marking: Step 1 — Describing nuclear membrane and pores (1 mark).
Step 2 — Two correctly named components with functions (1 mark each; 2 marks for both).

Accept: Any three correctly named components with correct functions.

Common Error: Confusing nucleolus with nucleus; omitting functions.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5

Q15.  [5 Marks]

Answer:

Part (a) — 3 marks:
Robert Hooke (1665) — Observed dead cork cells and coined the term "cell".
Anton van Leeuwenhoek (1674) — First to observe living free-moving cells (bacteria, protists) using an improved microscope.
Matthias Schleiden (1838) — Concluded all plants are made of cells.
Theodor Schwann (1839) — Extended this to animals; co-proposed the Cell Theory.
Rudolf Virchow (1855) — Added that all cells arise from pre-existing cells ("Omnis cellula e cellula").

Part (b) — 2 marks:
(i) All living organisms, whether unicellular or multicellular, are made of cells — making the cell the basic structural unit.
(ii) All life processes (respiration, nutrition, excretion, reproduction) occur within the cell — making it the basic functional unit.

Marking: Part (a): 1 mark each for any three scientists with correct contributions (3 marks).
Part (b): 1 mark per valid reason (2 marks).

Accept: Any three correctly attributed scientists; any two valid justifications for the cell as fundamental unit.

Common Error: Attributing the Cell Theory solely to one person; not distinguishing structural from functional role.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5; NCERT Exemplar Ch. 5

Q16.  [5 Marks]

Answer:

Part (b) — 2 marks:
Endocytosis: The process by which a cell engulfs external material by the inward folding of the plasma membrane, forming a vesicle. The cell membrane wraps around the substance.
Exocytosis: The reverse process — vesicles inside the cell fuse with the plasma membrane and release their contents to the outside.
Importance for Amoeba: Since Amoeba has no mouth, it uses endocytosis (phagocytosis) to engulf food particles and exocytosis to expel undigested waste. These are its primary mechanisms for feeding and waste removal.

Marking: Part (a): 3 marks for a complete table with all four features correctly contrasted.
Part (b): Definition of endocytosis (0.5 mark) + exocytosis (0.5 mark) + importance for Amoeba (1 mark).

Accept: Any three valid rows in the table for full marks; any unicellular organism for the importance example.

Common Error: Confusing endocytosis/exocytosis with osmosis; stating Amoeba eats through a mouth.
