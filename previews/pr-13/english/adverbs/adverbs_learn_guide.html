What Is an Adverb?

The modifier that reaches beyond the noun — its essential grammar role

An adverb is a word (or phrase) that modifies a verb, an adjective, another adverb, or an entire clause. Adverbs answer the questions how?, when?, where?, how often?, how much?, or to what extent? The grammatical principle behind adverbs is modification: they add information without changing the core subject–predicate relationship of a sentence.

The word adverb comes from the Latin adverbium (“added to the verb”), but this etymology is misleading for learners who then assume adverbs only modify verbs. In Modern English, adverbs have a far wider scope—they can modify adjectives (very tall), other adverbs (quite slowly), prepositional phrases (right around the corner), and even entire sentences (Frankly, I disagree).

Prescriptive grammarians (those who describe rules that should be followed) traditionally define adverbs narrowly as modifiers of verbs, adjectives, and adverbs. Descriptive grammarians (those who describe how the language is actually used) broaden the category to include sentence adverbs, discourse markers, and focus particles. For examination purposes, both traditions are examinable, so this guide covers all subtypes.

What Can an Adverb Modify?

Target of Modification Example Question Answered
A verb She sings beautifully. How?
An adjective He is extremely tall. To what extent?
Another adverb She ran very quickly. How much?
A prepositional phrase Right before dawn. Exactly when?
A whole sentence Fortunately, no one was hurt. Speaker’s attitude
✓ The Four-Question Test for Adverbs
  • Does it modify a verb, adjective, or adverb? If yes → it is likely an adverb.
  • Does it answer how / when / where / why / how much / how often? A yes confirms adverbial function.
  • Can it be removed without making the sentence ungrammatical? Most adverbs are optional; removing them leaves a grammatically complete (if less informative) sentence.
  • Can you move it to a different position? Adverbs are more positionally flexible than adjectives, which must stay near their noun.
⚠ Not Every “–ly” Word Is an Adverb

Many learners treat the –ly suffix as a guaranteed adverb marker. This is incorrect. Several common –ly words are adjectives, not adverbs, and cannot modify verbs.

  • ✗ “He behaved friendly.” Wrong: friendly is an adjective; use in a friendly manner
  • ✓ “He behaved in a friendly manner.”
  • ✗ “She looked lovely at the verb.” Wrong: lovely is an adjective; it cannot modify a verb

Other adjectives ending in –ly: lively, lonely, elderly, cowardly, likely, timely, motherly.

✓ Topper’s Tip: Adverb vs. Adjective at a Glance

Ask: What word does it sit next to, and what word does it describe? If it describes a noun or pronoun → adjective. If it describes a verb, adjective, or adverb → adverb. The syntactic head being modified, not the form of the word, is the ultimate test.

Adverbs of Manner

How something is done — the most frequently tested adverb class

An adverb of manner describes how or in what way an action is performed. It modifies a verb or verb phrase and answers the question “How?” Most adverbs of manner are formed by adding –ly to an adjective (quick → quickly), though flat adverbs like fast, hard, straight, and well are notable exceptions.

Adverbs of manner are the largest and most productive class of adverbs in English. They are the primary vehicle for describing the quality of an action and carry significant expressive weight in both written and spoken registers. Because they are formed so regularly from adjectives, they are also a major source of examination errors.

Flat Adverbs

✓ Rules for Adverbs of Manner
  • Position: Typically placed after the verb or after the object if one exists — She spoke clearly / She explained the problem clearly.
  • Before the verb (emphatic): Adverbs of manner may precede the verb for emphasis in literary or formal writing — He carefully examined the document.
  • Flat adverbs have no –ly form: fast, hard, straight, late, early, high, deep, near are used directly as adverbs. Never add –ly to these.
  • Well vs. good: Well is the adverb of manner corresponding to the adjective good. Use well after action verbs; use good only before nouns or after linking verbs.
⚠ The “Hardly” / “Hard” Trap

Several –ly adverbs have a completely different meaning from the flat adverb they appear to derive from. Mixing them is a high-frequency error.

  • hard (manner: with effort) ≠ hardly (degree: scarcely, almost not)
  • late (time: after the expected time) ≠ lately (time: recently)
  • near (place: close) ≠ nearly (degree: almost)
  • high (place: at a high level) ≠ highly (degree: to a great degree)
  • ✗ “She works hardly.” Wrong: hardly means “scarcely”—the sentence implies she barely works at all
  • ✓ “She works hard.”
  • ✓ “The surgeon operated skillfully on the patient.” adverb of manner after verb + object
  • ✓ “Run fast or you will miss the bus.” flat adverb—do not write fastly
  • ✗ “She dances beautiful.” Wrong: adjective used where adverb is needed; correct form is beautifully
  • ✗ “He played the piano good.” Wrong: good is an adjective; use adverb well to modify the verb played

Adverbs of Time

When something happens — point of time, duration, and sequence

An adverb of time indicates when an action takes place, for how long it continues, or in what temporal sequence events occur. These adverbs answer the questions “When?”, “How long?”, and “In what order?” Grammatically, they function as temporal adjuncts—elements that can often be fronted or moved to the end of a clause without changing core meaning.

Three Sub-Categories of Time

Sub-Category Key Adverbs Example
Point of Time now, then, today, yesterday, soon, already, yet, still, immediately I will call you tomorrow.
Duration long, briefly, temporarily, permanently, always, forever She waited long for the reply.
Sequence / Order first, then, next, finally, subsequently, eventually, previously First, stir the mixture; then add the sugar.
✓ Still, Yet & Already — The Three Tense-Sensitive Adverbs
  • still: Used in affirmative and interrogative sentences to indicate an ongoing situation. Position: before the main verb or after be. — He still lives here.
  • yet: Used in negative sentences and questions to refer to something expected but not yet done. Position: end of clause. — She hasn’t arrived yet.
  • already: Used in affirmative sentences to indicate something happened sooner than expected. Position: mid-position (before main verb). — He has already left.
⚠ Yet in Affirmative Sentences

Using yet in a straightforward affirmative sentence is non-standard in formal English (though permissible in some informal American dialects). Examiners expect the rule: affirmative → already; negative/question → yet.

  • ✗ “I have yet finished my homework.” Wrong: affirmative sentence requires already, not yet
  • ✓ “I have already finished my homework.”
  • ✓ “Have you finished your homework yet?”
✓ No-the Rule with Time Adverbs

Never insert the definite article before single-word time adverbs. Yesterday, today, tomorrow, now, and soon do not take the when functioning as adverbs.

  • ✗ “I will meet you the tomorrow.”
  • ✓ “I will meet you tomorrow.”

Adverbs of Place & Direction

Where and in what direction an action occurs

An adverb of place (also called a locative adverb) indicates where an action takes place or the direction in which it moves. It answers the question “Where?” or “Which direction?” Many adverbs of place double as particles in phrasal verbs (look up, come in), where their meaning is idiomatic rather than purely locational.

Static Place
herethereinsideoutsidenearbyeverywhere
Direction

Relative Place

✓ Here / There — Inversion Rule
  • When a sentence begins with here or there (place adverbs), the subject and verb invert if the subject is a noun (not a pronoun).
  • “Here comes the bus.” (noun subject → inversion) grammatically correct
  • “Here it comes.” (pronoun subject → no inversion) grammatically correct
  • “There goes the last train.” (noun subject → inversion)
⚠ Adverb vs. Preposition: Same Word, Different Job

Words such as up, down, in, out, on, off, over, under can function as either an adverb (no object follows) or a preposition (an object follows). Confusing the two leads to parse errors in examinations.

  • ✓ “Please come in.” Adverb — no object; modifies verb come
  • ✓ “She walked in the room.” Preposition — the room is its object
  • ✓ “The children ran outside to play.” adverb of place; no object follows
  • ✓ “Everywhere she looked, she saw flowers.” fronted for emphasis; still adverbial
  • ✗ “Here comes he.” Wrong: pronoun subjects do not invert; correct form is Here he comes

Adverbs of Frequency

How often an action occurs — from always to never

An adverb of frequency expresses how often an action takes place. Frequency adverbs divide into definite frequency (exact repetition: daily, twice, every week) and indefinite frequency (approximate repetition: always, usually, sometimes, rarely, never). The two groups behave differently with respect to sentence position, which is the dominant examination focus.

The Frequency Scale (Indefinite)

Adverb Approx. Frequency Example
always 100% She always arrives on time.
usually / generally ~80% He usually takes the bus.
often / frequently ~60% They often disagree.
sometimes / occasionally ~40% I sometimes cook dinner.
seldom / rarely ~15% He seldom complains.
never / hardly ever 0–5% She never lies.
✓ Mid-Position Rule for Indefinite Frequency Adverbs
  • With auxiliary verbs: Place the frequency adverb after the first auxiliary — She has always been kind.
  • With main verb be: Place the adverb after be — He is always cheerful.
  • With all other main verbs: Place the adverb before the main verb — They often visit us.
  • Definite frequency adverbs (daily, twice) usually appear at the end of the clause — I exercise daily.
⚠ Negative Inversion with Seldom, Rarely, Never

When never, seldom, rarely, scarcely, or hardly ever is placed at the front of a sentence for emphasis, the subject and auxiliary must invert (negative fronting). Failure to invert is a topper-level error.

  • ✗ “Never I have seen such courage.” Wrong: subject-auxiliary inversion required after fronted negative adverb
  • ✓ “Never have I seen such courage.”
  • ✓ “Seldom does he arrive on time.”
✓ Double Negative Trap with Never/Hardly

Because never, hardly, scarcely, and rarely are already negative in meaning, adding another negative word (not) creates an ungrammatical double negative.

  • ✗ “I can’t never understand him.”
  • ✓ “I can never understand him.”

Adverbs of Degree & Intensity

Scaling adjectives and adverbs up or down — amplifiers and downtoners

An adverb of degree (also called a degree adverb or intensifier) modifies an adjective or another adverb (rarely a verb) to indicate the extent or intensity of a quality. They answer the question “How much?” or “To what degree?” Degree adverbs subdivide into amplifiers (increase the degree: very, extremely) and downtoners (decrease the degree: slightly, barely).

Type Key Words Example
Maximiser absolutely, completely, totally, utterly, perfectly She is absolutely right.
Amplifier very, extremely, highly, terribly, awfully, remarkably It was extremely cold.
Approximator almost, nearly, pretty, fairly, rather, quite He is quite intelligent.
Downtoner slightly, a little, barely, scarcely, hardly, somewhat She was slightly annoyed.
✓ Gradable vs. Non-Gradable Adjectives
  • Gradable adjectives can be modified by standard amplifiers (very cold, extremely tired). They have comparative and superlative forms.
  • Non-gradable (absolute/extreme) adjectives already represent the maximum of a scale: perfect, unique, dead, impossible, enormous, furious. They cannot logically be very + adjective.
  • With non-gradable adjectives, use maximisers (absolutely, completely, utterly), not very.
⚠ Very + Absolute Adjective Trap
  • ✗ “The view was very unique.” Wrong: unique is absolute; something cannot be more or less unique — use absolutely unique or simply unique
  • ✗ “He was very furious.” Wrong: furious is an extreme adjective; use absolutely furious
  • ✓ “The view was absolutely unique.”
  • ✓ “He was absolutely furious.”
⚠ Quite — The Shape-Shifting Adverb

Quite has two distinct meanings depending on the adjective that follows it—a fact that trips up even advanced learners.

  • Before a gradable adjective, quite = fairly / moderately: “The film was quite good” (moderately good).
  • Before a non-gradable adjective, quite = absolutely / completely: “That is quite impossible” (absolutely impossible).
✓ Rather vs. Fairly vs. Pretty

These three approximators are often confused. Rather often implies a slight negative or surprising quality (rather rude), while fairly is more neutral (fairly simple). Pretty is informal and common in American English but avoided in formal writing.

Adverbs of Probability & Certainty

Expressing likelihood and the speaker’s epistemic stance

An adverb of probability expresses the speaker’s assessment of the likelihood of an event. These adverbs are epistemic in nature—they encode the speaker’s knowledge state rather than factual information about the world. Key examples: certainly, definitely, probably, perhaps, maybe, possibly, presumably, undoubtedly, apparently.

Certainty Level Adverb Example
Very certain certainly, definitely, undoubtedly She will certainly pass.
Probable probably, presumably, doubtless It will probably rain.
Possible possibly, perhaps, maybe, conceivably Perhaps he forgot.
Apparently reported apparently, supposedly, allegedly He is apparently ill.
✓ Maybe vs. May Be
  • maybe (one word) is an adverb meaning perhaps. It typically appears at the start of a clause.
  • may be (two words) is a modal verb + be. It functions as a predicate.
  • “Maybe he is right.” → adverb
  • “He may be right.” → modal + adjective complement
⚠ Probably in Negative Sentences

In negative sentences, probably and definitely can attach to either the auxiliary or the negative, creating a difference in meaning. Examinations test this distinction.

  • “She probably won’t come.” More natural; probably scopes over the negative predicate
  • “She won’t probably come.” Awkward; avoid in formal writing
✓ Perhaps & Maybe — Position Note

Perhaps and maybe are typically placed at the beginning of a sentence as sentence adverbs, though they can appear in mid-position. They never take subject–auxiliary inversion (unlike negative adverbs).

Interrogative & Relative Adverbs

When, where, why, and how as question-words and clause-linkers

Interrogative adverbs (when, where, why, how) introduce direct and indirect questions and function adverbially within their clause. Relative adverbs are the same words used to introduce relative clauses that modify a preceding noun of time, place, or reason. The grammatical principle is subordination: the adverb simultaneously connects the clause to the main sentence and fills an adverbial role inside its own clause.

Word Interrogative Use Relative Use
when When will she arrive? the day when we met
where Where do you live? the town where he grew up
why Why did you leave? the reason why she cried
how How did you do it? the way how she smiled ✗
✓ Relative Adverbs vs. Relative Pronouns
  • A relative pronoun (who, which, that) replaces a noun inside the relative clause: the book that I read (= I read the book).
  • A relative adverb (when, where, why) replaces an adverbial phrase: the year when he was born (= he was born in that year).
  • Test: Replace the relative adverb with a preposition + pronoun. If it works, the original is a relative adverb: the town where she lived = the town in which she lived.
⚠ The “The Way How” Error

English does not permit the way how. Either the way alone (without any relative word) or how alone (without the way) is acceptable. Using both is a pleonasm.

  • ✗ “I admire the way how she speaks.” Wrong: double marking of manner—use one or the other
  • ✓ “I admire the way she speaks.”
  • ✓ “I admire how she speaks.”

Conjunctive Adverbs

Adverbs that connect independent clauses — a topper’s punctuation challenge

A conjunctive adverb (also called a connective adverb or adverbial conjunction) is an adverb that connects two independent clauses by expressing a logical relationship between them (contrast, cause-effect, sequence, addition, etc.). Unlike coordinating conjunctions (and, but, so), conjunctive adverbs are not true conjunctions—they cannot join clauses with a comma alone. They require a semicolon before them or a full stop and a new sentence.

Contrast

Cause/Result
thereforeconsequentlythushenceaccordingly
Addition
furthermoremoreoverbesidesadditionally
Time/Sequence
subsequentlymeanwhilefinallythen
✓ The Semicolon Rule for Conjunctive Adverbs

When joining two independent clauses, the pattern is:

Independent Clause 1 → ; conjunctive adverb, → Independent Clause 2
  • “The report was incomplete; however, the committee accepted it.”
  • “She studied hard; therefore, she passed.”
  • A comma after the conjunctive adverb is optional for shorter adverbs (thus, hence) but standard for longer ones (however, therefore).
⚠ The Comma Splice — Using However with Only a Comma

This is one of the most common punctuation errors at all levels. A comma splice occurs when two independent clauses are joined by only a comma + conjunctive adverb (without a semicolon or full stop).

  • ✗ “The test was difficult, however, I passed.” Wrong: comma splice—a comma before however is insufficient to join two independent clauses
  • ✓ “The test was difficult; however, I passed.”
  • ✓ “The test was difficult. However, I passed.”
✓ Distinguishing However (Adverb) from However (Subordinator)

However can also function as a subordinating conjunction meaning in whatever way or no matter how. In this use, no semicolon is needed: “However hard he tries, he fails.” The key difference: as a conjunctive adverb it joins two full clauses; as a subordinator it introduces a dependent clause.

Formation of Adverbs

Morphological rules: suffixes, spelling changes & irregular forms

Most English adverbs are formed by adding the suffix –ly to an adjective. However, a significant number of adjectives undergo spelling changes before –ly, some adjectives cannot form adverbs this way at all, and a set of important adverbs are irregular or identical to their adjective form. Mastery of formation rules prevents spelling errors in written examinations.

Spelling Rules for –ly Formation

Adjective Pattern Rule Adjective → Adverb
Most adjectives Add –ly slow → slowly, loud → loudly
Ends in –y Change y to i, add –ly happy → happily, lazy → lazily
Ends in –le Drop e, add –y gentle → gently, simple → simply
Ends in –ic Add –ally basic → basically, dramatic → dramatically
Ends in –ll Add –y only full → fully, dull → dully
Ends in –ue Drop e, add –ly true → truly, due → duly
✓ Irregular and Flat Adverbs
  • good → well (not goodly): She sings well.
  • fast, hard, late, high, deep, long, near, far, straight, wide — same form as adjective: drive fast, work hard.
  • public → publicly (not publically): the –ic rule overrides the temptation to double –l.
  • whole → wholly (not wholely): drop the e before –ly.
⚠ The –ic Exception: Public vs. Tragic

The rule is “adjectives ending in –ic add –ally”—but public is a notable exception: publicly, not publically (which is non-standard).

  • ✓ basic → basically, tragic → tragically, economic → economically
  • ✓ public → publicly Exception—not “publically”
  • ✗ public → publically Wrong: non-standard form
✓ “Truly” vs. “Truely” — Silent ‘e’ Drop

Adjectives ending in a silent e + ly: always drop the e before adding –ly. Retaining the e is a frequent spelling error.

  • true → truly   whole → wholly   due → duly   awe → awfully (bonus: ful drops an l too)

Position of Adverbs

Front, mid, and end position — rules that determine meaning and style

The position of an adverb within a sentence is a core grammatical concern because placement can alter meaning, emphasis, and grammatical acceptability. English recognises three canonical positions: front position (before the subject), mid-position (between subject and main verb, or after first auxiliary), and end position (after the main verb or its object). Different adverb classes have different positional preferences.

Positional Preferences by Adverb Class

Adverb Type Preferred Position(s) Example
Manner End (most common); mid (formal) She spoke clearly. / She clearly spoke.
Place End; occasionally front The cat sat outside.
Time End or front; not mid Yesterday I saw him. / I saw him yesterday.
Frequency (indefinite) Mid (canonical) She often reads.
Degree Immediately before the word they modify Very tall; ran quite fast.
Sentence adverbs Front (+ comma); occasionally end Fortunately, the plan worked.
✓ The SAVE Order — When Multiple Adverbs Cluster at End Position

When two or more adverbs of different types appear in end position, the conventional English order is S-A-V-E:

S = Style/Manner → A = PlAce → V = FreVuency → E = TimE
  • ✓ “She danced gracefully (manner) on the stage (place) every evening (frequency) last summer (time).”
  • ✗ “She danced last summer every evening on the stage gracefully.” Wrong: reversed order sounds unnatural
⚠ The Adverb-Between-Verb-and-Object Rule

In Standard English, an adverb of manner must not be placed between a transitive verb and its direct object. This is the “verb-object split” error.

  • ✗ “She reads carefully the book.” Wrong: manner adverb cannot split verb from its object
  • ✓ “She reads the book carefully.”
  • ✓ “She carefully reads the book.” mid-position is also acceptable
⚠ The Split Infinitive Debate

A split infinitive occurs when an adverb is placed between to and the bare infinitive: “to boldly go”. Prescriptive grammarians traditionally condemn this. Descriptive grammarians accept it when ambiguity would result from placing the adverb elsewhere. For formal examinations, avoid splitting infinitives unless doing so would change the meaning of the sentence.

  • ✗ “She decided to quickly leave.” Prescriptively incorrect split infinitive
  • ✓ “She decided to leave quickly.”

Comparison of Adverbs

Positive, comparative, and superlative degrees in adverbs

Like adjectives, many adverbs can be compared across three degrees: positive (base form: quickly), comparative (comparing two: more quickly), and superlative (comparing three or more: most quickly). Most adverbs form their comparative and superlative analytically using more and most, but a set of common short adverbs use inflectional forms (–er / –est).

Positive Comparative Superlative Type
fast faster fastest Inflectional
hard harder hardest Inflectional
late later latest / last Inflectional
quickly more quickly most quickly Analytic
carefully more carefully most carefully Analytic
well better best Irregular
badly worse worst Irregular
far farther / further farthest / furthest Irregular
little less least Irregular
much more most Irregular
✓ Farther vs. Further
  • farther is used for literal, measurable physical distance: “He ran farther than his brother.”
  • further is used for figurative or abstract extension: “The committee discussed the matter further.”
  • In British English, further is acceptable in both senses. In formal examinations, observe the distinction.
⚠ Double Comparison Error

A double comparative or double superlative uses both an inflectional ending and an analytic word. This is non-standard in formal English.

  • ✗ “She worked more harder than ever.” Wrong: double comparative—use either harder or more hard
  • ✗ “He ran the most fastest.” Wrong: double superlative—use fastest alone
  • ✓ “She worked harder than ever.”
✓ “Than” vs. “Then” in Comparisons

Than is the conjunction used in comparisons; then is an adverb of time or sequence. Confusing them is an elementary but penalised error.

  • ✗ “She runs faster then him.”
  • ✓ “She runs faster than him.”

Adverbs vs. Adjectives

The most common source of grammatical confusion at every level

Adverbs and adjectives are frequently confused because they look similar (both often end in –ly forms), they can appear near the same words in a sentence, and some words function as both depending on context. The core grammatical distinction is: adjectives modify nouns and noun equivalents (appearing before the noun or after a linking verb); adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, and other adverbs.

Linking Verbs — The Critical Battleground

The most frequent adverb–adjective confusion centres on linking verbs: be, seem, appear, become, feel, look, smell, taste, sound, remain, stay, grow. These verbs do not describe an action—they link the subject to a description of it. Therefore, the word after a linking verb must be an adjective (a subject complement), not an adverb.

Sentence Verdict Reason
“The soup smells good.” ✓ Correct smells is a linking verb; good (adj) is the subject complement
“The soup smells well.” ✗ Wrong well (adverb) cannot be a subject complement after a linking verb
“She looks beautiful.” ✓ Correct looks = linking verb; beautiful is adjective subject complement
“She looks beautifully.” ✗ Wrong Adverb cannot serve as subject complement after linking verb looks
“He felt bad about it.” ✓ Correct felt = linking verb; bad is adjective complement
“He felt badly about it.” ✗ Wrong* Non-standard; felt is linking, not action, so adverb badly is incorrect
✓ The Action vs. Linking Verb Test
  • Replace the verb with a form of be. If the sentence still makes sense with essentially the same meaning, the verb is a linking verb → use an adjective.
  • “The roses smell sweet.” → “The roses are sweet.” ✓ Makes sense → sweet (adjective) is correct.
  • “He looked at the painting carefully.” → “He was at the painting carefully.” ✗ Doesn’t make sense → looked is an action verb → carefully (adverb) is correct.
⚠ Drive Careful vs. Drive Carefully

Action verbs must always be modified by adverbs. This seems obvious in theory but is frequently violated in informal speech, where adjectives are substituted for adverbs after action verbs.

  • ✗ “Drive careful.” Wrong: careful is an adjective; action verb drive needs adverb carefully
  • ✓ “Drive carefully.”
  • ✗ “Think deep about this.” Wrong in formal contexts; the action verb think requires deeply
  • ✓ “Think deeply about this.”
✓ Well — Adjective or Adverb?

Well has a special dual status. As an adverb, it modifies action verbs (She sings well). As an adjective, it describes a person’s health after a linking verb (She is not well today = She is not in good health). This is one of the few cases where the “same word” legitimately functions as both, so context is essential.

Sentence & Focus Adverbs

Adverbs that comment on the whole clause or single out a constituent

Sentence adverbs (also called disjuncts) modify an entire sentence or clause rather than a single word. They express the speaker’s attitude towards, or evaluation of, the proposition contained in the sentence: fortunately, surprisingly, frankly, apparently. Focus adverbs (also called particles of focus) single out a particular element for emphasis: only, even, just, merely, also, mainly. Their placement is critical because it changes meaning.

Sentence Adverbs (Disjuncts)

Sentence adverbs stand outside the main predication—they are parenthetical comments by the speaker. A comma is standard after a sentence adverb at the front of a clause. They can be tested by the paraphrase “It is [adjective] that…”: “Fortunately, she survived” paraphrases to “It is fortunate that she survived.”

Focus Adverbs: Position Changes Meaning

✓ The “Only” Placement Rule

The focus adverb only should be placed immediately before the constituent it modifies. Moving it changes the meaning of the sentence entirely. This is a favourite examination question type.

  • “Only I gave money to the charity.” → No one else gave money.
  • “I only gave money to the charity.” → I gave (but nothing else—didn’t volunteer etc.).
  • “I gave only money to the charity.” → I gave money, not goods.
  • “I gave money only to the charity.” → The charity was the exclusive recipient.
⚠ Hopefully — Prescriptive vs. Descriptive War Zone

The use of hopefully as a sentence adverb (“Hopefully, she will recover”) has been criticised prescriptively on the grounds that hopefully should only mean “in a hopeful manner” (manner adverb). Descriptively, the sentence-adverb use is now well established in all registers. For formal examinations, be aware of both usages and avoid it in very formal writing when an unambiguous alternative exists.

  • ✓ “She looked at him hopefully.” Manner adverb—universally accepted
  • ✓ “Hopefully, the train won’t be late.” Sentence adverb—accepted in modern usage
✓ Even — Scalar Focus Adverb

Even as a focus adverb places the modified element on a scale and implies that what is said is at the extreme end of expectation. Its position, like only, is immediately before what it focuses on. “Even children understood it” implies that children are the least expected group to understand—yet they did.

Common Errors & Traps

The definitive error compendium — from foundation mistakes to topper-level pitfalls

This chapter consolidates every major trap associated with adverbs in English grammar. Each entry is presented with a wrong example, an explanation of the grammatical principle being violated, and the correct form. Systematic revision of these errors is the highest-yield activity for competitive examinations.

Foundation-Level Errors

  • ✗ “She is a fastly runner.” Wrong: fast is a flat adverb with no –ly form; and here it should be adjective fast modifying the noun runner
  • ✗ “He plays the guitar good.” Wrong: action verb plays requires adverb well, not adjective good
  • ✗ “I did real well on the exam.” Wrong: real is an adjective; use adverb really to modify another adverb (well)
  • ✗ “She spoke very angrily at him.” Acceptable, but note: at is required with shout angrily/yell angrily not speak angrily at—check collocations
  • ✗ “The test was the yesterday.” Wrong: time adverbs do not take the definite article

Intermediate-Level Errors

  • ✗ “I have yet received my results.” Wrong: yet in affirmative sentences is non-standard; use already
  • ✗ “She is still not yet arrived.” Wrong: still and yet are redundant together here; use one only
  • ✗ “He works hardly every day.” Wrong: hardly means scarcely; use flat adverb hard to mean with effort
  • ✗ “I can’t barely hear you.” Wrong: double negative; barely is already negative—use I can barely hear you
  • ✗ “She reads carefully the instructions.” Wrong: manner adverb cannot split transitive verb from its object; place after object
  • ✗ “It was an absolutely tired performance.” Wrong: absolutely is a maximiser for non-gradable adjectives; tired is gradable—use very tired

Advanced and Topper-Level Errors

  • ✗ “Never she has betrayed her friends.” Wrong: fronted negative adverb never requires subject-auxiliary inversion: Never has she…
  • ✗ “The committee met frequently, however, it made no decision.” Wrong: comma splice; however as conjunctive adverb requires semicolon before it
  • ✗ “She decided to immediately resign.” Avoid: split infinitive; rewrite as decided to resign immediately for formal writing
  • ✗ “He is very exhausted.” Wrong: exhausted is an extreme adjective; use maximiser absolutely or completely
  • ✗ “I admire the way how she leads.” Wrong: pleonasm—use the way alone or how alone, never both
  • ✗ “She ran more faster than him.” Wrong: double comparative; use either faster or more fast (latter is rare)
  • ✗ “Only I told him the truth.” Ambiguous: if the intended meaning is that I told no one except him, place only before him: I told only him the truth
  • ✗ “The view was very unique.” Wrong: unique is absolute; cannot be intensified with very—use absolutely unique or just unique
