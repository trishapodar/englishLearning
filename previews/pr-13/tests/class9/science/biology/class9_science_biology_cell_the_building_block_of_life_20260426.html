Q2.   Option (iii)   [1 Mark(s)]

The correct answer is (iii): Water moved into Cell X and moved out of Cell Y through the cell membrane.

→ Cell X is in a hypotonic solution (pure water); water enters by osmosis → cell swells.

→ Cell Y is in a hypertonic solution (concentrated salt); water exits by osmosis → cell shrinks.

Osmosis is the movement of water molecules from a region of higher water concentration to a region of lower water concentration through a selectively permeable membrane.

Marking: 1 mark for selecting option (iii). No partial credit.
Accept: Option iii / (iii) / third option

Q1.   Three guided differentiations — 1 mark each   [3 Mark(s)]

(i) Cell membrane vs Cell wall — Permeability (1 mark):
Cell membrane is selectively permeable — it allows only certain substances to pass through, regulating entry and exit. Cell wall is fully (freely) permeable — it allows all substances to pass through without restriction.

(ii) RER vs SER — Structure (1 mark):
RER (Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum) has ribosomes attached on its outer surface, giving it a rough, granular appearance. SER (Smooth Endoplasmic Reticulum) lacks ribosomes on its surface and therefore has a smooth appearance.

(iii) Chloroplasts vs Chromoplasts — Pigments (1 mark):
Chloroplasts contain the green pigment chlorophyll along with other photosynthetic pigments (xanthophylls, carotenoids). Chromoplasts contain coloured pigments other than chlorophyll, such as carotenoids (yellow, orange, red) — they do not perform photosynthesis.

Marking: 1 mark for each of (i), (ii), and (iii). Each mark is awarded for one clear, accurate point of contrast on the specified basis only.
Accept: For (i): 'cell membrane is semi-permeable; cell wall is freely permeable'. For (ii): 'ribosomes present on RER, absent on SER'. For (iii): any mention of chlorophyll in chloroplasts vs carotenoids/non-chlorophyll pigments in chromoplasts.
Common Error: For (i): students compare function (protection vs transport) instead of permeability. For (iii): students confuse chromoplasts with chromosomes, or describe function instead of pigment type.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5 — The Fundamental Unit of Life

Q13.   Apoptosis; yes, plants can develop tumours (crown gall disease)   [3 Mark(s)]

Phenomenon and explanation (2 marks): Apoptosis (programmed cell death) is the phenomenon that inhibits tumour formation in the human body. In normal cells, when they become old, damaged, or potentially cancerous, they undergo a controlled, self-destructive process called apoptosis, preventing them from dividing uncontrollably. Cancer cells lose the ability to undergo apoptosis and ignore normal cell-cycle regulatory signals (due to mutations in control genes), so they divide continuously and form tumours. (2 marks)

Tumours in plants (1 mark): Yes, plants can also develop tumours. A well-known example is crown gall disease, caused by the soil bacterium Agrobacterium tumefaciens. This bacterium inserts a segment of its DNA (T-DNA from the Ti plasmid) into plant cells, causing uncontrolled cell division similar to tumour formation in animals. (1 mark)

Marking: 1 mark: Apoptosis named as the inhibitory phenomenon. 1 mark: Explanation of how apoptosis prevents tumours OR how cancer cells bypass it. 1 mark: Plants can develop tumours — crown gall / Agrobacterium tumefaciens as example.
Accept: Accept 'programmed cell death' in place of 'apoptosis'. For plants: accept 'gall formation' or any valid reference to plant tumours.
Common Error: Students describe only cancer cell behaviour without naming the specific inhibitory mechanism (apoptosis). Many students incorrectly state that plants cannot develop tumours.

Q6.   Structural and functional similarities and differences between mitochondria and chloroplasts   [5 Mark(s)]

Similarities (2 marks):

Structural similarity (1 mark): Both mitochondria and chloroplasts are double membrane-bound organelles. Both contain their own circular DNA and 70S ribosomes, indicating their semiautonomous nature and endosymbiotic origin. Both have an inner membrane system that increases surface area — cristae in mitochondria; thylakoids (arranged as grana) in chloroplasts.

Functional similarity (1 mark): Both are energy-transducing organelles — they convert one form of energy into another. Both produce ATP: mitochondria through cellular respiration (chemical energy from glucose → ATP); chloroplasts through photosynthesis (light energy → chemical energy stored in glucose and ATP).

Differences (3 marks):

Structural differences (1 mark): Mitochondria have a folded inner membrane called cristae; chloroplasts have a flat inner membrane system of thylakoids organised into grana, embedded in fluid-filled stroma. Chloroplasts contain the green pigment chlorophyll in thylakoid membranes; mitochondria contain no pigments.

Functional differences (1 mark): Mitochondria perform aerobic cellular respiration — breaking down glucose in the presence of oxygen to release energy as ATP (catabolic). Chloroplasts perform photosynthesis — using light energy to synthesise glucose from CO₂ and water (anabolic). Mitochondria consume oxygen; chloroplasts release oxygen.

Distribution (1 mark): Mitochondria are present in virtually all aerobic eukaryotic cells (both plant and animal cells). Chloroplasts are found only in plant cells and photosynthetic protists (e.g., algae) — they are absent in animal cells and non-photosynthetic plant parts such as roots.

Marking: 1 mark: Structural similarity (double membrane + own DNA/ribosomes). 1 mark: Functional similarity (both produce ATP / energy transduction). 1 mark: Structural difference (cristae vs thylakoids/grana; chlorophyll only in chloroplasts). 1 mark: Functional difference (respiration/catabolism vs photosynthesis/anabolism; O₂ consumed vs released). 1 mark: Distribution difference (mitochondria in all eukaryotes; chloroplasts in plants/algae only).
Accept: Accept tabular or prose format. Award marks for each correct, distinct, well-explained point.
Common Error: Students list only differences and omit similarities. Students confuse cristae (mitochondria) with thylakoids (chloroplasts). Students incorrectly state mitochondria are found only in animal cells — they are present in all eukaryotes including plant cells.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5 — The Fundamental Unit of Life, sections on Mitochondria and Plastids

Q10.   Osmosis: hypertonic interior draws water into cups B and C; Cup A is control; boiled cells in D lack functional membrane   [5 Mark(s)]

(i) Water in Cup B and Cup C (2 marks):

→ Sugar (Cup B) and salt (Cup C) dissolved in the hollow of the potato cups create a hypertonic solution inside the cup compared to the surrounding water in the beaker and within the potato cells. By osmosis, water moves from a region of higher water concentration (potato cells / surrounding water) through the selectively permeable cell membrane into the region of lower water concentration (the hypertonic solution in the hollow). This water accumulates visibly in the hollowed portions of Cup B and Cup C. (2 marks)

(ii) Why Cup A is necessary (1 mark):

→ Cup A is the negative control of the experiment. It is empty (no solute) and shows what happens to a normal potato cup under identical conditions but without any concentration gradient. Without Cup A, we cannot confirm that the water movement observed in other cups is specifically due to the solute (osmosis) and not due to some other variable such as temperature, time, or natural moisture release from the potato. (1 mark)

(iii) No water in Cup A and Cup D (2 marks):

→ Cup A (empty): There is no solute inside the hollow, so no osmotic concentration gradient is created. Without a gradient, there is no net movement of water into the hollow. (1 mark)

→ Cup D (boiled potato + sugar): Boiling kills the potato cells and denatures the membrane proteins, destroying the selectively permeable nature of the cell membrane. Dead, damaged membranes cannot facilitate osmosis. Even though sugar is present, water cannot move into the hollow by osmosis because the functional membrane required for osmosis no longer exists. (1 mark)

Marking: 2 marks for (i): osmosis named + hypertonic solution explanation + direction of water movement. 1 mark for (ii): Cup A as control — what it controls for. 2 marks for (iii): 1 mark for Cup A (no solute/no gradient), 1 mark for Cup D (boiling kills cells/destroys membrane permeability).
Accept: For (i): accept 'water enters by osmosis due to higher solute concentration inside the cup'. For (iii/D): accept 'cell membrane is damaged or denatured by boiling'.
Common Error: For (i): students say 'sugar/salt attracts water' without mentioning osmosis or semi-permeable membrane. For (iii/D): students explain Cup D as 'sugar doesn't work in boiled potato' without linking to membrane integrity being destroyed by heat.

Ref: NCERT Class 9 Science, Ch. 5 — The Fundamental Unit of Life, section on Osmosis; NCERT Exemplar Class 9 Activity

Q16.   Osmosis/plasmolysis for preservation; hypertonic environment kills microbes; healthy recipe example; scientific and social values   [5 Mark(s)]

(i) Scientific concept (1 mark): The farmer has applied the concept of osmosis, specifically exosmosis and plasmolysis, for food preservation. By adding high concentrations of salt, sugar, or jaggery, she creates a hypertonic environment around microbial cells. (1 mark)

(ii) Mechanism of preservation (2 marks): When salt or sugar is added in high concentrations, it creates a hypertonic environment relative to the interior of bacterial and fungal cells. The cells of these microorganisms have a lower solute concentration inside compared to the surrounding preserved medium. As a result, water moves out of the microbial cells by osmosis (exosmosis). This leads to plasmolysis — the cell membrane shrinks away from the cell wall as the cell loses water — causing the microbial cells to dehydrate, become metabolically inactive, and eventually die. With the spoilage-causing microorganisms incapacitated, the food is preserved. (2 marks)

(iii) Healthy recipe (1 mark): Any scientifically sound example — e.g., Amla murabba: blanch whole amla briefly, then immerse in thick sugar syrup (≥60–70% concentration) in sterilised glass jars; seal and store. Alternatively: lemon and ginger pickle with rock salt (no oil); raw mango slice preserve with salt and jaggery. (1 mark)

(iv) Scientific values (1 mark): Any two relevant values from: application of scientific knowledge to solve a practical problem; sustainable thinking and reduction of post-harvest food waste; entrepreneurship supported by science; food security and community welfare; evidence-based innovation linking traditional practice with biological principles.
