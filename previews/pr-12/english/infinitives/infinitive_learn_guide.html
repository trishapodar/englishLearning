What Is an Infinitive?

The base form of a verb—the most fundamental non-finite verb form in English

An infinitive is the base (dictionary) form of a verb, used either with the particle to (to write) or without it (write). It is a non-finite verb form: it carries no tense of its own and does not agree with any subject. Grammatically, the infinitive occupies a unique position—it functions simultaneously as a verb (taking objects and adverbs) and as a noun, adjective, or adverb.

Every English verb has an infinitive form, and that form is the one listed in the dictionary. When you look up ran, the dictionary redirects you to run—that base form is the infinitive. The critical insight at the foundation level is distinguishing between finite verbs (which express tense, person, and number) and non-finite verbs (which do not).

Finite vs. Non-Finite: The Core Distinction

A finite verb is limited (finis = Latin for “end” or “limit”) by tense, person, and number. A non-finite verb is not so limited—it has no built-in tense marker and cannot stand alone as the predicate of a clause. The infinitive is one of three non-finite forms in English, the others being the gerund (-ing form used as a noun) and the participle (-ing or -ed form used as an adjective).

Form Example Type Role in sentence
writes “She writes novels.” Finite verb Main predicate; tied to 3rd-person singular present
to write “She wants to write novels.” To-infinitive Object of wants; no tense of its own
write “She must write novels.” Bare infinitive Complement of modal must
writing “Writing novels is hard.” Gerund Subject of the sentence (noun function)
✓ The Golden Rule of Infinitives
  • Non-finite = no agreement: the infinitive never changes for person or number. “I want to go,” “She wants to go,” “They want to go”—the infinitive to go is identical in all three.
  • Two forms exist: the to-infinitive (to + base verb) and the bare infinitive (base verb alone). Which one to use is determined entirely by the governing word—not by the speaker’s preference.
  • Verb properties retained: an infinitive can still take a direct object (to write a letter), an indirect object (to give her a book), and adverbial modifiers (to run quickly).
⚠ Infinitive vs. Gerund—They Are NOT Interchangeable

At foundation level, students frequently confuse the infinitive with the gerund. Both are non-finite, but their grammatical roles often differ and some verbs strictly require one or the other.

  • ✗ “She enjoys to swim.” Wrong: enjoy requires a gerund object, not a to-infinitive.
  • ✓ “She enjoys swimming.”
  • ✗ “He decided going.” Wrong: decide requires a to-infinitive object.
  • ✓ “He decided to go.”
✓ Topper’s Insight: Prescriptive vs. Descriptive Grammar

In descriptive grammar (how language is actually used), the boundary between infinitive and gerund has blurred in some constructions—native speakers often treat them interchangeably with start, begin, continue. In prescriptive grammar (the formal rules tested in examinations), each verb has a stipulated behaviour. This guide follows prescriptive norms throughout, noting where natural usage diverges.

Forms of the Infinitive

Simple, perfect, continuous, passive—a complete taxonomy of infinitive forms

The infinitive is not a single, invariable form. English allows a range of complex infinitive structures that encode temporal relationships (perfect), aspect (continuous), and voice (passive). Every complex infinitive is built by combining the base infinitive to be or to have with a participle. Recognising these forms is essential for advanced writing and comprehension.

The simple infinitive refers to an action that is concurrent with, or future to, the action of the main verb. The perfect infinitive refers to an action prior to the main verb’s time. The continuous infinitive emphasises ongoing action. The passive infinitive indicates that the subject receives the action rather than performing it. These distinctions operate independently of the finite verb’s tense.

Form Structure Example Time relation
Simple active to + V to write Same time or future
Simple passive to be + V-ed to be written Same time or future; subject receives action
Perfect active to have + V-ed to have written Earlier than main verb
Perfect passive to have been + V-ed to have been written Earlier; subject receives action
Continuous active to be + V-ing to be writing Ongoing at reference time
Perfect continuous to have been + V-ing to have been writing Ongoing before reference time
✓ Building Complex Infinitives—The Formula

Every complex infinitive follows a predictable auxiliary-participle chain:

to → have / be (auxiliary) → V-ed / V-ing (participle)
  • Perfect: to have + past participle → “She seems to have forgotten.”
  • Passive: to be + past participle → “The letter needs to be signed.”
  • Continuous: to be + present participle → “He appeared to be sleeping.”
  • Perfect passive: to have been + past participle → “The error appears to have been corrected.”
  • ✓ “The suspect is believed to have escaped through the window.” Perfect passive infinitive; action (escaping) precedes the belief.
  • ✓ “She pretended to be working when I arrived.” Continuous infinitive; ongoing action at the time of arrival.
  • ✗ “He seems to be left the office already.” Wrong: past-action meaning requires perfect infinitive to have left, not continuous to be left.
  • ✓ “He seems to have left the office already.”
⚠ Confusing Perfect and Simple Infinitives after Modal-like Expressions

The distinction is tested heavily in gap-fill and error-correction tasks. After expressions of belief, appearance, or probability (seems, appears, is believed, is said), use the perfect infinitive when the infinitive action precedes the main verb’s time.

  • ✗ “She is said to win the prize last year.” Wrong: last year is past relative to is said; must use perfect infinitive.
  • ✓ “She is said to have won the prize last year.”

The To-Infinitive: Core Uses

The seven main grammatical functions of to + base verb

The to-infinitive is the form examined most extensively in grammar tests. It performs a remarkable range of functions—it can be the subject of a sentence, the object of a verb, the complement of a noun, or an adverbial of purpose. Understanding which function is active in a given sentence prevents both analytical errors and production errors.

✓ Seven Core Functions of the To-Infinitive
  • Subject: “To err is human.” — The infinitive phrase acts as a noun performing the role of subject.
  • Object of a verb: “She decided to leave.” — The infinitive is the direct object of a transitive verb.
  • Subject complement: “Her ambition is to succeed.” — Completes the subject after a linking verb.
  • Object complement: “They elected him to lead.” — Describes or renames the object after certain verbs.
  • Post-nominal modifier (adjective function): “She had a report to write.” — Modifies a noun.
  • Adverb of purpose: “He left early to catch the train.” — Expresses the purpose of the main action.
  • Adverb of result / comment: “She grew up to become a doctor.” — Expresses an outcome or evaluative comment.

✓ Topper’s Test: Identifying Infinitive Function

To identify the function of a to-infinitive, apply this sequence of diagnostic questions:

  • Does it answer “What is the subject?” → Subject function.
  • Does it answer “What did the subject [verb]?” → Object function.
  • Does it follow a linking verb and rename the subject? → Complement function.
  • Does it answer “Why?” or “For what purpose?” → Adverbial of purpose.
  • Does it follow a noun and say more about it? → Post-nominal modifier.
⚠ “In Order To” vs. Bare “To” for Purpose

To alone is the neutral marker of purpose. In order to and so as to are more emphatic and formal; they are obligatory when a negative purpose clause is needed.

  • ✗ “He whispered to not wake the baby.” Wrong: negative purpose requires so as not to or in order not to.
  • ✓ “He whispered so as not to wake the baby.”
  • ✓ “He whispered in order not to wake the baby.”

The Bare Infinitive: Core Uses

When and why the particle to is dropped—the grammatical principles explained

The bare infinitive (also called the plain infinitive or base form) is used in specific, grammatically determined environments. The to is absent not because of stylistic choice but because the governing element already provides directionality or modality. Students who add to in these contexts commit a basic but heavily penalised error.

The bare infinitive is the base form of the verb used without to. It appears after modal auxiliaries, after causative and perception verbs in certain patterns, after had better / would rather / would sooner, and in a handful of fixed expressions. The grammatical principle is that the governing element already supplies the relational meaning that to would otherwise contribute.

✓ Four Environments Requiring the Bare Infinitive
  • After modal auxiliaries (can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must, ought to — note that ought uniquely retains to): “You must leave.”
  • After causative make and let in the active voice: “She made him apologise.” “Let me help you.”
  • After perception verbs (see, hear, watch, feel, notice) when the whole action is perceived: “I saw him run across the street.”
  • After had better, would rather, would sooner, had sooner, cannot but, cannot help but: “You had better leave now.”
Governing word Bare infinitive ✓ To-infinitive ✗
must You must go. ✗ You must to go.
make (active) They made her sign. ✗ They made her to sign.
let Let him speak. ✗ Let him to speak.
had better You had better hurry. ✗ You had better to hurry.
would rather I’d rather stay. ✗ I’d rather to stay.
⚠ Ought to—The Modal That Keeps Its To

Ought is the single modal auxiliary that retains the particle to. Dropping it is a common trap.

  • ✗ “You ought go now.” Wrong: ought always requires to; unlike other modals.
  • ✓ “You ought to go now.”
✓ Perception Verb + Bare vs. -ing—Meaning Matters

After perception verbs, both bare infinitive and -ing are possible, but they carry different meanings. The bare infinitive indicates a complete action witnessed from start to finish; the -ing form indicates an action witnessed in progress.

  • “I saw him cross the street.” — I saw the entire crossing.
  • “I saw him crossing the street.” — I saw him in the middle of crossing.

Infinitive as Subject

How a verb phrase becomes the subject of a sentence

When a to-infinitive or infinitive phrase functions as the grammatical subject of a sentence, the entire phrase is treated as a singular noun for the purposes of verb agreement. This construction is common in formal and literary prose, though everyday speech tends to replace it with the anticipatory it construction.

An infinitive subject occurs when a to-infinitive phrase occupies the subject slot of the main clause. The principle is that the infinitive, despite being a verb form, undergoes nominalisation (noun-like conversion) at the level of clause structure. The predicate verb agrees with it as though it were a third-person singular noun phrase.

✓ Infinitive Subject—Agreement and Transformation Rules
  • Agreement: The main verb is always singular when an infinitive phrase is the subject. “To err is human”—never are.
  • Extraposition with it: Heavy infinitive subjects are routinely moved to the end, replaced by the dummy subject it: “It is difficult to master a new language.” Both forms are grammatically equivalent.
  • Formal vs. informal register: The front-placed infinitive subject (“To err is human”) is more literary and aphoristic; the extraposed form (“It is human to err”) is more common in conversation and modern writing.
[To + V phrase] → is/was/seems → [predicate]
  • ✓ “To understand the problem is the first step.” Infinitive phrase as subject; verb is is singular.
  • ✓ “It is the first step to understand the problem.” Extraposed version with dummy it; both are equally correct.
  • ✗ “To make mistakes are natural.” Wrong: despite the sense of plurality, the infinitive phrase subject takes a singular verb—is not are.
  • ✓ “To make mistakes is natural.”
⚠ Confusing Infinitive Subject with Gerund Subject

Both infinitive and gerund can be subjects. In most contexts they are interchangeable, but subtle tonal differences exist: the gerund is more idiomatic in informal prose; the infinitive is more hypothetical or prescriptive in flavour. Examiners test this distinction in register-based questions.

  • “Smoking is bad for you.” — General habitual activity (gerund, informal register).
  • “To smoke is to court disaster.” — Emphatic, aphoristic, literary register (infinitive).

Infinitive as Object

Verbs that take a to-infinitive as their direct object

The most common use of the to-infinitive is as the direct object of a finite verb. This pattern, often called verb + infinitive, is governed entirely by the lexical choice of the main verb. There is no general rule by which you can predict whether a verb takes an infinitive or gerund object; this must be learned verb by verb—though semantic groupings help.

When a to-infinitive functions as the object of a transitive verb, it completes the meaning of that verb by stating what the subject intends, hopes, decides, or attempts to do. The grammatical principle is that certain verbs project their meaning into a subsequent event or state, and the infinitive represents that projected eventuality.

Semantic Groupings of Verb + To-Infinitive

While there is no algorithmic rule, verbs that take infinitive objects tend to fall into semantic clusters. Recognising these clusters helps predict the pattern for new or unfamiliar verbs.

✓ Verb + Object + To-Infinitive Pattern

Some verbs take a noun / pronoun object between the main verb and the infinitive. In this pattern, the noun/pronoun is the logical subject of the infinitive action:

Main Verb → Object (noun/pronoun) → To-Infinitive
  • “She asked him to leave.” — him is the one who leaves.
  • “They persuaded the committee to approve the plan.”
  • “I want you to understand this.”
  • Verbs in this pattern include: ask, tell, order, allow, permit, encourage, persuade, advise, warn, invite, require, expect, want, need.
⚠ Tell and Ask—Object Is Mandatory

The verbs tell and ask (in the sense of requesting or instructing) require a noun/pronoun object before the infinitive. Omitting it produces an ungrammatical sentence.

  • ✗ “She told to leave.” Wrong: tell in this sense requires an object person.
  • ✓ “She told him to leave.”
  • ✗ “They asked to go immediately.” Ambiguous at best; if the meaning is “they requested someone else to go,” an object is required. Only if the subject is asking permission for themselves is the objectless form acceptable.

Infinitive as Complement

Subject complements and object complements formed by infinitive phrases

Beyond its subject and object roles, the infinitive frequently appears as a complement—a clause element that completes the meaning of a subject or an object rather than adding new information. Distinguishing complements from objects is a precision skill tested in parsing and analysis questions.

A subject complement (also called a predicative complement) follows a linking verb (primarily be, but also seem, appear, remain, become) and says something about the subject. A object complement follows a transitive verb’s direct object and says something about that object. The to-infinitive can fill either role.

Type Structure Example
Subject complement Subject + linking verb + to-inf “Her dream is to travel the world.”
Subject complement Subject + linking verb + to-inf “The plan was to meet at dawn.”
Object complement Verb + Obj + to-inf “They elected her to serve as chair.”
Object complement Verb + Obj + to-inf “I consider it wise to wait.”
✓ Testing for Subject Complement vs. Object
  • Subject complement test: Can you substitute the infinitive for the subject? If so, it is a complement. In “Her goal is to succeed,” to succeed = her goal (they refer to the same thing).
  • Object test: Does the infinitive follow a non-linking transitive verb? Is it a different entity from the subject? Then it is an object. In “She wants to succeed,” the infinitive is what she wants—it is the object of wants.
⚠ Complement vs. Object—A Subtle but Tested Distinction

Examiners sometimes ask students to identify whether an infinitive is the object or the subject complement. The key: after a linking verb, the infinitive is a complement; after an action/transitive verb, it is an object.

  • “She wants to win.” — to win is the object (action verb wants).
  • “Her desire is to win.” — to win is the subject complement (linking verb is).

Infinitive as Adjective

Post-nominal infinitive phrases that modify nouns

When a to-infinitive follows a noun and modifies it (telling us more about the noun), it functions as an adjective—more precisely, as a post-nominal modifier. This is one of the most elegant and compact structures in English, allowing whole verbal predicates to serve the role normally played by a single adjective or relative clause.

An infinitive functioning as an adjective always appears immediately after the noun it modifies—never before it (unlike most adjectives). The grammatical principle behind this is that the infinitive, as a complex predicative element, cannot be placed in the pre-nominal slot reserved for single-word adjectives. It is semantically equivalent to a reduced relative clause: “a book to read” = “a book that you can/should read.”

✓ Three Sub-types of Post-nominal Infinitive Modifier
  • With implicit modal meaning: The infinitive suggests obligation, possibility, or purpose. “She had homework to finish.” = work she should finish.
  • After first, last, next, only and ordinal numerals: “She was the first woman to walk on the moon.” “He was the only student to pass.”
  • After abstract nouns derived from verbs (ability, decision, desire, effort, failure, plan, refusal, tendency): “Her ability to concentrate impressed us.” “The decision to withdraw was unanimous.”
  • ✓ “He was the last person to leave the building.” Post-nominal modifier after last + noun.
  • ✓ “She showed a remarkable ability to adapt under pressure.” Infinitive modifies abstract noun ability.
  • ✗ “She showed a to-adapt remarkable ability.” Wrong: post-nominal infinitives cannot be placed in pre-nominal position.
  • ✓ “There is plenty of work to be done.” Passive infinitive used post-nominally; subject of work-doing is unspecified.
✓ Preposition Stranding in Post-nominal Infinitives

When an infinitive modifier modifies a noun that is semantically the object of a preposition within the infinitive phrase, the preposition is stranded (left at the end). This is grammatically correct in standard English despite old prescriptive objections.

  • “She needed a chair to sit on.” — She sits on the chair. The preposition on is correctly stranded.
  • “He had no one to talk to.” — He talks to someone. Stranding is correct.
  • ✗ “He had no one to talk.” Omitting the preposition entirely is ungrammatical.

Infinitive as Adverb

Purpose, result, condition, and comment—the adverbial functions of the infinitive

The to-infinitive is extremely productive as an adverbial modifier. It can modify verbs (expressing purpose or result), adjectives (expressing the stimulus for a feeling or a range of application), and even entire clauses (as an evaluative or commenting adverb). This flexibility makes the adverbial infinitive one of the most frequently tested structures.

✓ Five Adverbial Functions of the To-Infinitive
  • Purpose (the most common): “She saved money to buy a car.” — Answers why or for what purpose.
  • Result / outcome: “She lived to regret that decision.” — The result of living was regret.
  • Cause / reason (after adjectives of emotion): “I was shocked to hear the news.” — The infinitive gives the cause of the emotion.
  • Condition (formal register): “To hear him speak, you would think him a genius.” — = If you were to hear him speak…
  • Commenting adverb (with only, never): “She arrived early, only to find the doors locked.” — Unexpected or ironic result.
Function Marker / Pattern Example
Purpose to / in order to / so as to “He practised daily to improve his technique.”
Result enough to / too… to / only to “She was tall enough to reach the shelf.”
Cause of emotion adjective + to + V “We were delighted to receive your letter.”
Condition initial To-V phrase “To see her dance, you’d be amazed.”
Commenting only to / never to “He climbed to the top, only to slip and fall.”
⚠ “Too… To” and “Enough… To”—Word Order Traps

These result patterns have fixed word order that cannot be altered. Too precedes the adjective/adverb it modifies; enough follows the adjective but precedes the noun it modifies.

  • ✗ “He is enough strong to lift it.” Wrong: enough must follow the adjective it modifies: strong enough.
  • ✓ “He is strong enough to lift it.”
  • ✗ “She is too much tired to continue.” Wrong: too directly precedes the adjective with no intervening word.
  • ✓ “She is too tired to continue.”
✓ The “Only To” Construction—A Topper’s Favourite

The only to infinitive conveys a sense of bitter irony or unexpected disappointing outcome. It is a hallmark of sophisticated writing and frequently appears in advanced comprehension passages.

  • “He spent years building the company, only to watch it collapse overnight.”
  • “She ran to the station, only to find that the last train had already gone.”

A systematic reference of verbs that govern the to-infinitive—without exception

The single largest category of examination error is choosing the wrong non-finite form after a governing verb. This chapter provides a comprehensive, systematically organised reference of verbs that are always followed by the to-infinitive. These must be memorised; there is no reliable grammatical rule that predicts them from first principles.

A verb that exclusively takes a to-infinitive as its complement does so because its semantic profile projects a future, intended, or unrealised event. Verbs Followed by Bare Infinitive

Modal auxiliaries, causatives, and perception verbs—the complete picture

A strictly limited set of verbs and expressions requires the bare infinitive (without to). These cannot be grouped by meaning alone—they must be learned as a closed list. The most common exam trap is adding to where it must be absent. The governing grammatical principle is that these verbs already carry the directional or modal force that to would supply, making its addition redundant and ungrammatical.

✓ The Complete Set of Bare-Infinitive Governors
  • Modal auxiliaries: can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must, dare (modal use), need (modal use)
  • Semi-modal / idiomatic expressions: had better, would rather, would sooner, had sooner, had rather, cannot but, cannot help but, need not (modal)
  • Causatives make and let (active voice only)
  • Perception verbs in active voice (complete action): see, hear, watch, feel, notice, observe, perceive
  • Verb help (optionally bare in informal/American English)
Category Correct (bare) Wrong (with to)
Modal She can swim. ✓ She can to swim. ✗
Modal You must try. ✓ You must to try. ✗
Semi-modal You had better go. ✓ You had better to go. ✗
Causative She made him wait. ✓ She made him to wait. ✗
Perception I heard him sing. ✓ I heard him to sing. ✗
⚠ Make in the Passive Requires To

This is one of the most frequently tested rules in all of English grammar. When make (causative) is converted to the passive, the bare infinitive becomes a to-infinitive. The rationale is that in the passive, the “makefulness” of the causative is expressed through the passive construction itself; the infinitive must restore its particle.

  • ✓ Active: “They made him sign the document.” — Bare infinitive.
  • ✓ Passive: “He was made to sign the document.” — To-infinitive.
  • ✗ Passive: “He was made sign the document.” Wrong: passive of make always requires to-infinitive.
✓ Dare and Need—Modal vs. Main Verb Behaviour

Dare and need function as either modal auxiliaries (no to, no third-person -s) or main verbs (with to, with -s). The choice changes the meaning subtly and the grammar significantly.

  • “He dare not mention it.” (modal—bare infinitive; mainly British) vs. “He doesn’t dare to mention it.” (main verb)
  • “Need he go so soon?” (modal) vs. “Does he need to go so soon?” (main verb)

Verbs Taking Either Form

When choice of infinitive or gerund changes—or doesn’t change—meaning

A critical subset of English verbs can be followed by either the to-infinitive or the gerund (-ing form). For some of these, the two forms are freely interchangeable with negligible meaning difference. For others, the choice produces a significant and examinable shift in meaning. Mastering this class is the single highest-yield area for advanced grammar marks.

Verbs that accept both infinitive and gerund complements fall into two groups: (A) meaning-neutral pairs, where the choice of form is stylistic; and (B) meaning-sensitive pairs, where the choice of form signals a different tense relationship, a different aspect (completive vs. habitual), or a different logical proposition (prospective vs. retrospective).

⚠ Stop to Do vs. Stop Doing—The Most Tested Pair

This is the single most tested meaning-sensitive pair in competitive examinations. The trick: with stop + to-infinitive, the infinitive is an adverb of purpose, not the object of stop. The object of stop is always a gerund.

  • ✓ “He stopped smoking.” — He quit the habit (gerund = object of stop).
  • ✓ “He stopped to smoke.” — He paused [his walk] in order to smoke (infinitive = purpose adverb).
  • ✗ “He stopped to smoke means he gave up smoking.” Wrong interpretation: this meaning requires the gerund form.

The Perfect Infinitive

To have + past participle—encoding anteriority, unfulfilment, and regret

The perfect infinitive (to have + past participle) is one of the most nuanced and frequently misused structures in English. It encodes the idea that the infinitive action is prior to or contrary to fact in relation to the main verb’s time. It appears after modal auxiliaries, semi-modals, and raising verbs, and its misuse is one of the clearest markers of intermediate-level fossilisation.

The perfect infinitive (to have + V-ed) signals that the event expressed by the infinitive is located at a time earlier than the reference time of the main verb, or that the event was expected, obliged, or possible but did not occur. This second use—the unreal or hypothetical perfect infinitive—is particularly important after modal auxiliaries (should have, could have, must have, ought to have).

Use 1: Anteriority (Earlier Action)

  • ✓ “The fire appears to have started in the kitchen.” The fire started earlier than it appears now; perfect infinitive after raising verb.
  • ✓ “She is believed to have authored three anonymous novels.” Authorship is prior to the current belief.

Use 2: Unfulfilment after Modals (The “Modal + Perfect” System)

This is the system examined most heavily at topper level. When a modal auxiliary is combined with the perfect infinitive, the result is a retrospective modal expressing what was (but wasn’t) possible, obligatory, advisable, or permissible in the past.

Modal + have + V-ed Meaning Example
should have + V-ed Past obligation that was not fulfilled; reproach “You should have called ahead.”
could have + V-ed Past possibility that was not realised “She could have been a champion.”
must have + V-ed Logical deduction about a past event “He must have forgotten.”
may have + V-ed Past possibility (uncertain) “She may have already left.”
might have + V-ed Past remote possibility “He might have survived.”
needn’t have + V-ed Past action performed unnecessarily “You needn’t have rushed.”
ought to have + V-ed Past moral obligation unfulfilled “He ought to have apologised.”
would have + V-ed Past conditional (hypothetical result) “I would have helped if asked.”
⚠ Needn’t Have vs. Didn’t Need To

This pair is a classic examination trap with a significant meaning difference:

  • “You needn’t have hurried.” — You did hurry, but it was unnecessary. (The action took place.)
  • “You didn’t need to hurry.” — Either you hurried or you didn’t; merely states that hurrying wasn’t required. (Neutral about whether the action occurred.)
  • ✗ Using needn’t have when the action never happened is therefore logically contradictory.
✓ Must Have vs. Can’t Have—Logical Deduction Pair

Must have + V-ed expresses positive deduction (I am sure this happened); can’t have / couldn’t have + V-ed expresses negative deduction (I am sure this did not happen). Examiners love testing which is appropriate in context.

  • “She must have taken the wrong bus.” — I’m certain she did.
  • “He can’t have known about it.” — I’m certain he didn’t.
14

Continuous & Passive Infinitives

To be + -ing and to be + past participle—aspect and voice in the infinitive system

Two further infinitive forms—the continuous infinitive and the passive infinitive—complete the grammar of the infinitive system. Both are formed with the verb be as an auxiliary, and both carry systematic semantic weight that examination candidates must understand precisely.

✓ Continuous Infinitive—Form and Meaning

Structure: to be + V-ing

  • Ongoing action at reference time: “She seemed to be enjoying herself.” — Enjoyment was in progress at the time of seeming.
  • Future arrangement: “He is to be leaving tomorrow.” — Formally scheduled departure.
  • After modals (ongoing): “You should be working right now.” — Activity expected to be in progress.
✓ Passive Infinitive—Form and Meaning

Structure: to be + V-ed (past participle)

  • Subject receives the action: “The letter needs to be signed.” — Someone must sign it; the letter is the recipient.
  • After modals: “This rule must be observed.” — Obligation; the rule is the patient of observing.
  • In passive transformations: “She wanted to be promoted.” — She wanted someone to promote her.
Active infinitive Passive equivalent Meaning shift
He expects to complete the work. He expects the work to be completed. Agent shifts from subject to unspecified third party
She wants to invite everyone. She wants to be invited. Desire changes from giving to receiving an invitation
They need to fix the machine. The machine needs to be fixed. Topic shifted; subject of needs is now the patient
⚠ Need + -ing vs. Need to Be + V-ed

When need is used as a main verb with an inanimate subject, English allows a special construction where the gerund form has passive meaning without a passive auxiliary. Both structures are correct but differ in register.

  • ✓ “The car needs washing.” — Active gerund, passive meaning; informal.
  • ✓ “The car needs to be washed.” — Passive infinitive; neutral/formal.
  • ✗ “The car needs to wash.” Wrong: active infinitive implies the car washes something else, not that it needs washing.
✓ Perfect Continuous and Perfect Passive Infinitives

These complex forms are rare but appear in advanced texts. Knowing them marks a topper candidate:

  • Perfect continuous: “She appears to have been working all night.” — Ongoing past action prior to present.
  • Perfect passive: “The law is said to have been changed.” — Past event, passive, prior to present belief.
15

The Split Infinitive

The grammar, the history, and the facts about “boldly going”

Few grammatical topics have generated as much controversy as the split infinitive. A split infinitive occurs when an adverb or other modifier is placed between the particle to and the base verb. The “rule” prohibiting split infinitives was a 19th-century prescriptive imposition derived from Latin grammar; Latin infinitives are single words and therefore literally cannot be split. Modern grammarians and style guides overwhelmingly agree that the split infinitive is grammatically acceptable in English.

A split infinitive is an infinitive in which an adverb (or other modifier) is inserted between the particle to and the base verb: “to boldly go,” “to fully understand,” “to carefully consider.” The term itself is slightly misleading because to is not technically part of the verb—it is a grammatical particle whose bond with the base form is strong but not identical to the bond between morphemes within a single word.

✓ When Splitting Is Preferred, Neutral, or to Be Avoided
  • Splitting is preferred when moving the adverb outside the infinitive would change the meaning or create ambiguity: “The plan is to more than double profits”—you cannot sensibly move more than elsewhere.
  • Splitting is neutral in informal and journalistic writing where it causes no ambiguity: “She decided to quietly leave.”
  • Splitting is best avoided in formal academic, legal, and official writing, where the conservative reader may view it as careless: avoid “to hereby certify” in legal documents if “hereby to certify” or a rewrite is available.
Split infinitive Unsplit version Meaning preserved?
“to boldly go” “to go boldly” Yes — rewrite acceptable
“to further improve” “further to improve” / “to improve further” Partially — nuance shifts
“to more than double profits” Not possible without structural rewrite No — split required
“to completely rewrite the clause” “to rewrite the clause completely” Yes — rewrite preferred in formal prose
⚠ Examination Context Matters

In Indian competitive examinations (SSC, UPSC, IELTS, TOEFL error correction), split infinitives are sometimes marked as errors because the test follows conservative prescriptive norms. In international academic writing (CAE, IELTS band 8+), avoiding splits in formal essays is the safe choice. Know your examination board’s standard.

✓ Topper’s Principle: Prescriptive vs. Descriptive on Split Infinitives

Descriptive linguistics (Quirk, Huddleston & Pullum Cambridge Grammar of the English Language) unambiguously endorses the split infinitive as standard English. Prescriptive handbooks (Fowler, Strunk & White older editions) advise avoidance. At topper level, the best answer acknowledges both positions and applies the relevant standard to the context in question.

16

Negative Infinitives

How to negate an infinitive phrase—and why position matters

Negating an infinitive requires careful attention to position: the negative particle not must be placed before the to—or, in the bare infinitive, immediately before the base verb. Placing not after to (producing a split negative infinitive like to not go) is acceptable in informal English but is the weaker prescriptive choice and can be penalised in formal writing contexts.

A negative infinitive is formed by placing not immediately before the infinitive marker to: not to go, not to speak, not to have seen. The position of not in relation to to and the base verb determines whether the negation targets the infinitive itself or the entire proposition.

✓ Rules for Negative Infinitives
  • Standard position: not precedes to: “He told her not to worry.”
  • In negative purpose clauses: Use so as not to or in order not to: “She tiptoed so as not to wake anyone.”
  • Bare infinitive negation: not precedes the base verb: “He told me not [to] move.” (When the bare infinitive is a complement, not is directly prefixed.)
  • Perfect negative infinitive: not to have + V-ed: “It seems not to have worked.”
  • ✓ “The doctor advised him not to smoke.” not precedes to; standard prescriptive form.
  • ✓ “She agreed not to reveal the secret.”
  • ✗ “She agreed to not reveal the secret.” Acceptable informally but weaker prescriptively; not should precede to.
  • ✓ “He left early so as not to cause a disturbance.”
  • ✗ “He left early to not cause a disturbance.” Wrong for negative purpose: must use so as not to or in order not to.
⚠ Negating the Purpose vs. Negating the Verb

Consider the ambiguity in placing not in different positions. Only not to (negating the full infinitive phrase) vs. to not (informal split) are at issue; but the purpose clause negative is a distinct structural requirement.

  • ✓ “I am writing not to complain but to offer a suggestion.” — Two contrasted infinitives, first negated.
  • ✗ “He whispered to not wake the baby.” Wrong negative purpose: must use so as not to wake the baby.
17

Infinitives After Adjectives & Nouns

The rich system of infinitive complements in adjective and noun phrases

Beyond their verbal contexts, infinitives are heavily used as complements of certain adjectives and nouns. These patterns, collectively called adjective + infinitive and noun + infinitive complements, represent a major source of examination questions at the advanced level because they involve subtle meaning distinctions and structural variations that parallel the verb + infinitive system.

Adjective + To-Infinitive Patterns

Many English adjectives take a to-infinitive as a complement—a following phrase that completes the adjective’s meaning. These fall into four semantic sub-types: (1) adjectives of willingness / reluctance; (2) adjectives of emotion / reaction; (3) adjectives of ease / difficulty; and (4) adjectives of likelihood / certainty. Each sub-type has a distinct structural pattern that the student must internalise.

Type Adjectives Pattern Example
Willingness willing, ready, reluctant, eager, anxious, loath Subject + be + adj + to-inf “She was eager to help.”
Emotion/reaction glad, happy, sad, sorry, surprised, shocked, delighted, horrified Subject + be + adj + to-inf “I was shocked to hear the news.”
Ease/difficulty easy, hard, difficult, impossible, tough, simple It + be + adj + to-inf or Object + be + adj + to-inf “It is easy to make mistakes.” / “Mistakes are easy to make.”
Likelihood likely, unlikely, certain, sure, bound Subject + be + adj + to-inf “She is likely to succeed.”
⚠ The “Tough Movement” Trap in Ease/Difficulty Adjectives

With adjectives like easy, difficult, hard, impossible, the surface subject of the sentence is actually the logical object of the infinitive. This is called tough movement in linguistics. The two surface forms are logically equivalent but structurally very different.

  • ✓ “It is difficult to satisfy her.” = “She is difficult to satisfy.”
  • ✗ “She is difficult to satisfy her.” Wrong: once she is moved to subject position (tough movement), the pronoun copy her must be deleted.
  • ✗ “It is difficult to satisfy.” Incomplete: the infinitive lacks an object; what is being satisfied is unstated.

Noun + To-Infinitive Complements

Many abstract nouns—especially those derived from verbs—take a to-infinitive complement. This mirrors the corresponding verb pattern. The principle is that any noun derived from a verb that takes an infinitive object will itself take an infinitive complement.

✓ Likely vs. Probable—A Structural Contrast

Likely allows the person or thing as subject before the infinitive. Probable does not—it requires an impersonal it or a that-clause. This is a topper-level distinction.

  • ✓ “She is likely to win.” — Personal subject + likely + infinitive.
  • ✗ “She is probable to win.” Wrong: probable does not permit this construction.
  • ✓ “It is probable that she will win.” — Only correct pattern for probable.

Common Errors & Traps

A diagnostic compendium of the most heavily tested infinitive mistakes

This final chapter consolidates the most frequently tested, most commonly made, and most heavily penalised errors in infinitive usage. Every trap listed here has appeared in major competitive, academic, and proficiency examinations. Work through each systematically—the ability to identify and correct these errors in real time is the hallmark of an examiner-ready candidate.

⚠ Trap 1: Adding To after Modal Auxiliaries

The most basic error—inserting to after modals (except ought) because the to-infinitive is familiar.

  • ✗ “He can to swim very well.” Wrong: modal auxiliaries take bare infinitive without exception.
  • ✓ “He can swim very well.”
  • ✗ “She would to prefer tea.”
  • ✓ “She would prefer tea.”
⚠ Trap 2: Forgetting To in Passive of Make

The active causative make takes a bare infinitive; the passive form be made requires a to-infinitive. This shift is missed by the majority of intermediate students.

  • ✗ “She was made work overtime every day.” Wrong: passive of make requires to-infinitive.
  • ✓ “She was made to work overtime every day.”
⚠ Trap 3: Using Simple Infinitive Instead of Perfect Infinitive

When the infinitive action precedes the main verb’s time, or when a past obligation was unfulfilled, the perfect infinitive is required.

  • ✗ “He should call you yesterday.” Wrong: past unfulfilled obligation requires should have called.
  • ✓ “He should have called you yesterday.”
  • ✗ “The thief appears to leave through the roof.” Wrong: the leaving happened before the present appearance; use to have left.
  • ✓ “The thief appears to have left through the roof.”
⚠ Trap 4: Using Stop to Do When Stop Doing Is Meant
  • ✗ “The government has stopped to fund the project.” Wrong if meaning cessation: this says the government halted in order to fund. Use gerund for cessation.
  • ✓ “The government has stopped funding the project.” — The funding has ended.
⚠ Trap 5: Remember / Forget + Infinitive vs. Gerund
  • ✗ “I remember to meet her five years ago.” Wrong: past memory requires gerund. Remember to = prospective reminder.
  • ✓ “I remember meeting her five years ago.”
  • ✗ “I forgot locking the door and came back to check.” Wrong: the subject came back because they didn’t lock it; use to-infinitive for unfulfilled action.
  • ✓ “I forgot to lock the door and came back to check.”
⚠ Trap 6: Wrong Negative Infinitive Form in Purpose Clauses
  • ✗ “She moved quickly to not miss the bus.” Wrong: negative purpose requires so as not to or in order not to.
  • ✓ “She moved quickly so as not to miss the bus.”
⚠ Trap 7: Enough and Too Word-Order Errors
  • ✗ “He is enough old to understand.” Wrong: enough must follow adjectives and adverbs, not precede them.
  • ✓ “He is old enough to understand.”
  • ✗ “The box is too heavy for lift.” Wrong: to is required before the infinitive after too… to.
  • ✓ “The box is too heavy to lift.”
⚠ Trap 8: Likely / Certain Used Without Infinitive vs. Probable
  • ✗ “She is probable to pass the exam.” Wrong: probable does not take a personal subject + infinitive construction.
  • ✓ “She is likely to pass the exam.”
  • ✓ “It is probable that she will pass the exam.”
⚠ Trap 9: Incorrect Subject of the Infinitive (Dangling Infinitive)

The implied subject of an infinitive phrase must match the grammatical subject of the main clause. When it does not, a dangling infinitive results—a logical error that examiners penalise heavily.

  • ✗ “To write clearly, good vocabulary is needed.” Wrong: good vocabulary cannot “write clearly”; the implied subject of to write is a person.
  • ✓ “To write clearly, you need good vocabulary.”
⚠ Trap 10: Omitting the Preposition in Post-nominal Infinitive
  • ✗ “She had no one to talk.” Wrong: talk requires the preposition to because you talk to someone.
  • ✓ “She had no one to talk to.”
  • ✗ “I needed a pen to write.” Wrong: if the intended meaning is “a pen with which to write,” the preposition with must be retained or the sentence rewritten. Without with, the meaning is that the pen will write something—illogical if a pen is the subject.
  • ✓ “I needed a pen to write with.”
