What Is a Verb?

The engine of every English sentence — expressing action, state, or occurrence

A verb is a word or group of words that expresses an action, a state of being, or an occurrence. Grammatically, every finite clause must contain a verb; it is the only obligatory element of a predicate and is the syntactic head of the verb phrase (VP).

Verbs are the engines of English sentences. Without a verb, a group of words may contain nouns, adjectives, and adverbs, yet it remains a fragment—not a clause. Every complete thought, from the simplest exclamation to the most complex subordinate clause, revolves around a verb.

Linguists distinguish between a prescriptive view (the rules grammarians codify) and a descriptive view (how native speakers actually use verbs). At topper level, you must understand both: you apply the prescriptive standard in formal writing while recognising that colloquial usage may diverge.

Three Functions of Verbs

✓ The Three Core Functions
  • Action (dynamic): The verb describes something a subject does — “She runs every morning.”
  • State (stative): The verb describes a condition or state — “He knows the answer.”
  • Occurrence: The verb describes an event that happens — “The accident occurred at dawn.”

Quick Identification Test

The simplest test for a verb is the inflection test: can the word change form to show tense? Walk → walked, walks, is walking. If it inflects for tense, it is (or can be) a verb. A secondary test is the negation test: can not follow it directly? “She does not walk” confirms walk as a verb.

✓ Topper’s Trick — The “Yesterday” Test

Insert yesterday before the predicate. If the verb changes form, it is a true (finite) verb.

  • “She runs every day.” → “Yesterday she ran.” — ✓ verb confirmed
  • “Her running is beautiful.” → “Yesterday her running is beautiful.” — does not change → running here is a gerund (noun), not a finite verb.
⚠ Trap 1 — Confusing Verbals with Verbs

Words derived from verbs—infinitives, gerunds, and participles—are called verbals. They look like verbs but function as nouns, adjectives, or adverbs. They are not finite verbs and cannot serve as the main predicate alone.

  • ✗ “Running in the park good exercise.” No finite verb; running here is a gerund, not a predicate verb.
  • ✓ “Running in the park is good exercise.”
  • “The children played in the garden.” Action verb — past tense, finite
  • “The soup smells delicious.” Linking verb — stative, present tense
  • “An earthquake occurred last night.” Occurrence verb — intransitive, past tense
  • “She has been waiting for an hour.” Verb phrase (auxiliary + present participle) — present perfect continuous

Types of Verbs

Action, linking, and auxiliary — three families with distinct grammatical behaviour

English verbs fall into three broad classes based on their grammatical function: action verbs (also called lexical or main verbs), linking verbs (also called copulas), and auxiliary verbs (also called helping verbs). Every verb phrase must contain a main verb; auxiliaries and linking verbs modify or complete its meaning.

Action Verbs

An action verb describes something the subject physically or mentally does. Action verbs can be transitive (taking a direct object) or intransitive (not taking a direct object), a distinction explored in Chapter 3.

Linking Verbs (Copulas)

A linking verb connects the subject to a subject complement—a predicate noun or predicate adjective that renames or describes the subject. The primary linking verb in English is be (is, am, are, was, were, being, been). Secondary linking verbs include verbs of sense (seem, appear, feel, smell, taste, sound, look) and verbs of becoming (become, grow, turn, remain, stay, prove).

✓ The Substitution Test for Linking Verbs

Replace the suspected linking verb with a form of be. If the sentence retains its meaning, the verb is a linking verb.

  • “The milk smells sour.” → “The milk is sour.” — ✓ linking verb
  • “She smells the flowers.” → “She is the flowers.” — ✗ nonsensical → action verb
⚠ Trap 2 — Adjective vs. Adverb after a Linking Verb

A linking verb must be followed by a predicate adjective, not an adverb, because the complement modifies the subject, not the verb.

  • ✗ “The roses smell sweetly.” Sweetly is an adverb; it cannot be a subject complement. The subject does not perform an action sweetly.
  • ✓ “The roses smell sweet.”
  • ✗ “He looks tiredly.” After a linking verb, the complement describes the subject; use the adjective tired.
  • ✓ “He looks tired.”

Transitive & Intransitive Verbs

Whether the verb’s action passes to an object or stays with the subject

A transitive verb (from Latin transire, to pass across) transfers its action to a direct object—a noun or noun phrase that receives the action. An intransitive verb does not transfer its action to an object; the predicate is complete without one. Many verbs can be used both ways depending on context.

The distinction matters because only transitive verbs can form passive voice constructions. If a verb is intransitive, it has no object to promote to subject position in the passive, and so passive transformation is impossible.

✓ The “What / Whom” Test for Transitivity

After the verb, ask “What?” or “Whom?” If there is a sensible answer that is part of the sentence, the verb is transitive.

  • “She wrote a letter.” → Wrote what? A letter. — ✓ transitive
  • “She wrote carefully.” → Wrote what? (No answer from the sentence.) — intransitive

Ditransitive Verbs

A special category of transitive verb is the ditransitive verb, which takes two objects: an indirect object (the recipient) and a direct object (the thing given). Common ditransitive verbs include give, send, show, tell, teach, lend, offer, bring.

Subject → Ditransitive Verb → Indirect Object → Direct Object
  • “She gave [him — IO] [the book — DO].”
  • “They offered [the refugees — IO] [shelter — DO].”
⚠ Trap 3 — Verbs That Change Meaning When Transitive vs. Intransitive

Several verbs have different meanings depending on whether they are used transitively or intransitively. Do not assume the same definition in both uses.

VerbTransitive UseIntransitive Use
run“She runs a company.” (manages)“She runs every day.” (exercises)
grow“He grows roses.” (cultivates)“The child grows fast.” (becomes bigger)
move“She moved the chair.” (displaced it)“She moved quickly.” (went)
smell“The dog smelled the bone.” (sniffed)“The bone smells.” (has a smell)
⚠ Trap 4 — Passivising Intransitive Verbs

Since intransitive verbs have no object, they cannot be made passive. This is one of the most common examination errors.

  • ✗ “The accident was occurred by careless driving.” Occur is intransitive; it has no object and cannot be passivised.
  • ✓ “The accident occurred because of careless driving.”
  • ✗ “A sneeze was happened suddenly.” Happen is intransitive; passive is impossible.
  • ✓ “A sneeze happened suddenly.”

Auxiliary Verbs

Primary and modal helpers that build tense, aspect, voice, and mood

An auxiliary verb (helping verb) precedes a main verb and helps express grammatical distinctions of tense, aspect, voice, mood, and modality. English has two sets: primary auxiliaries (be, have, do) and modal auxiliaries (can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must, ought to).

Primary Auxiliaries: Be, Have, Do

AuxiliaryMain UseExample
beContinuous & passive constructions“She is writing a report.” / “It was built in 1900.”
havePerfect aspect“They have arrived.” / “He had left before noon.”
doQuestions, negation, emphasis“Do you agree?” / “I do understand.”

Modal Auxiliaries

Modal auxiliaries express the speaker’s attitude toward an action—its possibility, necessity, permission, obligation, or ability. Crucially, modals do not inflect: they have no -s third-person singular form and no infinitive or participle form.

ModalCore MeaningExample
can / couldAbility; possibility“She can speak French.” / “It could rain.”
will / wouldFuture; conditional“He will arrive soon.” / “I would help if I could.”
shall / shouldFuture (formal); obligation“You shall not pass.” / “We should apologise.”
may / mightPermission; weak possibility“You may leave.” / “It might be true.”
mustStrong necessity; deduction“You must submit by Friday.” / “She must be tired.”
ought toMoral obligation (weaker than must)“You ought to apologise.”
✓ Four Key Properties of Modal Auxiliaries
  • No inflection: Never add -s, -ing, or -ed. There is no *cans, *willing, or *musted.
  • Bare infinitive follows: After a modal, the main verb takes the base (bare infinitive) form. “She can go” — not *“can to go” or *“can goes”.”
  • Inversion in questions: The modal moves before the subject: “Can she go?”
  • Negation with not: “She cannot (can’t) go.” Note: cannot is one word.
⚠ Trap 5 — Double Modals and Modal + To-Infinitive

In standard written English, two modal auxiliaries cannot be stacked, and most modals do not take to before the following verb.

  • ✗ “She might could come.” Double modal — dialectal; not standard written English.
  • ✗ “He must to leave now.” Must takes bare infinitive: *must to leave is non-standard.
  • ✓ “She might be able to come.” — use a periphrastic construction instead.
  • ✓ “He must leave now.”
✓ Ought to vs. Should

Ought to implies a moral duty or external expectation; should expresses the speaker’s personal recommendation. In formal contexts they are nearly interchangeable, but ought to carries a slightly stronger sense of objective obligation.

  • “You ought to respect your elders.” (moral/social norm)
  • “You should try the new restaurant.” (personal advice)

The Five Principal Parts

Every English verb is built from five foundational forms

The five principal parts of an English verb are the base form, the simple past, the past participle, the present participle, and the third-person singular present. All other verb forms are derived from these five. Mastering them is essential for correct tense formation.

✓ Regular vs. Irregular Verbs
  • Regular verbs form their simple past and past participle by adding -ed (or just -d if the base ends in -e): walk → walked; love → loved.
  • Irregular verbs change their vowel, their ending, or both in unpredictable ways: sing → sang → sung. These must be memorised.
  • Flat verbs are a subset of irregulars whose three forms are identical: cut → cut → cut; hit → hit → hit.
⚠ Trap 6 — Confusing Simple Past with Past Participle

The simple past is used alone (no auxiliary); the past participle requires an auxiliary. Mixing the two is a serious grammatical error.

  • ✗ “She has went to the market.” Went is simple past; perfect needs past participle gone.
  • ✓ “She has gone to the market.”
  • ✗ “He done his homework.” Done is past participle; without auxiliary, use simple past did.
  • ✓ “He did his homework.”

Overview of the 12 Tenses

Tense, time, and aspect — understanding the full system before diving into each family

English uses a system of 12 tenses built from the intersection of three times (past, present, future) and four aspects (simple, perfect, continuous, perfect continuous). Tense is a grammatical category that locates an event in time. Aspect describes whether the event is ongoing, completed, or ongoing-and-completed relative to a reference point.

A key distinction at topper level is between tense and time. Tense is a grammatical form; time is a semantic concept. The present tense does not always refer to present time (e.g., “He leaves tomorrow” uses present tense to express future time), and the past tense can express hypothetical situations in conditionals (e.g., “If I had a million dollars”).

AspectPastPresentFuture
Simple She walked She walks She will walk
Perfect She had walked She has walked She will have walked
Continuous She was walking She is walking She will be walking
Perfect Continuous She had been walking She has been walking She will have been walking
✓ What Each Aspect Signals
  • Simple: States a fact, habit, or completed event without emphasising duration or completion relative to another event.
  • Perfect: Connects a past event to a reference point (usually the present or another past event); emphasises completion or relevance.
  • Continuous: Emphasises the ongoing, in-progress nature of an action at a specific time.
  • Perfect Continuous: Combines both—an action that was ongoing up to and possibly including a reference point, emphasising both duration and connection to that point.
✓ The Auxiliary Stack

Every tense beyond the simple present and simple past is built by layering auxiliaries before the main verb in a predictable order: modal → have → be → main verb. Only the first element in the stack inflects.

(Modal) → (have) → (be) → Main Verb
  • “She will have been writing” = modal (will) + perfect (have) + continuous (be) + main (write)
⚠ Trap 7 — Tense is Not the Same as Time

Do not assume the grammatical tense of a verb always matches the real-world time of the event. Several common constructions break the tense=time rule.

  • “The train leaves at 9 a.m. tomorrow.” Present tense — future time (scheduled event)
  • “If I were you, I would apologise.” Past tense (were) — hypothetical present meaning
  • “Napoleon marches on Moscow.” Present tense — past time (historical present for dramatic effect)

Simple Tenses

Present, past, and future simple — facts, habits, completed events, and scheduled actions

The simple tenses present actions or states as whole, undivided events—without implying any specific duration or connection to another time. They are the most frequently used tenses in English and form the foundation for all other aspects.

Simple Present

The simple present is formed with the base form of the verb (adding -s or -es for third-person singular). It expresses: habitual actions, general truths, states, scheduled future events, and narration in the “historic present.”

✓ Uses of the Simple Present
  • Habits/routines: “She drinks coffee every morning.”
  • General/scientific truths: “Water boils at 100 °C.”
  • Stative conditions: “He knows the answer.”
  • Scheduled future: “The match starts at 7 p.m.”
  • Instructions/directions: “You take the second left, then turn right.”
⚠ Trap 8 — Omitting the -s in Third Person Singular

In formal writing, the third-person singular present always requires -s (or -es). Dropping it is a fundamental error.

  • ✗ “He write very clearly.” Third-person singular requires the inflection -s.
  • ✓ “He writes very clearly.”

Simple Past

The simple past locates a completed action at a definite point in the past. It is formed with the second principal part (e.g., walked, wrote, went). Key signals include time expressions such as yesterday, last year, in 2005, ago, when.

⚠ Trap 9 — Using Present Perfect When Simple Past Is Required

If a specific past time is mentioned, use the simple past, not the present perfect. The present perfect is incompatible with definite past-time adverbials.

  • ✗ “I have met her yesterday.” Yesterday is a specific past time; present perfect requires no specific time reference.
  • ✓ “I met her yesterday.”

Simple Future

The simple future is most commonly formed with will + base form. It expresses: predictions, spontaneous decisions, promises, and offers. For planned or arranged future events, English prefers be going to or the present continuous.

FormBest Used ForExample
will + basePredictions, promises, spontaneous decisions, offers“I will call you tonight.”
be going to + baseIntentions already decided; evidence-based predictions“Look at those clouds — it is going to rain.”
Pres. continuousFixed arrangements (diary future)“We are meeting the director tomorrow.”
Pres. simpleTimetabled / scheduled events“The flight departs at 6 a.m.”

Perfect Tenses

Connecting past events to a reference point — present perfect, past perfect, and future perfect

The perfect aspect is formed with have / had / will have + past participle. It signals that an event is relevant to, completed before, or connected to a specific reference point in time. The three perfect tenses are present perfect, past perfect (pluperfect), and future perfect.

Present Perfect

The present perfect (have/has + past participle) links a past event to the present. It is used when the past action has current relevance, when the exact time is unknown or unimportant, or to describe experience up to now.

✓ Four Core Uses of the Present Perfect
  • Experience (ever/never): “Have you ever visited Japan?”
  • Unfinished past (for/since): “She has lived here for ten years / since 2014.”
  • Recent past with just: “He has just left.”
  • Result in the present (already/yet): “The bus has already gone.”
⚠ Trap 10 — Since vs. For with Perfect Tenses

Since introduces a point in time (a specific moment); for introduces a duration (a period of time). Confusing them is a very common error.

  • ✗ “I have known her since three years.” Three years is a duration, not a point in time.
  • ✓ “I have known her for three years.”
  • ✗ “She has been here for Monday.” Monday is a point in time, not a duration.
  • ✓ “She has been here since Monday.”

Past Perfect (Pluperfect)

The past perfect (had + past participle) is used when two past events are narrated and you need to show that one occurred before the other. The earlier event takes the past perfect; the later event takes the simple past.

Earlier event → had + past participle → Later event → simple past
  • “When she arrived, the meeting had already started.” Meeting started first (past perfect); then she arrived (simple past)
  • “He had never seen snow before he moved to Norway.”
⚠ Trap 11 — Overusing Past Perfect

The past perfect is only necessary when there is a risk of ambiguity about which past event came first. When before or after makes the sequence clear, the simple past is acceptable for both events.

  • ✓ “She finished her work before she left.” Before signals the order clearly; past perfect not obligatory here.
  • ✓ (Also acceptable) “She had finished her work before she left.”

Future Perfect

The future perfect (will have + past participle) expresses an action that will be completed before a specified future time.

  • “By the time you arrive, I will have finished cooking.” Completion (finish) is projected before the future moment (your arrival).
  • “By 2030, scientists will have discovered new treatments.”

Continuous (Progressive) Tenses

Emphasising ongoing action at a specific moment in past, present, or future

The continuous (progressive) aspect is formed with the appropriate form of be + the present participle (-ing form). It signals that an action is in progress at a specific moment, is temporary, or contrasts with habitual/permanent states. Continuous tenses cannot be used with stative verbs in most contexts (see Chapter 17).

Present Continuous

The present continuous (am/is/are + -ing) expresses actions happening now, temporary situations, changing situations, and arranged future events.

UseSignal WordsExample
Action at this momentnow, at the moment, currently“She is writing an email right now.”
Temporary situationthis week/month, for the time being“He is staying with his aunt this month.”
Changing/developing situationgradually, increasingly“Temperatures are rising globally.”
Future arrangementtomorrow, next week, on Monday“We are flying to Rome next Friday.”
⚠ Trap 12 — Continuous with Stative Verbs

Stative verbs describe states, not actions, and are generally incompatible with the continuous aspect. This is one of the most tested traps in grammar examinations.

  • ✗ “I am knowing the answer.” Know is stative; it describes a state, not an ongoing action.
  • ✓ “I know the answer.”
  • ✗ “She is owning a house.” Own is stative (possession); use simple present.
  • ✓ “She owns a house.”

Past Continuous

The past continuous (was/were + -ing) expresses an action that was in progress at a specific moment in the past or that was interrupted by another past event (expressed in the simple past).

✓ The Classic Interrupted-Action Pattern
  • Longer background action: past continuous — “She was reading a book…”
  • Shorter interrupting action: simple past — “…when the phone rang.”
  • Two parallel ongoing actions: “While he was cooking, she was setting the table.”

Future Continuous

The future continuous (will be + -ing) indicates an action that will be in progress at a specific future moment, or a politely enquiring use that avoids imposing.

  • “At this time tomorrow, I will be sitting on the beach.” In progress at a future moment
  • “Will you be using the car tonight?” Polite enquiry (not demanding an explanation)

Perfect Continuous Tenses

Duration, ongoing activity, and connection to a reference point — the most nuanced aspect

The perfect continuous aspect combines perfect (have/had/will have) with continuous (be + -ing) to form have/had/will have + been + -ing. It emphasises both the duration of an activity and its connection to a reference point. It often implies that the activity has just stopped or is still ongoing, and explains a present or past result.

Present Perfect Continuous

The present perfect continuous (have/has been + -ing) stresses duration of an activity up to and including now, often explaining a visible present result.

✓ Present Perfect vs. Present Perfect Continuous
  • How many / how much? → Use present perfect (result, quantity): “She has written three chapters.”
  • How long? → Use present perfect continuous (duration): “She has been writing for three hours.”
  • “Your eyes are red. Have you been crying?” Activity explains a visible present result; duration implied
  • “I have been waiting for you for forty minutes!” Emphasis on duration from past to now, implying frustration

Past Perfect Continuous

The past perfect continuous (had been + -ing) describes an action that was in progress for a period before a past reference point. It is the past-time equivalent of the present perfect continuous.

  • “She was exhausted because she had been running for two hours.” Running was ongoing before the past state of exhaustion
  • “He had been working at the firm for a decade before he retired.”
⚠ Trap 13 — Perfect Continuous with Stative Verbs

Just as the simple continuous is incompatible with stative verbs, so is the perfect continuous. Even topper students make this error with “know,” “believe,” “want,” and similar verbs.

  • ✗ “I have been knowing her for years.” Know is stative; use present perfect simple.
  • ✓ “I have known her for years.”
  • ✗ “She has been believing in ghosts since childhood.” Believe is stative; use present perfect simple.
  • ✓ “She has believed in ghosts since childhood.”

Future Perfect Continuous

The future perfect continuous (will have been + -ing) describes an action that will have been in progress for a specified duration by a future point in time.

  • “By December, they will have been building the bridge for three years.”
  • “When she graduates, she will have been studying medicine for six years.”
✓ Topper’s Tip — The Diagnostic Question

To choose between perfect simple and perfect continuous, ask two diagnostic questions:

  • Is the verb stative? → If yes, use perfect simple only.
  • Is emphasis on duration or a visible result of ongoing activity? → If yes and verb is dynamic, prefer perfect continuous.
  • Is emphasis on completion or a quantity? → If yes, use perfect simple.

Subject-Verb Agreement

The grammatical rule that the verb must match its subject in number and person

Subject-verb agreement (concord) is the grammatical principle that a finite verb must agree with its subject in number (singular or plural) and person (first, second, or third). In practice, this usually means adding or dropping -s/-es in the present tense for third-person singular subjects.

✓ Core Agreement Rules
  • Singular subject → singular verb: “The dog barks.”
  • Plural subject → plural verb: “The dogs bark.”
  • Compound subject with and → plural verb: “Tom and Jerry are here.”
  • Compound subject with or/nor → agree with nearest subject: “Neither the teacher nor the students were late.”

Tricky Subjects

Subject TypeAgreementExample

Collective noun (as individuals)Plural (BrE)“The team are arguing among themselves.”

None (formal)Singular“None of the evidence was conclusive.”

⚠ Trap 14 — Intervening Phrase Confusion

A prepositional or relative clause between the subject and verb does not change the agreement. Always identify the true grammatical subject.

  • ✗ “The quality of the reports are poor.” Subject is quality (singular), not reports.
  • ✓ “The quality of the reports is poor.”
  • ✗ “One of the boys were missing.” Subject is one (singular), not boys.
  • ✓ “One of the boys was missing.”
⚠ Trap 15 — Inverted Sentences

When the sentence is inverted (verb before subject) — as in questions or sentences beginning with There is/are — the verb must still agree with its grammatical subject, which follows it.

  • ✗ “There is three apples on the table.” Subject is apples (plural).
  • ✓ “There are three apples on the table.”
  • ✗ “Has the children arrived?” Subject is children (plural).
  • ✓ “Have the children arrived?”

Active & Passive Voice

Who does the action, and who receives it — syntactic transformation and stylistic choice

In the active voice, the grammatical subject is the agent (the doer of the action). In the passive voice, the grammatical subject is the patient (the receiver of the action), and the agent is either expressed in a by-phrase or omitted. The passive is formed with be (in appropriate tense) + past participle of a transitive verb.

Active: Subject (Agent) → Verb → Object (Patient)
Passive: Subject (Patient) → be + Past Participle → (by + Agent)

Passive in All Tenses

TenseActivePassive
Simple PresentShe writes the report.The report is written (by her).
Simple PastShe wrote the report.The report was written.
Simple FutureShe will write the report.The report will be written.
Present PerfectShe has written the report.The report has been written.
Past PerfectShe had written the report.The report had been written.
Present ContinuousShe is writing the report.The report is being written.
Past ContinuousShe was writing the report.The report was being written.
ModalShe must write the report.The report must be written.
✓ When to Use Passive Voice
  • Agent unknown or unimportant: “The window was broken.” (We do not know or care who broke it.)
  • Agent obvious from context: “The accused was sentenced to five years.” (Clearly by the court.)
  • Scientific / formal writing: To foreground the process rather than the researcher: “The samples were analysed.”
  • Diplomatic vagueness: “Mistakes were made.”
⚠ Trap 16 — Passive with Intransitive Verbs

Only transitive verbs can form passive constructions. Applying passive to an intransitive verb is a fundamental error.

  • ✗ “The accident was happened.” Happen is intransitive; passive transformation is impossible.
  • ✗ “The sun was risen.” Rise is intransitive; do not confuse with transitive raise.
  • ✓ “The flag was raised.” (Raise is transitive.)
✓ The Get-Passive

In informal English, get can replace be in the passive, often with a nuance of misfortune or unexpectedness:

  • “He got sacked last week.” (informal passive = “He was dismissed.”)
  • “The car got stolen.”

Avoid get-passives in formal or academic writing; use be-passives instead.

Mood — Indicative, Imperative & Subjunctive

Grammatical mood signals the speaker’s relationship to the reality of the proposition

Mood is a grammatical category that expresses the speaker’s attitude toward the factuality, possibility, desirability, or contingency of what is expressed. English has three moods: indicative (stating facts), imperative (giving commands), and subjunctive (expressing hypotheticals, wishes, suggestions, and demands).

Indicative Mood

The indicative mood is the default mood of English. It is used for statements, questions, and exclamations about real or assumed real situations. All the tenses studied so far (chapters 7–10) operate in the indicative mood.

Imperative Mood

The imperative mood is used to give commands, instructions, invitations, or warnings. It uses the base form of the verb without a stated subject (the understood subject is you).

  • “Close the door, please.”
  • “Do not enter without permission.”
  • “Let us begin.” First-person inclusive imperative using let

Subjunctive Mood

The subjunctive mood is used to express wishes, hypothetical conditions, demands, suggestions, and requirements. It is more prominent in formal and written English than in casual speech. There are two forms: the present subjunctive (base form) and the past subjunctive (were for all persons).

✓ When to Use the Subjunctive
  • After verbs of demand/suggestion/recommendation: insist, demand, suggest, recommend, require, propose, urge, ask + that + subject + base form (not -s form).
  • After certain adjectives: essential, important, necessary, vital, imperative + that + subject + base form.
  • In hypothetical/counterfactual conditionals: if + were (for all persons).
  • Set expressions: be that as it may; far be it from me; God save the Queen; come what may.
ContextIndicative (Incorrect Here)Subjunctive (Correct)
Demand verb + that-clauseThe judge insisted that she pays. ✗The judge insisted that she pay. ✓

⚠ Trap 17 — Was vs. Were in Hypotheticals

In formal prescriptive English, the past subjunctive were is used for all persons in counterfactual conditions and wishes, including first and third person singular. Using was here is colloquial and non-standard in formal writing.

  • ✗ “If he was here, he would help.” Hypothetical; formal prescriptive standard requires were.
  • ✓ “If he were here, he would help.”
14

Verbals — Infinitives, Gerunds & Participles

Verb forms that function as nouns, adjectives, and adverbs — not as finite verbs

A verbal is a verb form that does not function as the main predicate of a clause. The three verbals are the infinitive (to + base or bare base), the gerund (-ing form used as a noun), and the participle (present -ing or past -ed/-en form used as an adjective or in an absolute phrase). Verbals are non-finite—they do not inflect for person, number, or tense on their own.

Infinitives

The infinitive appears either as the to-infinitive (to run, to know) or the bare infinitive (run, know). It can function as a noun (subject or object), an adjective, or an adverb (showing purpose).

FunctionExampleNotes
Subject“To err is human.”Formal; gerund more common in speech
Object“She wants to leave.”After want, hope, plan, decide
Adjective“She needs something to read.”Modifies indefinite pronoun
Adverb (purpose)“He ran to catch the bus.”Can be replaced by in order to
⚠ Trap 18 — The Split Infinitive Controversy

A split infinitive places an adverb between to and the verb (“to boldly go”). Prescriptively, many style guides advise against it; descriptively, it is standard in most contexts. In examination writing, avoid splitting infinitives unless not splitting would create ambiguity or awkwardness.

  • “She needs to carefully review the document.” Split infinitive — avoid in formal/exam writing.
  • ✓ “She needs to review the document carefully.”

Gerunds

A gerund is the -ing form of a verb functioning as a noun. It can serve as a subject, object, or object of a preposition. The grammatical test: if the -ing word can be replaced by it or a noun phrase without loss of meaning, it is a gerund.

✓ Gerund vs. Infinitive after Verbs
  • Gerund only after: enjoy, avoid, admit, deny, finish, consider, mind, suggest, postpone, practise — “She enjoys swimming.”
  • Infinitive only after: want, hope, decide, refuse, manage, offer, plan, expect, agree — “She decided to leave.”
  • Both (same meaning): begin, start, continue, like, love, hate, prefer
  • Both (different meaning): remember, forget, stop, regret, try, mean
⚠ Trap 19 — Remember/Forget + Gerund vs. + Infinitive

This is one of the most tested distinctions in grammar examinations. The meaning changes completely depending on whether a gerund or infinitive follows.

  • “I remember locking the door.” Gerund → memory of a past action already done
  • “Remember to lock the door.” Infinitive → reminder to perform a future action
  • “She forgot posting the letter.” Gerund → she posted it but then forgot she had done so
  • “She forgot to post the letter.” Infinitive → she did not post it because she forgot

Participles

A participle is a verb form used as an adjective or in an absolute phrase. The present participle (-ing) suggests ongoing or active quality; the past participle (-ed/-en) suggests completed or passive quality.

⚠ Trap 20 — Dangling Participles

A dangling participle occurs when the participial phrase’s implied subject does not match the subject of the main clause. This creates an absurd or unintended meaning and is a classic high-mark examination trap.

  • ✗ “Walking down the street, the trees looked beautiful.” The participle walking implies someone walks; logically the trees are doing the walking.
  • ✓ “Walking down the street, she found the trees beautiful.”
  • ✗ “Having finished the exam, the room fell silent.” The room did not finish the exam; the students did.
  • ✓ “Having finished the exam, the students fell silent.”

Irregular Verbs

Patterns, groupings, and the most commonly confused principal parts

An irregular verb does not form its simple past and past participle by adding -ed to the base form. Instead, it changes its vowel (ablaut), changes its ending, or remains unchanged. There are approximately 200 irregular verbs in common use; recognising their patterns reduces the burden of memorisation.

⚠ Trap 21 — Lie vs. Lay — The Most Confusing Pair

Lie (to recline) is intransitive; lay (to place) is transitive. Their past forms overlap, causing widespread confusion even among native speakers.

⚠ Trap 22 — Hanged vs. Hung

Hanged is used specifically for execution by hanging (a legal or historical context); hung is used for all other meanings.

  • ✓ “The criminal was hanged at dawn.”
  • ✓ “She hung the painting on the wall.”
  • ✗ “The painting was hanged on the wall.” Use hung for objects.

Causative Verbs

Make, have, let, get — verbs that express causing someone else to do something

A causative verb expresses that the subject causes or enables another person (or thing) to perform an action. The four primary causative verbs in English are make, have, let, and get. Each has a distinctive grammatical pattern governing whether the following verb takes a bare infinitive, a to-infinitive, or a past participle.

CausativePatternMeaningExample
makemake + object + bare infinitiveForce; compel (often against will)“The teacher made them rewrite the essay.”
havehave + object + bare infinitive (active) OR past participle (passive)Arrange; delegate; experience“She had the mechanic fix her car.” / “She had her car fixed.”
letlet + object + bare infinitiveAllow; permit“He let the children stay up late.”
getget + object + to-infinitive (active) OR past participle (passive)Persuade; manage to arrange“She got him to apologise.” / “She got her car serviced.”
✓ Active vs. Passive Causative
  • Active causative (a person performs the action): have/get + person + bare/to-infinitive — “I had the plumber fix the leak.”
  • Passive causative (a thing receives the action): have/get + thing + past participle — “I had the leak fixed.”
⚠ Trap 23 — Make in Passive Takes To-Infinitive

In the active voice, make takes a bare infinitive. But in the passive voice, make must be followed by a to-infinitive. This switch catches even advanced learners.

  • Active: “They made her sign the contract.” (bare infinitive)
  • ✗ Passive: “She was made sign the contract.” Bare infinitive cannot follow passive make.
  • ✓ Passive: “She was made to sign the contract.” (to-infinitive)
✓ Nuance: Make vs. Let vs. Get

These three causatives carry distinct nuances of coercion, permission, and persuasion:

  • Make: compulsion — the subject forces the object; the object may be unwilling.
  • Let: permission — the subject allows; there is no compulsion.
  • Get: persuasion — the subject convinces or manoeuvres the object through effort or negotiation.

Stative vs. Dynamic Verbs

Understanding which verbs resist the continuous aspect — and when even stative verbs can go continuous

A stative verb (also called a state verb) describes a condition, mental state, perception, or relationship that is relatively permanent and does not involve deliberate, observable activity. A dynamic verb (action verb) describes an event, process, or activity that is bounded in time and can be observed. This distinction governs whether a verb can appear in the continuous aspect.

✓ The Dynamic Use of Stative Verbs

Several stative verbs can shift to dynamic use, acquiring a different, more deliberate meaning. This is a topper-level distinction.

  • Think (stative = hold an opinion): “I think you are right.”
  • Think (dynamic = actively considering): “I am thinking about your proposal.”
  • Have (stative = possession): “She has a car.”
  • Have (dynamic = experience/enjoy): “She is having a great time.”
  • See (stative = perceive): “I see the point.”
  • See (dynamic = meet/visit): “I am seeing the doctor tomorrow.”

⚠ Trap 24 — Overextending the Stative Rule

Some students, having learned that stative verbs avoid the continuous, overcorrect and use simple forms for verbs that are legitimately dynamic in context. Always ask: is the verb expressing a genuine ongoing activity (dynamic) or a state?

  • ✗ “She eats her lunch right now.” Eat is dynamic; at this precise moment, continuous is correct.
  • ✓ “She is eating her lunch right now.”

Common Errors & Traps

The definitive checklist of verb errors — from foundation mistakes to topper-level pitfalls

This chapter consolidates the most frequently examined verb errors in English grammar. Each error is categorised by difficulty level. Eliminating these errors systematically is the single most effective way to raise marks in grammar sections of competitive examinations.

Foundation Level: The Most Common Errors

Error TypeWrong ✗Correct ✓Principle
Missing -s (3rd sg.) He go to school. He goes to school. 3rd person singular present requires -s/-es
Wrong tense with yesterday I have seen him yesterday. I saw him yesterday. Definite past time → simple past, not present perfect
Simple past used for past participle She has went home. She has gone home. Present perfect = have/has + past participle
Passive of intransitive verb The event was occurred. The event occurred. Intransitive verbs cannot be passivised
Stative verb in continuous I am knowing the answer. I know the answer. Stative verbs take simple tense, not continuous

Intermediate Level: Subtler Mistakes

Error TypeWrong ✗Correct ✓Principle
Since / for confusion I have lived here since five years. I have lived here for five years. Since = point in time; for = duration
Modal + to-infinitive She must to attend. She must attend. Modals take bare infinitive
Double modal He might could help. He might be able to help. No double modals in standard English
Subject-verb agreement with collective noun The committee are decided. The committee has decided. Collective noun acting as unit = singular verb
Intervening phrase confusion The box of chocolates are on the table. The box of chocolates is on the table. Subject is box (singular), not chocolates
Remember + wrong form Remember to lock the door = remembered locking Remember to lock (future duty) / remember locking (past action) Gerund = past; infinitive = future obligation

Topper Level: Advanced Traps

Error TypeWrong ✗Correct ✓Principle
Subjunctive omitted in demand clause She insisted that he was present. She insisted that he be present. Demand verbs + that-clause require present subjunctive (base form)
Dangling participle Walking home, the rain started. Walking home, she was caught in the rain. Participial phrase subject must match main clause subject
Causative make (passive) She was made sign the form. She was made to sign the form. Passive make requires to-infinitive
Lie vs. lay She laid down to rest. She lay down to rest. Lie (recline) past = lay; lay (place) past = laid
Perfect continuous + stative I have been knowing her for years. I have known her for years. Stative verbs cannot take perfect continuous aspect
Tense ≠ time confusion Using simple past for hypothetical: “If I was you.” If I were you… (past subjunctive, all persons) Hypothetical conditionals use past subjunctive were

The Ultimate Verb Self-Check List

✓ Before You Submit — Run This Checklist
  • ✓ Does every clause have a finite verb (not just a verbal)?
  • ✓ Does the third-person singular present have -s?
  • ✓ Have you used simple past (not present perfect) with definite past-time adverbials?
  • ✓ In perfect constructions, is the past participle (not simple past) used?
  • ✓ Are all stative verbs in the simple form (not continuous)?
  • ✓ Do modals take bare infinitives (no to, no -s)?
  • ✓ Does the verb agree with its true grammatical subject (not a nearby noun)?
  • ✓ Are demand / suggestion verbs followed by the subjunctive base form in that-clauses?
  • ✓ Is the passive only applied to transitive verbs?
  • ✓ Are participial phrases correctly anchored to the main clause subject (no dangling participles)?
  • ✓ Have you distinguished lie/lay, rise/raise, and hung/hanged?
  • ✓ In causative make (passive), is the to-infinitive used?
✓ Prescriptive vs. Descriptive — A Topper’s Perspective

Many “errors” in this guide are errors only by prescriptive standards—the rules that educated formal writing follows. Descriptively, native speakers routinely say “If I was you” and use the progressive with stative verbs for stylistic effect (“I’m loving it”). In examinations and formal essays, apply prescriptive rules rigorously. In creative writing and speech, descriptive use is acceptable. Know which standard the context demands.
