What Is an Adjective?

The foundational definition — function, form, and grammatical role

An adjective is a word that modifies or describes a noun or pronoun, providing additional information about its quality, quantity, size, colour, origin, or identity. Grammatically, adjectives belong to an open lexical class and function as noun phrase modifiers or subject/object complements within the predicate.

Adjectives answer one of three key questions about a noun: What kind? (descriptive), How many / How much? (quantitative), or Which one? (determinative). Every adjective in English can be tested against at least one of these questions.

✓ The Three Diagnostic Questions for Adjectives
  • What kind? → “a red apple” — describes a quality of the noun.
  • How many / how much? → “three apples,” “some water” — quantifies the noun.
  • Which one? → “this apple,” “my apple” — identifies or points to the noun.

Prescriptive vs. Descriptive Perspective

In the prescriptive tradition (the grammar of formal examinations and standard writing), adjectives are strictly classified as modifiers of nouns and pronouns. The descriptive tradition (contemporary linguistics) further recognises that many words can function as adjectives without belonging to the adjective word-class — for example, nouns functioning as noun-phrase modifiers (“a stone wall”). For examination purposes, the prescriptive view is the standard.

⚠ Adjective or Noun? The “Stone Wall” Trap

When a noun modifies another noun (e.g., “stone wall,” “school bus”), the modifying noun is called a noun adjunct or attributive noun — it is not classified as an adjective in prescriptive grammar. Do not call stone an adjective in “a stone wall.”

  • ✗ “Stone is an adjective in ‘a stone wall.’” Stone remains a noun functioning as a modifier; it fails the gradability test (you cannot say *a very stone wall).
  • ✓ “Stone is a noun adjunct in ‘a stone wall.’”
✓ Topper’s Gradability Test

A reliable way to confirm a true adjective is the gradability test: if the word accepts degree modifiers such as very, extremely, rather, it is almost certainly an adjective.

  • “very cold” → ✓ cold is an adjective.
  • “very stone” → ✗ stone is not an adjective.

Types of Adjectives — An Overview

A taxonomic map of all adjective classes in English

English adjectives are classified into several major types based on the kind of information they convey about a noun. Memorising this taxonomy is essential for examinations that test identification, labelling, and usage.

Type Function Examples
Descriptive Describes a quality or characteristic tall, beautiful, honest, cold
Quantitative Indicates amount or quantity some, much, little, enough
Numeral Indicates number (cardinal or ordinal) three, first, second, dozen
Demonstrative Points to a specific noun this, that, these, those
Possessive Shows ownership or relationship my, your, his, her, our, their
Interrogative Introduces questions about a noun which, what, whose
Distributive Refers to members of a group individually each, every, either, neither
Emphasising Adds force or emphasis very (own), sheer, utter, complete
Exclamatory Expresses strong feeling what (a wonderful day!)
✓ The Determiner Umbrella

Modern linguistics groups demonstrative, possessive, interrogative, and distributive adjectives under the single category of determiners — words that “determine” or delimit the reference of a noun phrase. In traditional grammar (and most Indian curriculum examinations), however, each is still treated as a separate type of adjective. Know both frameworks.

⚠ Numeral vs. Quantitative Confusion

Students frequently confuse numeral adjectives (which denote a precise number: two, ten, first) with quantitative adjectives (which denote an indefinite amount: much, some, little). The key difference is precision vs. vagueness.

  • ✗ “‘Some’ is a numeral adjective.” Some does not denote a specific, countable number; it is quantitative.
  • ✓ “‘Three’ is a cardinal numeral adjective; ‘some’ is a quantitative adjective.”

Descriptive (Qualitative) Adjectives

The largest class — qualities, states, and characteristics of nouns

A descriptive adjective (also called a qualitative adjective) is one that describes a quality, property, or state of a noun or pronoun. This is the largest open class of adjectives in English, encompassing physical properties, emotional states, colours, sizes, shapes, and textures.

Descriptive adjectives are gradable — they can occur in comparative and superlative forms, and they freely accept degree adverbs such as very, quite, rather, extremely, somewhat. This gradability is the hallmark that distinguishes them from determiners.

✓ Identifying Descriptive Adjectives — Four Formal Tests
  • Gradability: Can you say very [word]? → very tall ✓
  • Comparison: Does it form comparatives? → taller, more beautiful ✓
  • Attributive use: Does it precede a noun? → “a tall man” ✓
  • Predicative use: Can it follow a linking verb? → “The man is tall.” ✓

Proper Adjectives

A sub-type of descriptive adjective worth noting at the topper level is the proper adjective — an adjective derived from a proper noun. These are always capitalised. Examples: Indian cuisine, Shakespearean drama, Victorian architecture, Buddhist philosophy. They describe origin, style, or association with a proper name.

  • “She wore a silken scarf.” silken = descriptive adjective, material; modifies scarf (noun).
  • “The child was extremely curious.” curious = descriptive adjective in predicative position; extremely is a degree adverb.
  • “They served Italian coffee.” Italian = proper adjective derived from the proper noun Italy; capitalised.
⚠ Non-Gradable Adjectives Are Still Descriptive

Some descriptive adjectives are absolute or non-gradable by meaning — you cannot be “very dead,” “somewhat unique,” or “quite perfect.” These adjectives describe all-or-nothing states. Using degree adverbs with them is a formal error.

  • ✗ “Her essay was very unique.” Unique means ‘one of a kind’ — an absolute concept; degree modification is illogical.
  • ✓ “Her essay was truly unique.” Truly here functions as an affirmer, not a degree modifier.
  • ✗ “He is more dead than alive.” Acceptable only as a fixed informal expression; formally, dead is absolute.

Quantitative Adjectives

Expressing amount, degree, and indefinite quantity

A quantitative adjective indicates the quantity or amount of the noun it modifies, but does so without specifying an exact number. It answers the question “How much?” for uncountable nouns and “How many?” for countable nouns in a non-specific way. The grammatical principle at work is the count/non-count noun distinction.

Countable vs. Uncountable: The Core Rule

Adjective With Countable Nouns With Uncountable Nouns With Both
few / a few a few books ✓ a few water ✗ —
little / a little a little books ✗ a little water ✓ —
much much books ✗ much water ✓ —
many many books ✓ many water ✗ —
some / any some books ✓ some water ✓ ✓ Both
enough / all enough books ✓ enough water ✓ ✓ Both
✓ Few vs. A Few — A Crucial Semantic Distinction
  • Few (without article): implies a disappointingly small number — a negative connotation. “Few students passed.” (most did not)
  • A few (with article): implies some, though not many — a positive or neutral connotation. “A few students passed.” (at least some did)
  • Little (without article): a disappointingly small amount. “There is little hope.”
  • A little (with article): some, at least. “There is a little hope.”
⚠ “Less” vs. “Fewer” — One of the Most Tested Traps

Use fewer with countable nouns and less with uncountable nouns. This is one of the most commonly tested distinctions in formal grammar examinations.

  • ✗ “There are less people here today.” People is countable; fewer is required.
  • ✓ “There are fewer people here today.”
  • ✗ “She drinks fewer water.” Water is uncountable; less is required.
  • ✓ “She drinks less water.”

Demonstrative Adjectives

Pointing and identifying — proximity, number, and the determiner function

A demonstrative adjective is one of the four words — this, that, these, those — that points to a specific noun or pronoun, indicating its proximity to the speaker in space or time. The grammatical principle is deixis (Greek: “pointing”), which encodes both distance (near vs. far) and number (singular vs. plural).

Near + Singular → this    Near + Plural → these    Far + Singular → that    Far + Plural → those
✓ Demonstrative Adjective vs. Demonstrative Pronoun
  • When this / that / these / those precede a noun, they are adjectives: “This book is mine.”
  • When they stand alone (replacing a noun), they are pronouns: “This is mine.”
  • The test: if you can add a noun after the word without changing the sentence’s meaning significantly, it is functioning as an adjective.
  • “This mango is ripe.” this = demonstrative adjective; near + singular; modifies mango.
  • “Those children are playing.” those = demonstrative adjective; far + plural; modifies children.
  • “That was a mistake.” that = demonstrative pronoun here; it stands alone without a following noun.
⚠ Agreement Error — This/That with Plural Nouns

Demonstrative adjectives must agree in number with the noun they modify. This and that can only modify singular nouns; these and those must be used with plural nouns.

  • ✗ “These kind of errors are common.” Kind is singular; this kind is the correct form.
  • ✓ “This kind of error is common.”
  • ✗ “That books are interesting.” Books is plural; those is required.
  • ✓ “Those books are interesting.”

Possessive Adjectives

Ownership, relationship, and the pronoun–adjective boundary

A possessive adjective is a word that modifies a noun by indicating ownership, association, or relationship. In English, the possessive adjectives are: my, your, his, her, its, our, their, whose. They are grammatically derived from personal pronouns and always precede a noun (or a noun phrase).

Person Possessive Adjective Possessive Pronoun (for contrast)
1st Singularmymine
2nd Singular/Pluralyouryours
3rd Singular (M)hishis
3rd Singular (F)herhers
3rd Singular (N)itsits
1st Pluralourours
3rd Pluraltheirtheirs
✓ Possessive Adjective vs. Possessive Pronoun Test
  • Possessive adjective: always followed by a noun — “my book,” “her idea.”
  • Possessive pronoun: stands alone, replacing a noun — “The book is mine.”
  • Note: his and its have identical forms for both functions — only context distinguishes them.
⚠ Its vs. It’s — The Apostrophe Catastrophe

Its (no apostrophe) is a possessive adjective. It’s (with apostrophe) is a contraction of it is or it has. These are never interchangeable.

  • ✗ “The dog wagged it’s tail.” It’s here would mean ‘it is tail’ — grammatically absurd.
  • ✓ “The dog wagged its tail.”
  • ✗ “Its raining outside.” The possessive adjective its cannot stand alone without a noun; it’s (it is) is needed.
  • ✓ “It’s raining outside.”
⚠ Their / There / They’re — A Classic Homophones Trap

These three homophones are among the most common spelling errors at every level:

  • Their = possessive adjective: “It is their responsibility.”
  • There = adverb of place or existential subject: “There is a problem.”
  • They’re = contraction of they are: “They’re coming soon.”

Interrogative Adjectives

Asking questions through noun modification

An interrogative adjective is one of the words which, what, or whose when used before a noun to introduce a direct or indirect question. The grammatical function is to modify a noun within an interrogative clause rather than to stand alone as a pronoun.

✓ Which vs. What — Choice vs. Open Question
  • Which implies a limited, defined set of choices: “Which book do you prefer?” (among specified books)
  • What implies an open, undefined range: “What book are you reading?” (any book at all)
  • Whose asks about possession or authorship: “Whose pen is this?”
  • “Which route should we take?” which = interrogative adjective; modifies route; implies routes are already defined.
  • “What time does the train leave?” what = interrogative adjective; modifies time; open question.
  • “Whose coat is hanging here?” whose = interrogative adjective; modifies coat; asks about possession.
⚠ Interrogative Adjective vs. Interrogative Pronoun

The words which, what, and whose are adjectives when they are immediately followed by a noun, and pronouns when they stand alone.

  • ✓ “Which pen do you want?” which + noun (pen) = interrogative adjective.
  • ✓ “Which do you want?” which stands alone = interrogative pronoun.
✓ Interrogative Adjectives in Indirect Questions

Interrogative adjectives also appear in indirect (embedded) questions, where the word order does not invert. This is a topper-level usage detail.

  • “I don’t know which train he took.” Indirect question; which modifies train; normal subject-verb order is maintained.
  • “She asked whose car was parked outside.”

Distributive Adjectives

Referring to members of a group one by one

A distributive adjective is one of the words each, every, either, neither (and sometimes both) when used before a noun to refer to the members of a group individually or separately, rather than collectively. The grammatical principle is individuation: each member is considered one at a time.

✓ Subject–Verb Agreement: Distributive Adjectives Require Singular Verbs
  • Each, every, either, neither + noun always take a singular verb, because each member is treated individually.
  • “Every student is responsible.”
  • “Each of the answers was correct.”
  • “Neither option is satisfactory.”
Adjective Usage Example
each Two or more, individually “Each child received a gift.”
every Three or more, collectively as individuals “Every student must submit the form.”
either One of two (affirmative) “Either road leads to the city.”
neither Not one of two (negative) “Neither answer is correct.”
⚠ Each vs. Every — Not Always Interchangeable

Each can be used for two or more individuals and emphasises individuality. Every is only used for three or more and emphasises totality. Additionally, every cannot be used in certain positions where each can.

  • ✓ “Each of the two teams played well.”
  • ✗ “Every of the two teams played well.” Every cannot precede ‘of’ directly and is not used for groups of exactly two.
  • ✗ “The players each received medals.” — Wait: this is actually correct! Each can float as a floating quantifier in postverbal position, but every cannot: ✗ “The players every received medals.” is wrong.
⚠ Either / Neither with More Than Two Options

Formally, either and neither should only be used when referring to exactly two options. For three or more, use any and none respectively.

  • ✗ “Neither of the three candidates is qualified.” Formal grammar requires ‘none’ when three or more are involved.
  • ✓ “None of the three candidates is qualified.”

Articles as Adjectives

A, An, The — definiteness, phonological choice, and topper-level nuances

The articles a, an (indefinite) and the (definite) are traditionally classified as a sub-type of adjective in prescriptive grammar because they modify nouns by specifying their definiteness. Linguistically, they belong to the class of determiners. The choice of article encodes whether a noun refers to a specific, known entity (the) or an unspecified member of a class (a / an).

✓ A vs. An — The Sound Rule (Not the Spelling Rule)
  • Use a before a word beginning with a consonant sound.
  • Use an before a word beginning with a vowel sound.
  • The decision depends on pronunciation, not spelling — this is the most-tested principle of article usage.
Word Initial Sound Correct Article Wrong Version
honest Vowel sound /ɒ/ (silent h) an honest man ✓ a honest man ✗
university Consonant sound /juː/ (yoo) a university ✓ an university ✗
hour Vowel sound /aʊ/ (silent h) an hour ✓ a hour ✗
European Consonant sound /juː/ (yoo) a European ✓ an European ✗
MBA Vowel sound /ɛm/ (em) an MBA ✓ a MBA ✗
⚠ Omission of “The” — Generic vs. Specific Nouns

The definite article the is omitted before generic, uncountable, and abstract nouns used in their general sense. Inserting the changes the meaning from general to specific.

  • ✗ “The honesty is the best policy.” Honesty here is abstract and generic; no article is needed.
  • ✓ “Honesty is the best policy.”
  • ✓ “The honesty she displayed was remarkable.” Here, a specific instance of honesty is referred to; the is correct.
✓ Topper Note — “The” with Superlatives and Unique Entities

The definite article the is obligatory before superlative adjectives and before nouns that are one-of-a-kind entities (celestial bodies, geographic features when unique).

  • “She is the tallest student in the class.”
  • “The sun rises in the east.”
  • “He is the Prime Minister.”

Attributive vs. Predicative Position

Where an adjective sits in a sentence — and why it matters

Every adjective in a sentence occupies one of two syntactic positions: attributive (directly before the noun it modifies, within the noun phrase) or predicative (after a linking verb such as be, seem, appear, become, feel, look, smell, taste). Some adjectives are restricted to one position only.

Attributive: → [Article] + Adjective + Noun    Predicative: → Subject + Linking Verb + Adjective
Position Example Structure
Attributive “a sleepy child” Adjective directly before noun
Predicative “The child is sleepy.” Adjective after linking verb
Postpositive “something unusual” Adjective after indefinite pronoun
✓ Adjectives Restricted to Attributive Position Only
  • Utter, sheer, mere, chief, main, principal, sole, only: These intensifiers and emphasising adjectives can only appear before a noun.
  • “an utter fool” ✓   —   “The fool is utter.” ✗
  • “a mere child” ✓   —   “The child is mere.” ✗
✓ Adjectives Restricted to Predicative Position Only
  • Afraid, asleep, awake, alive, aware, ashamed, alike, aghast, afoot, ajar: The “a-prefix” adjectives almost exclusively appear after linking verbs.
  • “The child is asleep.” ✓   —   “an asleep child” ✗
  • “She felt afraid.” ✓   —   “the afraid woman” ✗
⚠ Postpositive Adjectives — After Indefinite Pronouns

When an adjective modifies an indefinite pronoun (something, anything, nothing, someone, anyone, nowhere, everywhere), the adjective must come after the pronoun, not before. This is the postpositive position.

  • ✗ “Is there new anything to discuss?” Adjectives cannot precede indefinite pronouns.
  • ✓ “Is there anything new to discuss?”
  • ✓ “I need something cold to drink.”

The Royal Order of Adjectives

Why “a lovely little old rectangular green French silver whittling knife” sounds right

When multiple adjectives modify a single noun, English speakers follow a largely instinctive but rule-governed sequence known as the Royal Order of Adjectives. Violations of this order produce sequences that are grammatically permissible but deeply unnatural — a key topper-level distinction. The principle underlying the order is that adjectives closest in meaning to the noun are placed nearest to it.

Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose → NOUN
Category Position Examples
Opinion / General1stlovely, horrible, beautiful, strange
Size2ndbig, small, tall, tiny, enormous
Age3rdyoung, old, ancient, medieval, new
Shape4thround, square, oval, flat, jagged
Colour5thred, blue, golden, dark, pale
Origin / Nationality6thFrench, Italian, Indian, Nordic
Material7thwooden, silk, glass, plastic, stone
Purpose / Qualifier8thcooking, sleeping, racing, writing
  • “She bought a lovely small antique oval black French silver writing desk.” Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose: all categories in canonical order.
  • ✗ “a French lovely black small desk” Origin before Opinion and Colour before Size violates the Royal Order; deeply unnatural in English.
  • ✓ “a lovely big old brown wooden wardrobe” Opinion → Size → Age → Colour → Material: correct sequence.
✓ Mnemonic: OSASCOMP

Remember the Royal Order with the acronym OSASCOMP: Opinion — Size — Age — Shape — Colour — Origin — Material — Purpose.

⚠ Comma Rules with Multiple Adjectives

When adjectives of the same category (co-ordinate adjectives) are used together, separate them with commas or “and.” When adjectives are from different categories (cumulative adjectives following the Royal Order), no comma is used.

  • ✓ “a kind, patient teacher” Two opinion adjectives (co-ordinate) → comma used.
  • ✓ “a tall old Indian man” Different categories (size, age, origin) → no comma.
  • ✗ “a tall, old, Indian man” Commas between cumulative adjectives of different categories are incorrect in formal usage.

Degrees of Comparison — Positive & Comparative

How adjectives scale quality — formation, rules, and exceptions

Gradable adjectives in English exist in three degrees of comparison: the positive (base form, no comparison), the comparative (comparing two entities), and the superlative (comparing three or more, identifying the extreme). The grammatical principle is scalar modification — placing a quality on a scale relative to other referents.

Formation of the Comparative Degree

Adjective Type Rule Positive → Comparative
One syllable Add -er tall → taller, fast → faster
One syllable ending in -e Add -r only fine → finer, wise → wiser
One syllable CVC (consonant-vowel-consonant) Double final consonant + -er hot → hotter, big → bigger, sad → sadder
Two syllables ending in -y Change y to i + -er happy → happier, lazy → lazier
Two+ syllables (most) Add more before careful → more careful, modern → more modern
✓ Using the Comparative Degree Correctly
  • The comparative must be followed by than when the second term of comparison is expressed: “She is taller than her brother.”
  • The second term must be grammatically parallel to the first: compare like with like.
  • Never use more with a form already ending in -er (the double comparative trap).
⚠ The Double Comparative — A Classic Examination Trap

Using both more and the -er suffix simultaneously is a double comparative and is always incorrect in formal English.

  • ✗ “She is more taller than him.” More and -er cannot both mark the comparative degree on the same adjective.
  • ✓ “She is taller than him.”
  • ✗ “This is more easier than I expected.” Same error: more + -er = double comparative.
  • ✓ “This is easier than I expected.”
⚠ Illogical Comparison — Comparing Incomparable Things

One of the most subtle traps: when comparing a noun to a group that includes itself, use any other (not any) to exclude the subject from the group.

  • ✗ “Mumbai is larger than any city in India.” Mumbai is itself a city in India; this illogically implies Mumbai is larger than itself.
  • ✓ “Mumbai is larger than any other city in India.”

The Superlative Degree

The highest point on the scale — formation, the definite article rule, and traps

The superlative degree of an adjective is used when comparing three or more entities, singling out the one that possesses a quality to the greatest or least extent. Structurally, the superlative is formed either by adding the suffix -est or by using most / least before the adjective. The definite article the almost always precedes the superlative.

Adjective Type Superlative Rule Example
One syllable Add -est tall → tallest, fast → fastest
One syllable CVC Double consonant + -est hot → hottest, big → biggest
Two syllables ending in -y Change y to i + -est happy → happiest, easy → easiest
Two+ syllables (most) Add most before careful → most careful
✓ Superlative Usage Rules
  • Always use the before a superlative adjective in attributive or predicative position: “She is the most talented student.”
  • Superlatives imply membership of a group: use in for places and organisations (“the best student in the class”) and of for time periods and sets (“the best day of the year”).
  • Never follow a superlative with any other: use any or all other.
  • “He is the most diligent student in the school.” Superlative; three or more compared; in + place/group.
  • ✗ “Sachin is greater than any other cricketer who has ever played.” If superlative meaning is intended (‘the greatest’), use the superlative form directly.
  • ✓ “Sachin is the greatest cricketer who has ever played.”
⚠ Double Superlative — Most + -est

Like the double comparative, using both most and the -est suffix is a formal error.

  • ✗ “She is the most kindest person I know.” Most and -est cannot both mark superlative on the same adjective.
  • ✓ “She is the kindest person I know.”
⚠ Superlative with Only Two Items

When only two entities are being compared, the comparative degree must be used, not the superlative. Using the superlative with two items is a classic grammar error.

  • ✗ “Of the two sisters, Priya is the most intelligent.” Only two people; superlative is incorrect.
  • ✓ “Of the two sisters, Priya is the more intelligent.”

Irregular Comparisons

Adjectives that defy the standard formation rules

A small but frequently tested group of adjectives form their comparative and superlative degrees through suppletion — the use of entirely different word roots rather than suffixes or more / most. These forms must be memorised as they cannot be predicted by any rule.

Positive Comparative Superlative Meaning
good better best quality
bad worse worst quality (negative)
little less least amount (uncountable)
much / many more most amount / number
far farther / further farthest / furthest distance / degree
old older / elder oldest / eldest age
late later / latter latest / last time / sequence
near nearer nearest / next proximity
⚠ Further vs. Farther — A Topper-Level Distinction

Farther / farthest refer strictly to physical, measurable distance. Further / furthest refer to figurative, metaphorical, or additional degree — and also to physical distance in British English.

  • ✓ “The station is farther than I thought.” (physical distance)
  • ✓ “We need further discussion on this matter.” (additional / metaphorical)
  • ✗ “We need farther discussion.” Discussion is not a physical distance; further is required.
⚠ Elder vs. Older — Another Key Distinction

Elder / eldest are used only for members of the same family and cannot be followed by than. Older / oldest are used for any comparison and can take than.

  • ✓ “He is my elder brother.” (family member; attributive)
  • ✗ “He is elder than me.” Elder cannot be followed by than; older than is required.
  • ✓ “He is older than me.”
  • ✓ “She is the eldest daughter in the family.”
⚠ Later vs. Latter — Time vs. Sequence

Later refers to time (after a point in time). Latter refers to the second of two previously mentioned items, and it does not function as a straightforward comparative.

  • ✓ “We will discuss this at a later date.” (time)
  • ✓ “He studied science and history; the latter was his favourite.” (second of two)
  • ✗ “The latter I called him, the better he felt.” Latter cannot be used to indicate time; later is required.

Adjectives vs. Adverbs

The most commonly confused distinction in English usage

Adjectives modify nouns and pronouns. Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, other adverbs, or whole clauses. The grammatical confusion arises because many adverbs are formed from adjectives by adding -ly — but some adjectives themselves end in -ly (friendly, lovely, lonely), and some adverbs have the same form as the corresponding adjective (fast, hard, late, early).

✓ The Linking Verb Test
  • After a linking verb (be, seem, appear, feel, look, smell, taste, sound, become, grow, remain), use an adjective (it describes the subject).
  • After an action verb, use an adverb (it describes the action).
  • Test: Can you replace the verb with is / are / was? → If yes, use adjective; if not, use adverb.
  • ✗ “She looks beautifully.” Looks here is a linking verb; beautifully (adverb) is incorrect — the word should describe the subject, not the verb.
  • ✓ “She looks beautiful.” Beautiful (adjective) correctly describes the subject She via the linking verb looks.
  • ✗ “He ran quick.” Ran is an action verb; an adverb (quickly) is required to modify it.
  • ✓ “He ran quickly.”
⚠ Flat Adverbs — Words That Are Both Adjective and Adverb

Certain words function as both adjective and adverb with no change in form. These are called flat adverbs. Mistakenly adding -ly to them creates an error.

  • fast: “a fast car” (adjective) / “She ran fast” (adverb — not *fastly)
  • hard: “a hard exam” (adjective) / “He worked hard” (adverb — not *hardly, which means ‘scarcely’)
  • late: “a late train” (adjective) / “She arrived late” (adverb — not *lately, which means ‘recently’)
⚠ Hardly / Lately / Highly — Not the Adverbs of Hard / Late / High

The adverbs formed by adding -ly to certain adjectives carry completely different meanings from the flat adverb forms:

  • hard (adverb: with effort) ≠ hardly (scarcely, barely)
  • late (adverb: not on time) ≠ lately (recently)
  • high (adverb: at a great height) ≠ highly (to a great degree; figuratively)
  • ✗ “He hardly works.” (means he barely works) vs. “He works hard.” (with effort)

Participial Adjectives — -ed & -ing Forms

Verbs that become adjectives — and the logic behind choosing the right form

Participial adjectives are adjective forms derived from verbs — either the present participle (-ing) or the past participle (-ed / irregular). The key grammatical principle is the direction of feeling: the -ing form describes what causes a feeling or state; the -ed form describes who or what experiences it.

-ing form → causes the feeling → “a boring lecture” → the lecture causes boredom
-ed form → experiences the feeling → “a bored student” → the student feels boredom
-ing (Cause) -ed (Experiencer) Example sentences
boringbored“a boring speech / a bored audience”
interestinginterested“an interesting topic / an interested student”
confusingconfused“a confusing question / a confused candidate”
excitingexcited“an exciting match / an excited crowd”
exhaustingexhausted“an exhausting journey / an exhausted traveller”
terrifyingterrified“a terrifying nightmare / a terrified child”
disappointingdisappointed“a disappointing result / a disappointed player”
⚠ The Most Tested Participial Adjective Error

Using the -ing form when the -ed form is required — or vice versa — is one of the most common errors in competitive examinations. Always ask: Is the noun the cause or the experiencer?

  • ✗ “I was very boring at the party.” This says you caused others to feel bored, not that you felt bored yourself.
  • ✓ “I was very bored at the party.” (you experienced boredom)
  • ✗ “The teacher found the students very interested.” If the students caused disinterest in the teacher, interesting is needed; if the students felt interest, interested is correct. Context determines the choice.
✓ Compound Adjectives Using Participles

Participial adjectives frequently appear in compound adjectives (hyphenated before nouns): well-known, thought-provoking, hard-working, good-looking, long-lasting, heart-breaking. Note: the hyphen is used in the attributive position only.

  • “a well-known scientist” (attributive → hyphen) ✓
  • “The scientist is well known.” (predicative → no hyphen) ✓

Common Errors & Traps — A Comprehensive Summary

Every major pitfall in one chapter — for revision and examination preparation

This final chapter consolidates the most frequently tested traps across all areas of adjective usage. Mastering these errors is the difference between a good score and a topper-level performance.

Category 1 — Degree and Comparison Errors

  • ✗ “She is more prettier than her sister.” Double comparative: more + -er simultaneously. Use prettier alone.
  • ✗ “Of the two options, A is the best.” Superlative used for only two items; the better is required.
  • ✗ “She is the most unique candidate.” Unique is an absolute adjective; it cannot be graded. Write simply: she is a unique candidate.
  • ✗ “Delhi is bigger than any city in India.” Illogical comparison: Delhi is in India. Use any other city in India.
  • ✗ “He is elder than his colleague.” Elder cannot be followed by than; use older than.

Category 2 — Count / Non-Count Errors

  • ✗ “There are less students today.” Students is countable; fewer is required.
  • ✗ “We don’t have many time.” Time (in this sense) is uncountable; much is required.
  • ✗ “She has a little books in her bag.” Books is countable; a few is required.
  • ✗ “He has few patience.” Patience is uncountable; little patience is required.

Category 3 — Agreement and Number Errors

  • ✗ “These kind of mistakes are avoidable.” Kind is singular; this kind of mistake is are avoidable is the correct form.
  • ✗ “Every students must attend.” Every is a distributive adjective; it takes a singular noun: every student.
  • ✗ “Neither of the three roads are safe.” Three options require none, not neither; also, neither takes a singular verb.

Category 4 — Participial Adjective Errors

  • ✗ “The movie was very bored.” A movie cannot experience boredom; it causes it. The movie was boring.
  • ✗ “I am very interesting in history.” The subject (I) experiences the interest; I am very interested in history.
  • ✗ “The situation is very worrying for those who are worrying.” First worrying (cause) is correct; second should be worried (experiencer).

Category 5 — Article Errors

  • ✗ “She is a honest person.” Honest begins with a vowel sound /ɒ/ (silent h); an honest is required.
  • ✗ “He is an university student.” University begins with consonant sound /juː/; a university is required.
  • ✗ “The gold is valuable.” Gold used generically as an uncountable noun does not take the; drop the article.
  • ✗ “She is best student in the class.” Superlative adjectives require the: she is the best student.

Category 6 — Position and Order Errors

  • ✗ “Is there new anything on the agenda?” Adjectives must follow indefinite pronouns: anything new.
  • ✗ “She feels afraid of the dark.” — This is actually correct; afraid is a predicative-only adjective and correctly follows the linking verb feels.
  • ✗ “an afraid child” Afraid cannot be used in attributive position; a frightened child is correct.
  • ✗ “a French lovely old table” Royal Order violated; the correct order is: a lovely old French table (opinion → age → origin).

Category 7 — Adjective vs. Adverb Confusion

  • ✗ “The soup smells wonderfully.” Smells is a linking verb; an adjective (wonderful) is required to describe the subject.
  • ✗ “She works hardly.” Hardly means scarcely; to express great effort, use the flat adverb hard: she works hard.
  • ✗ “He came lately.” Lately means recently; to express not on time, use the flat adverb late: he came late.
✓ Master Quick-Reference: The 10 Golden Rules of Adjectives
  • 1. Use fewer with countable nouns; less with uncountable nouns.
  • 2. Never use both more / most AND an -er / -est suffix together (double comparison).
  • 3. Use the comparative (not superlative) when comparing exactly two entities.
  • 4. Unique, perfect, dead, eternal, infinite are absolute adjectives — do not grade them.
  • 5. Use an before a vowel sound; a before a consonant sound (sound, not spelling).
  • 6. The -ed participial form describes the experiencer; the -ing form describes the cause.
  • 7. Adjectives must come after indefinite pronouns (anything new, nothing wrong).
  • 8. Follow the Royal Order (OSASCOMP) when stacking multiple adjectives.
  • 9. Elder / eldest are used only within families and cannot take than.
  • 10. Each, every, either, neither + noun always demand a singular verb.
