What Is a Pronoun?

Efficiency in speech and writing

A pronoun is a word used in place of a noun or a noun phrase. Its primary purpose is to avoid repetitive naming and to make language more fluid. The noun that a pronoun replaces is called its antecedent.

The Replacement Test

✓ Diagnostic Test

If you can replace a word with a specific noun and the sentence still makes sense (though it may become repetitive), that word is a pronoun.

  • "He won the race." → "Ravi won the race." (Functional shift confirmed)

Nominative vs. Objective

The "It is I" Rule and prepositional traps
✓ The Linking Verb Rule

In formal grammar, pronouns following a linking verb (is, am, are, was, were) must be in the Nominative Case.

  • ✓ "It is I."
  • ✗ "It is me." (Colloquial, but wrong in exams)
⚠ The Between Trap

Pronouns following prepositions (like between, for, with) must be in the Objective Case.

  • ✓ "Between you and me..."
  • ✗ "Between you and I..."
5

Possessive Case

Pronouns vs. Adjectives & the "Its" Trap

Possessive Pronouns (mine, yours, hers) replace a noun phrase. Possessive Adjectives (my, your, her) modify a noun. A topper knows never to use an apostrophe with possessive pronouns.

Possessive AdjectivePossessive Pronoun
This is my seat.This seat is mine.
Is this your pen?Is this pen yours?
That is their problem.The problem is theirs.
⚠ The "Its" vs. "It's" Diagnostic

The most common error in formal writing. If you can replace it with "It is," use the apostrophe. If it shows possession, DO NOT.

  • "The dog wagged its tail." (Possessive)
  • "It's (It is) a beautiful day." (Contraction)
6

Reflexive & Emphatic

The -self and -selves forms

Reflexive Pronouns (myself, himself) are used when the subject and object are the same. Emphatic Pronouns are used only to add emphasis to a noun or pronoun.

  • Reflexive: "He cut himself while shaving." Subject 'He' and Object 'himself' are the same.
  • Emphatic: "I myself saw him do it." Used for emphasis; can be removed without breaking the sentence.
★ Topper's Rule: Non-Reflexive Verbs

Verbs like keep, stop, bathe, turn, move, rest, hide do not usually take reflexive pronouns in standard English.

  • ✓ "He kept away from the fire."
  • ✗ "He kept himself away from the fire."
7

Demonstrative Pronouns

Pointing out: This, That, These, Those

These pronouns point to specific objects. This/These refer to things near the speaker; That/Those refer to things further away.

✓ Pronoun vs. Adjective Test

If the word is followed immediately by a noun, it is a Demonstrative Adjective. If it stands alone, it is a Demonstrative Pronoun.

  • "This pen is red." (Adjective)
  • "This is my pen." (Pronoun)
8

Indefinite Pronouns

Everyone, Someone, Nobody, All, Few

Indefinite pronouns do not refer to a specific person or thing. Most singular indefinite pronouns (everyone, anything, nobody) take a singular verb.

★ Topper's Rule: The "One" Consistency

If a sentence starts with the indefinite pronoun One, it must be followed by one's, not his/her.

  • ✓ "One should do one's duty."
  • ✗ "One should do his duty."

11

Distributive Pronouns

Each, Either, Neither

Distributive pronouns refer to persons or things one at a time. For this reason, they are always singular and must take a singular verb.

  • "Each of the students has a pen." Subject is 'Each', not 'students'.
  • "Neither of the two accusations is true."
⚠ Number Limits

Either and Neither are used for exactly TWO persons or things. For more than two, use Any or None.

12

Reciprocal Pronouns

Each other vs. One another
★ Topper's Rule: The Count Constraint
  • Each other: Use for two people (The two brothers hit each other).
  • One another: Use for more than two (The students congratulated one another).
13

Antecedent Agreement

The Agreement Hierarchy
⚠ Pronoun-Noun Mismatch

A pronoun must agree with its antecedent in number, gender, and person. In exams, watch out for the plural bias with "Everyone."

  • ✗ "Everyone should love their country."
  • ✓ "Everyone should love his (or his/her) country."
14

The Order of Pronouns

The 2nd-3rd-1st Etiquette Rule
★ Topper's Rule: Sequence of courtesy

When multiple personal pronouns are used together in a positive sense, the sequence should be 2nd Person → 3rd Person → 1st Person (2-3-1 Rule).

You (2) → He (3) → and I (1)
⚠ The Confession Exception

When admitting a mistake, guilt, or in negative contexts, the order reverses to 1-2-3 (I, you, and he are responsible for the loss).

15

Relative Pronoun Traps

The "But" and "That" nuances
★ "That" after Superlatives

Use That (not who/which) after superlative adjectives, and words like all, only, any, same, none, nothing.

  • "He is the wisest man that ever lived."
  • "All that glitters is not gold."
16

Deep Dive: Who vs. Whom

The ultimate diagnostic for advanced learners
✓ The He/Him Substitution Test

If you aren't sure whether to use Who or Whom, try substituting He or Him.

  • If He works → Use Who (Subject)
  • If Him works → Use Whom (Object)
  • Who/Whom shall I say is calling? He is calling → Who is calling.
  • To who/whom should I address the letter? Address it to him → To whom.
