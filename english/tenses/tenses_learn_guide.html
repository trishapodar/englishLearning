What Is a Tense?

The grammatical category that locates events in time — a foundation concept.

A tense is a grammatical category expressed through verb inflection (or auxiliary + main-verb construction) that signals the time at which an action, event, or state occurs relative to a reference point — typically the moment of utterance. Tense is a deictic feature: it points outward from the utterance to a position on the time-line.

English grammarians traditionally recognise three primary time-frames — past, present, and future — each subdivided into four aspects: simple, continuous (progressive), perfect, and perfect continuous. This yields the canonical twelve-tense matrix taught in schools and tested in examinations.

The Twelve-Tense Matrix

AspectPastPresentFuture
Simpleworkedwork / workswill work
Continuouswas workingam / is / are workingwill be working
Perfecthad workedhave / has workedwill have worked
Perf. Continuoushad been workinghave / has been workingwill have been working
✓ Prescriptive vs. Descriptive View of Tense
  • Prescriptive grammar (school/exam tradition): twelve tenses formed by time × aspect.
  • Descriptive/formal linguistics: English has only two morphological tenses (present and past); “future” is expressed through modals, not inflection. For examination purposes, always follow the prescriptive twelve-tense model unless the paper explicitly invokes linguistics.
  • Aspect encodes the internal temporal structure of the event; tense encodes location on the time-line. Conflating the two is the most common topper-level error.
✓ The Three Questions That Identify Any Tense

Ask three questions in order to name any verb form:

  • Time: Is the action past, present, or future?
  • Completion: Is the action complete (perfect) or in progress (continuous) or neither (simple)?
  • Duration into another time: Does it continue up to another time-point (perfect continuous)?
⚠ “Tense” Does Not Always Equal “Time”

The present simple can refer to future events (“The flight leaves at noon tomorrow.”) and habitual truths (“Water boils at 100°C.”). The grammatical tense label is determined by form, not always by the real-world time being described.

  • ✗ “Since ‘leaves’ is about the future, it is the future tense.” Wrong — the form is present simple; the temporal reading is future.
  • ✓ “‘Leaves’ is present simple used with a future time-adverb.”

The Simple Present Tense

Habits, universal truths, scheduled events, and performatives — the most versatile tense.

The simple present is formed with the base form of the verb (V1), adding -s / -es for third-person singular subjects. It expresses habitual actions, permanent states, general truths, and — in several modal uses — future-scheduled events and performative speech acts.

Subject → V1 (+ s/es for he/she/it) → Simple Present

The Six Core Uses

✓ When to Use the Simple Present
  • Habitual/Repeated actions: “She walks to school every day.” — signal words: always, usually, often, sometimes, never, every…
  • Universal truths & scientific facts: “The sun rises in the east.” — true at all times.
  • Permanent states: “He lives in Mumbai.” — state verbs (see Chapter 16).
  • Scheduled future events: “The train departs at 6 a.m. tomorrow.” — fixed timetable.
  • Performative acts: “I hereby declare the meeting open.” — the utterance IS the action.
  • Narrative / Historic present: “Caesar crosses the Rubicon and changes history.” — vivid retelling.

Ends in consonant + yDrop y, add -iescarry → carries
Ends in vowel + yAdd -splay → plays
All other verbsAdd -srun → runs
⚠ Missing the Third-Person “-s”

The most penalised error in competitive exams is omitting the -s / -es suffix on third-person singular verbs in the simple present.

  • ✗ “She go to the market daily.” Subject is third-person singular; verb must be goes.
  • ✓ “She goes to the market daily.”
  • ✗ “He do not understand.” Auxiliary must agree: does not.
  • ✓ “He does not understand.”

The Present Continuous Tense

Actions in progress at the moment of speaking, temporary situations, and planned futures.

The present continuous (also called the present progressive) is formed with the present tense of be (am / is / are) + the present participle (V1 + -ing). It encodes an action as ongoing at or around the time of utterance, or a temporary state, or a future arrangement.

am / is / are → V1 + -ing → Present Continuous
✓ Four Uses of the Present Continuous
  • Action happening now: “She is writing a letter at this moment.”
  • Temporary situations: “I am staying at a hotel this week.” — contrasts with a permanent state.
  • Future personal arrangement: “We are meeting the client tomorrow.” — planned and confirmed.
  • Changing / developing situation: “The global temperature is rising.”

Spelling Rules for the Present Participle (-ing)

ConditionRuleExample
Ends in silent -eDrop e, add -ingmake → making
One syllable, CVC patternDouble final consonant, add -ingrun → running
Stress on final syllable, CVCDouble final consonant, add -ingbegin → beginning
Ends in -ieChange to y, add -inglie → lying
All other casesSimply add -ingread → reading
⚠ Using Continuous with Stative Verbs

Verbs of state, perception, and cognition cannot normally be used in the continuous aspect because they describe conditions, not deliberate, ongoing activities. This is the single most-tested trap in competitive examinations.

  • ✗ “I am knowing the answer.” Know is a stative verb; continuous is ungrammatical.
  • ✓ “I know the answer.”
  • ✗ “She is having a car.” Have (= possess) is stative.
  • ✓ “She has a car.”
✓ Signal Words for Present Continuous

Look for these time expressions to identify the present continuous:

  • now, at the moment, right now, currently, at present, today, this week, look! listen!

The Present Perfect Tense

The bridge between past events and the present moment — the most misused tense in English.

The present perfect is formed with have / has + the past participle (V3). It expresses the current relevance of a past event: the event happened at an unspecified or psychologically “open” past time, and its result or significance persists into the present. The precise past moment is either unknown, unimportant, or deliberately left vague.

have / has → Past Participle (V3) → Present Perfect
✓ Five Core Uses of the Present Perfect
  • Unspecified past with present relevance: “I have lost my keys.” (I don’t have them now.)
  • Life experience up to now: “She has visited Paris three times.”
  • Change over time: “Prices have risen sharply.”
  • With “just” (very recent past): “He has just arrived.”
  • With “since / for” (duration to present): “We have lived here for ten years.”

Since vs. For — The Classic Trap

WordFollowed by…Correct Example
sinceA point in time (when the action started)“I have worked here since 2015.”
forA period of time (how long)“I have worked here for nine years.”
⚠ Present Perfect vs. Simple Past — The Most-Tested Distinction

Use the simple past when the time of the event is specified or implied. Use the present perfect when the time is unspecified and the present relevance matters.

  • ✗ “I have seen that film yesterday.” Yesterday specifies a closed past time — use simple past.
  • ✓ “I saw that film yesterday.”
  • ✗ “She went to Paris three times in her life.” Life experience up to now requires present perfect.
  • ✓ “She has been to Paris three times in her life.”
✓ Signal Words for Present Perfect
  • just, already, yet, ever, never, recently, lately, so far, up to now, since, for, this week/month/year, still (negative)
  • Key rule: if when + past time appears, switch to simple past.

The Present Perfect Continuous Tense

Ongoing actions that started in the past and continue — or have just stopped — with a present result.

The present perfect continuous is formed with have / has been + the present participle (V1 + -ing). It emphasises the duration and continuity of an activity that began in the past and either still continues or has very recently ended, leaving a visible result in the present.

have / has been → V1 + -ing → Present Perfect Continuous
✓ Distinguishing Present Perfect from Present Perfect Continuous
  • Present Perfect: emphasises the completion or result of an action — “I have written three chapters.” (three chapters exist).
  • Present Perfect Continuous: emphasises duration or activity in progress — “I have been writing all morning.” (the writing activity is foregrounded).
  • When a number/quantity appears, prefer the present perfect: “She has sent twenty emails today.”
  • When how long or a visible present result is stressed, prefer the continuous: “You look tired — have you been running?”
  • ✓ “It has been raining since morning.” Duration up to now — activity still ongoing.
  • ✓ “She has been waiting for two hours.” Emphasises the length of the waiting activity.
  • ✗ “He has been knowing French for five years.” Know is stative; use present perfect: has known.
  • ✓ “He has known French for five years.”
⚠ Stative Verbs Cannot Take the Continuous Aspect

This rule applies to the present perfect continuous as much as to the present continuous. Verbs like know, love, believe, belong, contain, seem, consist never take -ing in standard prescriptive usage, regardless of the time-frame.

The Simple Past Tense

Completed actions at a definite past time — the backbone of narration.

The simple past is formed with the past form of the verb (V2) — either a regular -ed inflection or an irregular form. It expresses a completed action, event, or state at a definite, specified, or implied past time that is now closed and separate from the present.

Subject → V2 (past form) → Simple Past
✓ When to Use the Simple Past
  • Single completed action in the past: “She left the office at five.”
  • Series of completed actions: “He entered, sat down, and opened his laptop.”
  • Past habit or state: “When I was young, I played cricket every evening.”
  • Past condition in second conditional: “If I had more time, I would travel.”

Spelling Rules for Regular Past Forms

ConditionRuleExample
Most verbsAdd -edwalk → walked
Ends in -eAdd -dlive → lived
Consonant + yChange y to i, add -edtry → tried
Short verb, CVC patternDouble consonant, add -edstop → stopped
⚠ Using “Did” + V1 in Questions & Negatives

A classic error is combining the auxiliary did with V2 instead of V1. Because did carries all the past tense marking, the main verb must revert to its base form.

  • ✗ “Did she went to school?” Did already marks past; main verb must be base form go.
  • ✓ “Did she go to school?”
  • ✗ “He didn’t finished his work.” Same principle: didn’t + base form.
  • ✓ “He didn’t finish his work.”
✓ Signal Words for Simple Past
  • yesterday, last week/month/year, ago, in 2010, then, at that time, once, when (+ specified time), in the past, formerly

The Past Continuous Tense

The ongoing background of a past moment — interrupted actions, parallel activities, and scene-setting.

The past continuous is formed with was / were + the present participle (V1 + -ing). It describes an action that was in progress at a specific moment in the past, or that formed the ongoing background against which another shorter action occurred.

was / were → V1 + -ing → Past Continuous
✓ The Four Uses of the Past Continuous
  • Action in progress at a past moment: “At midnight, she was still reading.”
  • Background action interrupted by a shorter event: “I was cooking when the phone rang.” — cooking = ongoing (past continuous); rang = short interruption (simple past).
  • Two parallel past actions: “While she was singing, he was playing the guitar.”
  • Annoying habit in the past (with “always”): “He was always losing his keys.”
⚠ Confusing “When” and “While”

when typically introduces the shorter, interrupting action (simple past); while typically introduces the longer, ongoing action (past continuous). Mixing them up reverses the narrative logic.

  • ✗ “While the phone rang, I was cooking.” Ringing is instantaneous — use when for the interruption.
  • ✓ “When the phone rang, I was cooking.”
  • ✓ “While I was cooking, the phone rang.”
  • ✓ “They were watching TV when the power went out.” Past continuous (background) + simple past (interruption).
  • ✓ “He was driving to work at 8 a.m. yesterday.” Action in progress at a specific past time.

The Past Perfect Tense

The “past of the past” — establishing which of two past events happened first.

The past perfect is formed with had + the past participle (V3). It describes a past action that was completed before another specified past action or past time-point. It is essential for establishing chronological priority within a narrative.

had → Past Participle (V3) → Past Perfect
✓ Rules for Using the Past Perfect
  • Two past events: the earlier uses past perfect, the later uses simple past. “By the time the ambulance arrived, the patient had died.”
  • Reported speech back-shift: “She said she had finished the report.”
  • Third conditional: “If I had studied harder, I would have passed.”
  • After conjunctions of time: after, as soon as, before, when, once, by the time — use past perfect for the prior event.
⚠ Unnecessary Past Perfect (Over-Use)

The past perfect is required only when two past events are mentioned and sequence must be clarified. If before or after already makes the order unambiguous, the simple past may be used in informal contexts — but in formal/exam writing, use the past perfect for the earlier event.

  • “I finished before he arrived.” — acceptable informally (order clear from before).
  • “I had finished before he arrived.” — preferred in formal / exam contexts.
  • ✗ “After he had arrived, we had eaten dinner.” The later event (eating) should be simple past, not past perfect.
  • ✓ “After he had arrived, we ate dinner.”
✓ Signal Words for Past Perfect
  • before, after, already, by the time, just, never…before, once, as soon as, when (with two past events), no sooner…than, hardly…when

The Past Perfect Continuous Tense

An ongoing action that had been in progress up to a past moment — the rarest and most nuanced past tense.

The past perfect continuous is formed with had been + the present participle (V1 + -ing). It describes an action that had been continuously in progress up to a specific past point, or whose duration explains a past situation. It combines the “priority” of the past perfect with the “duration” of the continuous aspect.

had been → V1 + -ing → Past Perfect Continuous
✓ When to Use the Past Perfect Continuous
  • Duration up to a past moment: “She had been working for twelve hours when she finally stopped.”
  • Cause of a past state: “His eyes were red because he had been crying.”
  • Activity interrupted in the past: “They had been playing for an hour when it started to rain.”
⚠ Past Perfect Continuous vs. Past Perfect

Choosing between them depends on whether duration or completion is the focus.

  • “She had written the letter.” — the letter was completed before the past reference point (past perfect).
  • “She had been writing the letter for an hour.” — the duration of writing is emphasised (past perfect continuous).
  • ✗ “He had been knowing her for years when they met again.” Know is stative; use past perfect: had known.
  • ✓ “The ground was wet because it had been raining all night.” Past result explained by prior continuous activity.
  • ✓ “By 2010, scientists had been studying the virus for a decade.” Duration of research up to a past date.

The Simple Future Tense

Predictions, spontaneous decisions, promises, and offers — and the many ways English expresses futurity.

The simple future is most commonly formed with the modal auxiliary will + base form of the verb (V1). It expresses a future event viewed as a whole, covering spontaneous decisions, predictions based on opinion, promises, offers, and requests. The construction be going to + V1 is a closely related future form with distinct uses.

will / shall → V1 (base form) → Simple Future
✓ Will vs. Be Going To — The Critical Distinction
  • Will — Spontaneous decision: “The phone is ringing — I’ll get it.” (decided at the moment of speaking).
  • Will — Prediction based on opinion: “I think it will rain tomorrow.”
  • Will — Promises / offers / requests / threats: “I will always love you.”
  • Be going to — Prior intention: “We are going to visit Paris next summer.” (planned before speaking).
  • Be going to — Prediction from present evidence: “Look at those clouds — it is going to rain.” (visible evidence).
⚠ Shall vs. Will — Formal Register Trap

In prescriptive British English, shall is used with first person (I / We) for simple future; will with second and third person. Reversing them adds emphasis (determination or command). In modern usage, will has largely displaced shall in all persons. Exam papers testing formal grammar may still penalise misuse.

  • “I will go” (modern neutral) vs. “I shall go” (formal British).
  • “You shall not pass!” — shall with second person = strong command/determination.
✓ Other Ways English Expresses the Future
  • Present continuous: “I am meeting her tomorrow.” (personal arrangement).
  • Simple present: “The train leaves at noon.” (scheduled timetable).
  • Be about to + V1: “The film is about to begin.” (imminent future).
  • Be due to + V1: “The Prime Minister is due to speak at six.” (formal/expected).

The Future Continuous Tense

Actions that will be in progress at a specific future moment or over a future period.

The future continuous is formed with will be + the present participle (V1 + -ing). It describes an action that will be ongoing or in progress at a particular moment in the future, or an action that will happen as part of a routine or natural course of events (without special arrangement).

will be → V1 + -ing → Future Continuous
✓ Three Uses of the Future Continuous
  • Action in progress at a future moment: “At this time tomorrow, I will be flying to London.”
  • Politely enquiring about plans (neutral future): “Will you be attending the meeting?” — softer than Will you attend?
  • Action expected as part of a normal routine: “The manager will be reviewing the files shortly.”
  • ✓ “When you arrive, I will be waiting at the gate.” Action ongoing at the future arrival moment.
  • ✗ “At noon, I will be knowing the results.” Know is stative; use simple future: will know.
  • ✓ “At noon, I will know the results.”
⚠ Future Continuous vs. Future Simple

The difference is one of perspective: the future simple views the event from outside as a complete whole; the future continuous views it from inside, as an activity in progress. This often creates a difference in politeness or tone in questions.

The Future Perfect Tense

Actions that will be completed before a specified future moment — the “future looking back.”

The future perfect is formed with will have + the past participle (V3). It describes an action that will be completed before a stated future time-point. It invites the speaker to mentally project forward to a future moment and look back at something that will be finished by then.

will have → Past Participle (V3) → Future Perfect
✓ Key Rule for Future Perfect
  • Always used with a future deadline: by, by the time, before, by then, by next year.
  • “By Friday, she will have submitted the report.” — submission precedes Friday.
  • “By the time you read this, I will have left.” — leaving precedes your reading.
⚠ Clause After “By the Time” Must Be Present Tense

In the subordinate clause introduced by by the time, use present simple (not future) to refer to the future action, because English does not normally use a future form inside a time clause.

  • ✗ “By the time you will arrive, I will have cooked.” Future form inside a time clause is non-standard.
  • ✓ “By the time you arrive, I will have cooked.”
✓ Signal Words for Future Perfect
  • by, by the time, before, by then, by [future date/time], in [duration] from now
  • Example: “In three years, he will have graduated.”

The Future Perfect Continuous Tense

The duration of an ongoing action up to a future moment — the most advanced tense in the matrix.

The future perfect continuous is formed with will have been + the present participle (V1 + -ing). It expresses the duration of an activity that will still be in progress at a specified future time — emphasising how long the activity will have continued by that point.

will have been → V1 + -ing → Future Perfect Continuous
✓ When to Use the Future Perfect Continuous
  • Emphasises the uninterrupted duration of an activity up to a future point: “By December, she will have been working here for twenty years.”
  • Stresses cause at a future moment: “When you meet him, he will have been travelling for thirty hours — he will be exhausted.”
⚠ Future Perfect Continuous vs. Future Perfect

The difference mirrors that between present perfect and present perfect continuous:

  • “By noon, I will have written the essay.” — completion is emphasised (future perfect).
  • “By noon, I will have been writing for six hours.” — duration is emphasised (future perfect continuous).
  • ✗ Stative verbs cannot take this form: “By then, I will have been knowing her for years.”
  • ✓ “By then, I will have known her for years.”

Tense Concord & Sequence of Tenses

The systematic back-shifting of tenses in reported speech and complex sentences — an examination favourite.

Sequence of tenses (also called tense harmony or concordance of tenses) is the grammatical principle that the tense of a subordinate clause is determined — or attracted — by the tense of the main (reporting) clause. When the main verb is in the past, subordinate verbs are generally shifted one step back in time.

The Back-Shifting Chart for Reported Speech

Direct Speech TenseReported Speech TenseExample
Simple presentSimple past“I work here.” → He said he worked there.
Present continuousPast continuous“I am working.” → He said he was working.
Present perfectPast perfect“I have finished.” → He said he had finished.
Simple pastPast perfect“I went.” → He said he had gone.
WillWould“I will come.” → He said he would come.
CanCould“I can swim.” → He said he could swim.
MayMight“I may leave.” → He said he might leave.
✓ Exceptions to Back-Shifting
  • Universal / scientific truths: “She said the earth revolves around the sun.” — never back-shift a permanent fact.
  • Historical facts: “He said that Shakespeare was born in 1564.” — fact unchanged.
  • Modal “would, could, might, should, ought to”: these do not back-shift further — they are already the back-shifted forms.
  • Present reporting verb: if the main verb is present (says, tells), no back-shift is required: “She says she is tired.”
⚠ Failing to Back-Shift When Required
  • ✗ “He said that he is tired.” Reporting verb said is past; back-shift to was.
  • ✓ “He said that he was tired.”
  • ✗ “She told me she will finish by noon.” Will must back-shift to would.
  • ✓ “She told me she would finish by noon.”

⚠ Using “Would” in the If-Clause
  • ✗ “If you would help me, I would finish sooner.” Standard conditionals never use would in the if-clause.
  • ✓ “If you helped me, I would finish sooner.”

Tense vs. Aspect — The Formal Distinction

A topper-level theoretical framework that underpins every tense question in advanced examinations.

Tense is the grammatical encoding of time location relative to a deictic centre (usually the utterance time). Aspect is a grammatical feature that encodes the internal temporal structure of the event or situation — how the event unfolds over time, regardless of when it occurs. In English, tense and aspect interact to produce the twelve-tense matrix.

The Two Aspects in English

AspectTechnical NameAuxiliary MarkerMeaning
PerfectivePerfect aspecthave/has/had + V3Completed action with current relevance; retrospective view
ProgressiveContinuous aspectbe + V-ingOngoing, in-progress activity; internal view of event
✓ Formal Principles of Aspect
  • Perfective aspect (have + V3) creates a retrospective viewpoint — the speaker looks back from one time-point to an earlier event. This is why the present perfect can never co-occur with a closed past-time adverb like yesterday.
  • Progressive aspect (be + V-ing) creates an internal viewpoint — the speaker is, metaphorically, inside the event watching it unfold. This is why it cannot describe states (which have no internal structure to observe).
  • Both aspects combined (have been + V-ing) gives the perfect continuous, encoding both retrospective view AND internal progressive structure.
✓ The Aspectual Viewpoint Test for Toppers

To verify the correct aspect, ask: “Am I looking at the event from inside (progressive) or from outside/after completion (perfect)?”

  • “I was reading” — inside the event; progressive (continuous) viewpoint.
  • “I had read” — outside, after completion; perfect viewpoint.
  • “I had been reading” — inside a duration that itself precedes another past point; perfect progressive viewpoint.
⚠ “English Has Only Two Tenses” — The Linguist’s Claim

Advanced linguistics papers sometimes state that English has only two morphological tenses: present (works) and past (worked). The “future” is not a tense by this definition because English verbs do not inflect for futurity — instead, future meaning is achieved by modals (will, shall) or periphrastic constructions (be going to). In examinations, unless the question is from a university-level linguistics paper, use the prescriptive twelve-tense model.

Stative vs. Dynamic Verbs

The semantic distinction that governs which verbs can take the continuous aspect — and when “rules” can be broken.

Stative verbs describe states — conditions, mental attitudes, perceptions, and relationships that simply exist rather than being actively performed. Because they describe unchanging states with no internal temporal structure, they resist the progressive aspect. Dynamic verbs describe deliberate, ongoing activities or events with an internal structure, and freely take the continuous aspect.

✓ Dual-Use Verbs — Stative and Dynamic

Several high-frequency verbs can be either stative OR dynamic, depending on meaning. This is a premium topper-level trap:

  • Think: “I think you are right.” (stative = opinion) vs. “I am thinking about the problem.” (dynamic = mental process in progress).
  • Have: “She has a car.” (stative = possession) vs. “We are having dinner.” (dynamic = experiencing an activity).
  • See: “I see what you mean.” (stative = understand) vs. “I am seeing the doctor today.” (dynamic = meeting/visiting).
  • Smell / Taste: “The soup tastes wonderful.” (stative = property) vs. “The chef is tasting the soup.” (dynamic = deliberate action).
  • Feel: “The fabric feels soft.” (stative) vs. “She is feeling better.” (dynamic = gradually improving).
  • Be: “He is rude.” (stative = permanent characteristic) vs. “He is being rude.” (dynamic = behaving rudely right now, possibly atypically).
⚠ The “I Am Loving It” Controversy

Certain advertising campaigns (“I’m lovin’ it”) use stative verbs in the progressive for stylistic effect — suggesting an intensified, in-the-moment emotional experience. This is a deliberate deviation from prescriptive rules for rhetorical effect. It is accepted in descriptive/colloquial English but should never be replicated in formal writing or examinations.

  • ✗ “I am belonging to this club.” Belong is stative — cannot take progressive.
  • ✓ “I belong to this club.”
  • ✗ “He is resembling his father.” Resemble is a state, not an activity.
  • ✓ “He resembles his father.”
  • ✓ “She is thinking about the proposal.” Think used dynamically (= actively considering) — progressive is correct.

Common Errors, Traps & Pitfalls

A consolidated catalogue of every major tense error found in competitive examinations — Foundation to Topper level.

This chapter consolidates the most frequently penalised tense errors in grammar examinations from SSC, IBPS, CAT, UPSC, IELTS, and school board papers. Each error is classified, explained, and corrected.

Group 1 — Present Tense Traps

⚠ Trap 1: Using Present Continuous for Habits
  • ✗ “He is going to school every day.” Habitual action requires simple present, not continuous.
  • ✓ “He goes to school every day.”
⚠ Trap 2: Present Perfect with Specific Past Time Adverbs
  • ✗ “I have met him last Monday.” Last Monday closes the past time-frame; present perfect is invalid.
  • ✓ “I met him last Monday.”
  • ✗ “She has joined the company in 2019.” Year specified — use simple past.
  • ✓ “She joined the company in 2019.”
⚠ Trap 3: “Since” with a Period vs. “For” with a Point
  • ✗ “I have been here since three hours.” Three hours is a duration — use for.
  • ✓ “I have been here for three hours.”
  • ✗ “We have known each other for 2010.” 2010 is a point in time — use since.
  • ✓ “We have known each other since 2010.”

Group 2 — Past Tense Traps

⚠ Trap 4: Simple Past Instead of Past Perfect for Prior Events
  • ✗ “When I arrived, she left already.” Leaving preceded arriving; the prior action needs past perfect.
  • ✓ “When I arrived, she had already left.”
⚠ Trap 5: “No Sooner…Than” and “Hardly…When”

These inverted structures always require the past perfect in the main clause and the simple past in the subordinate clause. The incorrect pairing is tested heavily.

  • ✗ “No sooner did he leave than she arrived.” Main clause should have past perfect with inversion.
  • ✓ “No sooner had he left than she arrived.”
  • ✗ “Hardly had she spoken when he interrupted her.” — this is correct. The wrong version is:
  • ✗ “Hardly she spoke when he interrupted her.” Inversion after hardly is mandatory: had + subject + V3.
⚠ Trap 6: Using Past Tense in Reported Speech Without Back-Shifting
  • ✗ “The teacher told us that the exam will be hard.” Will must back-shift to would.
  • ✓ “The teacher told us that the exam would be hard.”

Group 3 — Future Tense Traps

⚠ Trap 7: Future Tense in Time & Conditional Clauses

After conjunctions of time (when, as soon as, until, after, before, by the time) and condition (if, unless, provided that), the future is expressed with the present simple, not with will.

  • ✗ “Call me when you will arrive.” Future tense inside a time clause is non-standard.
  • ✓ “Call me when you arrive.”
  • ✗ “If it will rain, we will stay home.” Future in if-clause of first conditional is incorrect.
  • ✓ “If it rains, we will stay home.”
⚠ Trap 8: Misusing “Would” for All Past References

Students over-generalise would as “the past of will” and use it even in direct past narration. Would in past context is only valid in conditional clauses, reported speech, and habitual past actions.

  • ✗ “Yesterday, he would go to the market and would buy vegetables.” Simple narration of specific past events requires simple past, not would.
  • ✓ “Yesterday, he went to the market and bought vegetables.”
  • ✓ “As a child, he would go to the market every Saturday.” — this is correct because it expresses habitual past.

Group 4 — Advanced / Topper-Level Traps

⚠ Trap 9: Continuous Form with Stative Verbs (All Tenses)

This trap applies across all twelve tenses. No stative verb may take the -ing progressive form in standard formal English.

⚠ Trap 10: Conditional Tense Mismatch
  • ✗ “If I would have studied, I would have passed.” Third conditional: if-clause requires past perfect, not would have.
  • ✓ “If I had studied, I would have passed.”
  • ✗ “If she works harder, she would succeed.” Mixing first (present in if) and second (would) conditionals creates a type mismatch.
  • ✓ “If she works harder, she will succeed.” (First conditional)
⚠ Trap 11: “Used to” vs. “Would” for Past Habits

Used to can describe both past habitual actions AND past states. Would can describe past habitual actions only — never past states.

  • ✓ “She used to live near the river.” (past state — used to correct).
  • ✗ “She would live near the river.” Would cannot replace used to for states.
  • ✓ “She would go for a walk every morning.” (past habit — would correct).
⚠ Trap 12: “It Is Time” Construction

The expression it is (high) time is followed by a past tense clause (technically a past subjunctive) even though the meaning is present or future.

  • ✗ “It is time you go to bed.” After it is time + subject, use past tense (subjunctive).
  • ✓ “It is time you went to bed.”
  • ✗ “It is high time we will leave.” Future is never used here.
  • ✓ “It is high time we left.”
⚠ Trap 13: “Wish” + Tense Selection

The verb wish requires a specific tense pattern in each subordinate clause:

  • Wish for an unreal present: wish + past simple — “I wish I knew the answer.”
  • Wish for an unreal past: wish + past perfect — “I wish I had studied harder.”
  • Wish for a future change: wish + would + V1 — “I wish it would stop raining.”
  • ✗ “I wish I know the answer.” Present tense after wish for unreal present is incorrect; use past simple.
